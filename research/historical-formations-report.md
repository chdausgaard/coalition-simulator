# How Denmark forms governments: every formation from 1973 to 2026

**Denmark's post-1973 history reveals a political system where the weakest-looking governments sometimes prove most durable, where budget votes function as annual survival tests, and where the real power often belongs to parties that refuse to enter the room.** Since the 1973 earthquake election shattered Denmark's tidy four-party system, the country has produced roughly 20 distinct government formations — nearly all minorities, all navigating a 179-seat Folketing where 90 votes means survival and 89 means death. The unwritten rules that govern this process are nowhere codified but are remarkably consistent: negative parliamentarism rewards those who can avoid being toppled rather than those who command active support; budget passage is the annual existential test; and the parties positioned at the ideological center — particularly Radikale Venstre and now Moderaterne — wield leverage vastly disproportionate to their size.

This account covers every government formation from Poul Hartling's astonishing 22-seat minority in 1973 through the still-unresolved negotiations following the March 2026 election, with primary emphasis on how the sausage was made.

## I. The earthquake and its aftermath: Hartling and Anker Jørgensen (1973–1982)

### Poul Hartling's 22-seat government (December 1973 – February 1975)

**The jordskredsvalg of December 4, 1973** doubled the number of parties in the Folketing from five to ten. Socialdemokratiet hemorrhaged 24 seats (from 70 to 46), the Conservatives lost 15 (from 31 to 16), and three entirely new parties entered: Fremskridtspartiet (28 seats), Centrum-Demokraterne (14), and Kristeligt Folkeparti (7). Nearly 44% of voters switched parties — the largest voter migration in Danish electoral history until 2022.

**Formation (15 days).** In the chaos, Venstre's Poul Hartling made a calculation that would define Danish minority governance for decades: **you don't need a majority for you — you just need no majority against you.** With only 22 seats, Hartling formed history's smallest Danish government on December 19. His reasoning, documented in Dansk Biografisk Leksikon, was that "næppe noget parti ville fremkalde nyvalg foreløbig" (hardly any party would provoke new elections for the time being). He was right. Anker Jørgensen, humiliated by S's worst result since WWI, promised not to file a no-confidence motion. CD and KrF backed Hartling explicitly. As KrF leader Jens Møller later wrote: "Da Hartling samtidig blev bakket klart op af CD og af os i KRF, måtte De Konservative bide i det sure æble" — the Conservatives had to swallow the bitter pill of exclusion.

**Budget strategy.** With 22 seats, every piece of legislation required ad hoc coalition-building. Nearly one-third of all bills during Hartling's tenure were introduced by parties other than the government. The Store Bededagsforlig of May 1974 — a major economic package passed with K, CD, KrF, and Fremskridtspartiet — demonstrated the governing method: shifting coalitions, deal by deal. Hartling refused formal cooperation with Fremskridtspartiet (Mogens Glistrup was a pariah), yet FrP's 28 votes proved mathematically unavoidable on multiple occasions.

**The fall.** By December 1974, Hartling judged he could not assemble a sustainable majority for his economic plan. He called elections for January 9, 1975. Venstre surged from 22 to 42 seats — but entirely at the expense of other bourgeois parties. S rose to 53. The resulting formation battle was epic.

### The 1975 formation: four dronningerunder and 35 days

The 1975 government formation was the **longest in post-war Danish history at that point (35 days)** and introduced the term *dronningerunde* into Danish political vocabulary.

**Round 1: Karl Skytte (RV) as kongelig undersøger.** The Folketingets formand was tasked with forming a broad majority government. Both S and V refused to yield the PM post. V proposed an S-V-RV government led by the "neutral" Skytte himself — a non-starter that was likely designed to fail.

**Round 2: Anker Jørgensen as kongelig undersøger.** Hartling, as outgoing PM, conveyed to the Queen that RV demanded AJ attempt a *flertalsregering* (majority government). The left was furious: SF's Morten Lange accused Hartling of "groft misbrug af dronningen" (gross misuse of the Queen). AJ made a "proformalignende forsøg" (pro forma-like attempt) at a patchwork government, then gave up after two days.

**Round 3: Hartling's failed VCQM bid.** Hartling attempted to form a four-party bourgeois coalition (V+K+CD+KrF). He was reportedly "langt fremme" (far advanced) when he pulled out, fearing Fremskridtspartiet would topple such a government. The FrP threat was decisive — its 28 unpredictable votes could destroy any configuration.

**Round 4: Anker Jørgensen succeeds.** The failed VCQM attempt left the bourgeois parties bitterly divided. The smaller parties (CD, KrF) were furious at Venstre for calling the January election nobody wanted. This bitterness paradoxically made them cooperative with S, and on **February 13, 1975**, AJ formed a pure Social Democratic minority government with 53 seats.

### Anker Jørgensen II–V (1975–1982): minority government as permanent condition

AJ governed continuously from 1975 to 1982 through four distinct government configurations, navigating with shifting majorities and demonstrating that negative parliamentarism could sustain even weak governments indefinitely — as long as the opposition remained divided.

**The SV experiment (August 1978 – October 1979)** stands out as the most dramatic episode. After the 1977 election boosted S to 65 seats, AJ pulled Venstre (21 seats) into government, creating an 86-seat "worker-farmer" coalition — still short of the 90-seat majority. The motives were strategic: breaking the emerging bourgeois *firkløver* (four-leaf clover) of V, K, CD, and KrF, while securing a stable base for economic tightening.

**The dysfunction was immediate.** LO chairman Thomas Nielsen called it a "kup." S's own gruppeformand Jens Risgaard Knudsen resigned in protest. AJ's diary: "Det må være min indtil dato værste dag i mit liv — kan det blive værre?" (This must be the worst day of my life to date — can it get worse?) Each minister was assigned a "contact minister" from the partner party, quickly renamed *kontrolministre* (control ministers). Ministers sat facing each other by party in meetings, "næsten som var de parter i en forhandling på arbejdsmarkedet" (almost like parties in a labor market negotiation). The Marienborgaftale (government program) was criticized as "uklar og visionsløs" (unclear and visionless). The second oil crisis destroyed any remaining basis for cooperation, and AJ dissolved the SV-government in September 1979.

**Anker Jørgensen's final government (AJ V, December 1981 – September 1982)** ended when AJ simply gave up. After S lost 9 seats in the 1981 election, the government was caught between SF (which had reached its pain threshold for austerity) and Radikale Venstre (whose leader Niels Helveg Petersen refused to govern with AJ as PM). On September 2, 1982, AJ went to Queen Margrethe with his resignation request **without calling elections** — he "smed håndklædet i ringen" (threw in the towel). This voluntary resignation without elections opened the door for the most consequential government change of the post-1973 era.

## II. The Schlüter decade: how a minority governed for ten years (1982–1993)

### Schlüter I — the firkløver forms without an election

**The mechanism was pure negative parliamentarism.** When AJ resigned on September 2, 1982, a dronningerunde produced Poul Schlüter (KF) as the consensus choice. Det Radikale Venstre — traditionally closer to Social Democrats — indicated it would support a bourgeois government on economic policy. On **September 10, 1982**, just 8 days after AJ's resignation, Denmark got its first Conservative PM since 1901.

**The firkløverregering** (four-leaf clover government) consisted of KF (26) + V (20) + CD (15) + KrF (4) = **65 seats**. With RV (9) and FrP (16), the support base reached exactly 90. Schlüter told his 21 ministers at their first meeting: "You are the Firkløver government's ministers. Your loyalty is to the government. Strife between parties is forbidden. We are a minority government. Perhaps we'll only last half a year, but let us do things right." He gave each minister a copy of *Yes, Minister* and the Ministerial Accountability Act.

### The alternative majority: Denmark's constitutional innovation

The most constitutionally unusual feature of the Schlüter era was the *det alternative flertal* (alternative majority) on security policy. From December 1982 onward, a majority consisting of S + RV + SF + VS repeatedly forced the government to adopt NATO positions contrary to its wishes — the so-called *fodnotepolitikken* (footnote policy), where Denmark had to add reservations to NATO communiqués. The US coined the term **"Denmarkization"** to describe this.

**The constitutional trick was compartmentalization.** RV supported the government's economic policy while voting against its security policy. The tacit understanding — never formally codified — was that foreign policy defeats were **not** confidence issues. Schlüter prioritized economic recovery over foreign policy control. Between 1982 and 1988, the alternative majority passed approximately **22-28 resolutions** against the government on security questions.

### Budget politics under Schlüter: the annual survival test

The firkløver's budget arithmetic evolved across the decade:

- **1982–1984:** 65 government seats + RV (9) + FrP (16) = 90. FrP was the uncomfortable partner; the "Rio Bravo-forliget" in 1983 was reportedly named after the film negotiators watched during a break.
- **1984–1987:** After the 1984 election (KF's best ever at 42 seats), the coalition reached 77 seats. With RV (10) and 3 North Atlantic mandates = 90 — crucially, **no longer dependent on FrP.**
- **1987–1988:** After losing seats, the coalition fell to 70. With RV (11) = 81 — insufficient. The government needed either FrP (9) or S cooperation.
- **1988–1990 (KVR):** After the "Atomvalget" (atom election), Schlüter dropped CD and KrF and brought RV into government. This was the strategic masterstroke: **by including RV, the alternative majority on security policy ceased to exist.** The formation took a record **24 days** and four dronningerunder. CD and KrF were "brutally fired" and never forgave this.
- **The 1989 finanslov crisis:** The KVR government's budget passed with only **63 votes for** — because **Socialdemokratiet abstained** rather than voting against. This was the first time in Danish history a finanslov passed without a proper majority. It established an important norm: the main opposition party would rather abstain than actively topple a government via budget rejection.

### The Tamil case and the fall

Justice Minister Erik Ninn-Hansen had illegally blocked Tamil refugee family reunification applications in 1987–88. Schlüter's infamous "gulvtæppetale" of April 25, 1989 — "Der er ikke fejet noget ind under gulvtæppet" (Nothing has been swept under the carpet) — became one of the most notorious phrases in Danish political history. On **January 14, 1993**, a 6,000-page judicial investigation report concluded that Schlüter's statements were "particularly misleading." He resigned the same day without calling elections.

**One key detail about the 1990 formation** deserves emphasis: after the 1990 election, S had 69 seats — a fantastic result — but its leader Svend Auken could not get the midterpartier (CD, KrF, RV) to support him as PM. Only S (69) + SF (15) = 84 pointed to Auken. The center parties' refusal to endorse him enabled Schlüter to form a slim KV government with only ~59 seats. **The center parties' personal veto on the PM candidate** was a decisive factor — a norm that would recur in 1993 when these same parties chose Nyrup Rasmussen over Auken.

## III. The Nyrup Rasmussen years: the SR formula (1993–2001)

### Nyrup I: majority government from the Tamil case wreckage

When Schlüter resigned on January 14, 1993, the transfer of power happened without elections — the second such non-election government change in a decade. Schlüter attempted to install Finance Minister Henning Dyremose (K) as successor, but the three center parties refused. They had already oriented toward Poul Nyrup Rasmussen, whom S had elected leader in 1992 specifically because the center parties (especially RV) found him acceptable — unlike Svend Auken. This was a rare instance where **an opposition party effectively selected the governing party's leader**.

Nyrup formed a four-party government: **S (69) + RV (7) + CD (9) + KrF (4) = 89 seats**. With one sympathetic North Atlantic mandate, this reached **90 — Denmark's first majority government since 1971**, and almost the last (until SVM in 2022). Formation took approximately 10 days.

### The evolving coalition: parties drop away

**KrF fell out automatically** in 1994 when it lost all its Folketing seats. **CD departed in December 1996**, explicitly protesting the government's finanslovsaftale with SF and Enhedslisten — it considered the budget deal too left-wing. This left the classic **SR-government** (S+RV) formula with approximately 70 seats, dependent on external support from SF and EL.

The S-RV relationship was structurally tense. RV was more fiscally conservative, favored abolishing efterløn (early retirement), and was more enthusiastically pro-EU. S was closer to trade unions and protective of welfare spending. Yet the partnership endured because neither party had a viable alternative: S needed RV for centrist credibility, and RV needed S for government participation.

### EL's budget behavior under Nyrup: the template

Enhedslisten's parliamentary behavior during 1994–2001 established patterns that persisted for decades. As EL's own history describes it: "Som regel forhandlede Enhedslisten så langt som muligt, for så at ende med at stemme imod den samlede finanslov" (As a rule, EL negotiated as far as possible, then ended up voting against the overall finanslov). The sole exception was **1996**, when EL struck a deal and **abstained** rather than voting against — achieving specific improvements in exchange.

**There was no formal forståelsespapir.** The arrangement was entirely informal, based on case-by-case negotiations. SF was the reliable støtteparti that generally voted for budgets; EL was the critical partner that negotiated hard but usually voted against the overall package while participating in specific sub-agreements.

### The 1998 election: decided by 176 Faroese votes

The 1998 election was a genuine thriller. With S (63) + RV (7) + SF (13) + EL (5) = 88 from Danish seats, the government's survival depended on North Atlantic mandates. The decisive Faroese mandate was won by social democrat Jóannes Eidesgaard, who beat the incumbent by just **176 votes**. This gave the government exactly 90 seats — the narrowest possible majority.

**The efterløn betrayal** defined Nyrup's final term. During the campaign, Nyrup issued a famous guarantee on March 6, 1998: "So long as Social Democrats are in government, the efterløn will not be diminished." After winning, the government negotiated an efterløn reform **with the blue bloc** (V, KF, CD, KrF), completely bypassing SF and EL. This cross-bloc budget deal demonstrated a critical feature of Danish politics: **minority governments can build shifting majorities, going left on some issues and right on others.** Nyrup later apologized: "Skruen fik et ekstra drej, da valgkampen var på sit højeste. Folk fik det indtryk, at vi ikke ville ændre ved efterlønnen. Jeg beklager." The betrayal haunted S through the 2001 election loss.

## IV. The VK+DF model: Denmark's most stable minority arrangement (2001–2011)

### Fogh I: blokpolitik arrives

The **November 20, 2001 election** produced a revolution. For the first time since 1926, a pure right-wing majority existed without needing centrist parties: V (56) + KF (16) + DF (22) = **94 seats**. Anders Fogh Rasmussen formed a VK government in just **7 days** — the fastest formation of the period.

**Why VK and not VKO?** DF was never offered cabinet seats, and DF did not seek them in 2001. The arrangement was mutually beneficial: DF could extract policy concessions without being bound by collective cabinet responsibility (especially problematic given DF's EU-skepticism), while VK could present a more moderate international image. DF's Kristian Thulesen Dahl later explained the strategic logic: "What we could get through in a government agreement was simply too small, and it would have made it impossible for us to fight for things like genuine border control."

### What DF extracted: the price of permanent support

**DF voted for every single finanslov from 2002 through 2011** — ten consecutive budgets without exception. In exchange, DF achieved:

- **Immigration policy as the central price.** The Udlændingeloven of 2002 was "Europe's strictest" immigration law: 24-year rule for family reunification, tilknytningskrav (aggregate attachment requirement), permanent residence only after 7 years, 30–40% lower benefits for recent arrivals (starthjælp). Family reunification permits dropped from ~8,151 in 2002 to 3,525 by 2005.
- **Ældrecheck** (elderly check), introduced 2003 as a supplementary payment to the poorest pensioners — initially temporary, DF extracted its permanent status in subsequent budget negotiations. This became DF's signature welfare achievement.
- **Criminal justice:** 720 additional police officers, 298 new prison places, stricter sentencing across the board.
- **Annual stramninger** (tightenings): Each year brought new immigration restrictions, often linked to budget negotiations.

**The VKO arithmetic was remarkably stable.** In both 2001 and 2005, VKO held exactly 94 seats — a 4-seat surplus over the 90-seat threshold. After the 2007 election, the margin narrowed to exactly 90 (including one Faroese mandate), when the new party Ny Alliance briefly threatened to break the majority but won only 5 seats.

### Cross-bloc exceptions within blokpolitik

Despite the VKO model's rigid bloc character, several major agreements crossed the aisle. The **Velfærdsforlig of June 2006** — linking retirement and pension ages to life expectancy — was agreed between VK, DF, S, and RV. The **Forårspakke 2.0 (2009)** tax reform included RV and Liberal Alliance. These cross-bloc agreements on structural reforms coexisted with strict bloc discipline on annual budgets.

### Løkke I: continuation and collapse

When Fogh departed for NATO in April 2009, Lars Løkke Rasmussen took over without a dronningerunde — the parliamentary situation was unchanged. The Løkke I government fell at the **September 2011 election**, primarily because the Conservative collapse (from 18 to 8 seats) destroyed the VKO majority. The financial crisis aftermath, unpopular dagpenge reforms, expense scandals around Løkke, and voter fatigue after 10 years all contributed.

## V. Fragmentation returns: Thorning-Schmidt and Løkke II–III (2011–2019)

### Thorning-Schmidt I (October 2011 – January 2014): the S-SF-RV triangle

The **2011 election** gave rød blok 89 Danish seats + 3 North Atlantic = **92 seats**. Helle Thorning-Schmidt was named kongelig undersøger on September 16 and presented her government on October 3 — **18 days** of negotiations in "det sorte tårn" (the black tower).

**The fundamental tension was the S↔SF↔RV triangle.** SF (16 seats) wanted redistributive economics; RV (17 seats) wanted fiscal discipline. S was caught between them. Despite having fewer seats, **RV's leverage was decisive** because its centrist position gave it a credible exit option (cooperating with blue bloc parties), while SF had no alternative but to stay.

The result was a government program that took the VK government's economic framework as its starting point — a bitter pill for S and SF. The S-SF joint platform "Fair Løsning" was largely abandoned: no millionærskat, no betalingsring (congestion charge), no caps on interest deductions. The opposition immediately accused S and SF of *løftebrud* (broken promises).

**EL's historic first finanslov vote.** Enhedslisten voted **for** a finanslov for the first time in its history in January 2012, passing 109-5 in the Folketing. But the approval in EL's hovedbestyrelse was razor-thin: **15-9**, with critics just one vote short of blocking. EL voted for FL 2013 "nødtvungent" (reluctantly), then refused FL 2014 — forcing the government to negotiate that budget with **Venstre and Konservative** instead. This demonstrated a key dynamic: **when the left støtteparti defects, the government goes right.** For FL 2015, EL returned.

**No forståelsespapir existed** for Thorning I. The government program was presented as a fait accompli after three-party negotiations from which EL was excluded. As Pelle Dragsted later described it: "Groft skåret kom Thorning, Corydon og Vestager ned fra det sorte tårn med et færdigt regeringsgrundlag" (Roughly put, Thorning, Corydon, and Vestager came down from the black tower with a finished government program).

### SF's departure: the DONG/Goldman Sachs crisis

In January 2014, the government's sale of an 18% stake in the state-owned energy company DONG Energy to Goldman Sachs for 8 billion DKK became incendiary. The "Luxembourg-finte" (Goldman structuring ownership through Luxembourg/Cayman entities to avoid Danish tax) and 200,000-signature protest petition made the sale a political crisis. On **January 30, 2014**, SF leader Annette Vilhelmsen resigned, announcing SF was leaving government. SF's Ole Sohn called it "noget af det mest pinlige og latterlige, jeg har oplevet" (one of the most embarrassing and ridiculous things I've experienced). Thorning II (S+RV) formed on February 3 with just **61 government seats** — 34% of the Folketing.

### Løkke II (June 2015 – November 2016): governing with 34 seats

The **2015 election** produced an extraordinary result: DF became the largest blue bloc party with **37 seats** — three more than Venstre's 34. Blue bloc total: exactly 90.

**Why didn't DF form the government?** Kristian Thulesen Dahl explicitly chose not to seek the PM post, referencing SF's catastrophic experience in government: "SF solgte ud af politikken for at komme i regering" (SF sold out their politics to get in government). He later acknowledged this was a mistake: "Et parti med et så stærkt folkeligt mandat skal i vælgernes øjne ende som regeringsparti... Måske skulle vi have forhandlet i månedsvis." DF's 2019 collapse (from 37 to 16 seats) partly reflected voters punishing the party for declining responsibility.

**Formation took 10 days** with two dronningerunder. On Day 1, Løkke was named kongelig undersøger with a mandate for a majority government. By Day 3, he announced "det ikke lader sig gøre at etablere en flertalsregering" — DF, LA, and KF all declined to join. After a second dronningerunde, he formed a **pure Venstre government with 34 seats** — the smallest since Hartling. Løkke acknowledged: "Jeg er meget bevidst om, at en ren Venstre-regering ikke bare er en mindretalsregering, men en lille mindretalsregering, som er nødt til at være ekstrem lydhør."

**Budget arithmetic:** V (34) + DF (37) + LA (13) + KF (6) = 90 — the bare minimum. The government was also forced into cross-bloc deals when individual støttepartier defected on specific issues.

### VLAK (November 2016 – June 2019): neutralizing threats by inclusion

Liberal Alliance leader Anders Samuelsen's persistent threats to table a mistillidsvotum unless topskatten was cut by 5 percentage points created a crisis. DF blocked any topskat cuts: "Vi er aldrig kommet til forhandlingsbordet med ultimative krav, men denne her situation er så ekstraordinær." The result was deadlock that Løkke resolved by **bringing LA and KF into government**, neutralizing LA's external threats by making them share cabinet responsibility.

VLAK negotiations took just one week (November 21–28, 2016). **LA got 6 ministerial posts** but dropped its absolutist topskat demand. The government program contained only vague language about "markedly reducing the number of topskat payers." LA's Samuelsen eventually admitted: "Lavere topskat ikke bliver en del af det skatteudspil." CEPOS director Martin Ågerup's verdict: "Liberal Alliance har på det ene punkt efter det andet givet efter for Dansk Folkeparti."

**VLAK + DF held exactly 90 seats.** DF voted for all three VLAK budgets but consistently blocked the tax reform LA wanted. The FL 2018 negotiations were especially dramatic, with LA threatening to vote against its own government's finanslov. Despite predictions of instability, **VLAK lasted the full term** — ambitions were simply adjusted downward.

## VI. The Frederiksen era: from forståelsespapir to cross-bloc experiment (2019–2026)

### Frederiksen I (June 2019 – December 2022): S alone with a written contract

The **June 5, 2019 election** gave rød blok exactly 91 mandates. Formation took **20 days**. Mette Frederiksen had announced a year earlier that she would seek a pure S minority government — a deliberate strategic choice to maximize freedom of maneuver, especially on immigration where she had moved S rightward.

**The forståelsespapir — "Retfærdig retning for Danmark"** — was signed on June 25, 2019 by Frederiksen (S), Morten Østergaard (RV), Pia Olsen Dyhr (SF), and Pernille Skipper (EL). At **18 pages**, it was a major innovation: not a regeringsgrundlag (Frederiksen explicitly stated "Det her er ikke et regeringsgrundlag"), but a formalized political understanding committing the government to specific initiatives. Previous støtteparti arrangements had been entirely informal. This was new.

Key provisions included a **70% greenhouse gas reduction by 2030**, minimumsnormeringer (minimum staffing ratios) in daycare, Arnepension (flexible early retirement for worn-out workers), and maintaining firm immigration policy. Each støtteparti got recognizable wins: SF got daycare norms, RV got climate ambitions, EL got welfare spending and a ydelseskommission. The approval in EL's hovedbestyrelse passed, but **9 of 24 members abstained** — a sign of the fragile internal balance between pragmatists and hardliners.

**EL's behavior transformed.** Under Frederiksen I, Enhedslisten voted **for all three finanslove** (FL 2020, 2021, 2022), adopting an incremental approach. This was a historic shift from EL's traditional pattern of voting against the overall budget. The forståelsespapir's structure gave EL enough ownership of government direction to justify support.

**The mink scandal and RV's ultimatum.** In November 2020, the government ordered all Danish mink culled without legal authority. The subsequent Minkkommission report (June 2022) was devastatingly critical. On July 2, 2022, RV leader Sofie Carsten Nielsen demanded Frederiksen call an election before the Folketingets åbning in October, or face a mistillidsvotum. Frederiksen called the election on October 5, 2022.

### Frederiksen II — SVM (December 2022 – March 2026): the unprecedented cross-bloc government

The **November 1, 2022 election** produced Denmark's most fragmented parliament in decades: 12 parties entering, no bloc with a clear majority. S won 50 seats; the new party Moderaterne (Lars Løkke Rasmussen) won **16 seats** and immediately became kingmaker. Rød blok reached 90 mandates (barely a majority with North Atlantic seats), but both S and RV had campaigned for a broad government across the center.

**The kongerunde (November 2).** Frederiksen received 103 mandates pointing to her as kongelig undersøger; V's Jakob Ellemann-Jensen received 58; Danmarksdemokraterne's Inger Støjberg received 14. Frederiksen was appointed.

**Formation took a record 41–42 days** (November 4 – December 15, 2022) — the longest in modern Danish history. Negotiations narrowed to SVM relatively early, though other configurations were explored. The most dramatic moment came at the end: **Radikale Venstre participated in negotiations through December 13**, with all four parties (S, V, M, RV) agreeing to form a government together. But RV's new leader Martin Lidegaard faced a **massive revolt in his folketingsgruppe**, and RV reversed course — announcing it would join neither government nor opposition but take an unprecedented "neutral" position.

**SVM's arithmetic: S (50) + V (23) + M (16) = 89 seats.** With sympathetic North Atlantic mandates, this was a de facto **majority government** — the first since Nyrup I in 1993. The 58-page regeringsgrundlag "Ansvar for Danmark" included abolishing Store Bededag (a public holiday) to fund defense spending, lowering topskat by 7.5 percentage points, shortening master's degrees, and reaching 2% GDP defense spending by 2030.

**The Store Bededag controversy** demonstrated both the power and the weakness of a majority government: the abolition passed despite 47,000 signatures against it (the largest petition in Danish history), precisely because the government didn't need external support. But the backlash was sustained.

**Was this considered norm-breaking?** Definitively yes. The last S-V government was Anker Jørgensen's failed SV experiment (1978–79), which lasted only 14 months. Comparisons were immediate and unflattering. SVM was described as a "fornuftsægteskab" (marriage of convenience), and all three parties suffered sustained polling declines. Scholars debated whether the formation had a genuine voter mandate.

**Important correction to a common misconception: Venstre did NOT leave the SVM government before the 2026 election.** Despite growing internal tensions — including Jakob Ellemann-Jensen's departure from politics (October 2023), multiple Moderaterne defections hemorrhaging the government's mandate from 89 to approximately 85, and Venstre's Troels Lund Poulsen increasingly positioning toward the blue bloc — the SVM government sat its **entire term** through to the March 2026 election. There was no "Frederiksen III."

### The 2026 election and ongoing formation

The **March 24, 2026 election** produced historic results. S fell to **38 seats** (worst since 1903). V fell to **18 seats** (worst in party history). All 12 parties on the ballot entered the Folketing — the first time since the electoral threshold was introduced in 1953. The arithmetic:

- **Rød blok** (S+SF+EL+RV+Å): **84 seats**
- **Blå blok** (V+LA+K+DD+DF+Borgernes Parti): **77 seats**  
- **Moderaterne:** **14 seats** — again kingmaker

On **March 25, 2026**, the first kongerunde under King Frederik X took place. Frederiksen received 84 mandates pointing to her; V's Troels Lund Poulsen received 73; Løkke pointed to himself. **Frederiksen was appointed kongelig undersøger** with a mandate to attempt forming a government with SF and Radikale Venstre.

The mathematical challenge is severe. S (38) + SF (20) + RV (9) = 67 — far from 90. Adding Moderaterne (14) reaches 81; adding EL (11) reaches 92. But Løkke has declared he will not accept a government resting on Enhedslisten, while EL demands no blue parties in government. Troels Lund Poulsen has explicitly ruled out another SVM. Experts predict formation could take months — professor Frederik Waage says he "wouldn't be shocked if a government first lands in June."

## VII. Synthesis: the unwritten rules of Danish government formation

### Government size and composition

**Empirical distribution of government size (1973–2026):**

| Parties in government | Count | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| 1 party | ~9 | Hartling, AJ II–V, Løkke II, Frederiksen I |
| 2 parties | ~5 | SR governments (Nyrup III–IV), VK (Fogh, Løkke I), SM (concept) |
| 3 parties | ~4 | S+SF+RV (Thorning I), VLAK, SVM |
| 4 parties | ~3 | Firkløver (Schlüter I–III), Nyrup I (S+CD+RV+KrF) |

**Denmark has had 4-party governments** — the firkløver ran from 1982 to 1988 as three successive governments. Nyrup I (1993–94) also had four parties. The firkløver was humorously nicknamed "WC-kumme-regeringen" (toilet bowl government) using the parties' ballot letters. Four parties was seen as somewhat unusual but functional when all were small and ideologically compatible. The key lesson: 4-party governments worked when parties were small and needed each other, but proved fragile when one partner felt expendable (CD and KrF's "brutal firing" in 1988).

**Typical seat share of minority governments ranges from 34 to 77 seats** (19%–43% of the Folketing), with most falling between 59 and 77 seats. The extremes — Hartling's 22 and Løkke II's 34 — are remembered precisely because they were exceptional.

**Excluding the largest party.** It has happened that the largest Folketing party is not in government — most notably when DF was blue bloc's largest party (37 seats) in 2015 but chose not to govern. In 1990, S was the largest party (69 seats) but Schlüter continued because midterpartier wouldn't support Svend Auken as PM. The norm is that the largest party within the winning bloc usually leads government, but there is no rule requiring the Folketing's single largest party to govern.

### Formation process patterns

**Formation duration has ranged from 0 to 42 days:**

| Formation | Days | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Schlüter II (1984) | 0 | Immediate continuation |
| AJ IV (1979) | 3 | No alternatives existed |
| Schlüter I (1982) | 8 | Non-election formation |
| Fogh I (2001) | 7 | Clear VKO majority |
| Thorning I (2011) | 18 | Three-party negotiation |
| AJ II (1975) | 35 | Four dronningerunder |
| SVM (2022) | ~42 | Record: cross-bloc complexity |

**Typical formations take 7–18 days** when bloc arithmetic is clear; formations become extended (30+ days) when no bloc has a majority and unusual configurations must be explored.

**Multiple kongerunder occur when the first attempt fails.** The 1975 formation required four rounds; the 1988 KVR formation required four rounds; the 2015 formation needed two. When the first formateur fails, the process can restart with a new investigator (as in 1975 when Skytte gave way to AJ who gave way to Hartling who gave way to AJ again) or the same person can be reappointed with a modified mandate (as in 2015 when Løkke failed at a majority government, then succeeded with a single-party government).

**Unexpected outcomes are not uncommon.** Hartling's 22-seat government was unthinkable before 1973; Schlüter's formation without an election in 1982 surprised many; Løkke's 34-seat solo government in 2015 was not the expected outcome; SVM in 2022 was genuinely unprecedented.

### Budget politics and support arrangements

**Forståelsespapirer are a recent innovation.** Before 2019, all støtteparti arrangements were informal, based on case-by-case negotiations. Frederiksen I's 18-page forståelsespapir was the first formalized written agreement between a government and its external supporters. The 2011 Thorning government had no such document. The VK+DF model (2001–2011) relied on mutual understanding and annual budget negotiations rather than any written compact.

**Budget vote arithmetic varies substantially.** Governments have passed budgets with as few as 63 votes for (KVR 1989, when S abstained) or as many as 170+ (broad-agreement budgets under SVM). The typical pattern for minority governments is **90–100 votes for the finanslov**, meaning the government parties plus their støttepartier and occasionally a few extra votes.

**Opposition abstention on budgets is a meaningful norm.** The most consequential example was S abstaining on the 1989 finanslov, allowing a budget to pass with only 63 votes. Historically, S always voted for the finanslov even from opposition — AJ's decision to vote against in 1983 was considered a break with tradition. The bourgeois parties have also generally accepted that "the sitting government first must try to find a majority with its parliamentary basis" before they offer to help.

**EL's empirical budget-vote record:**

| Period | Behavior |
|---|---|
| 1994–2001 (Nyrup) | Generally voted **against** the overall finanslov; **abstained** in 1996 |
| 2001–2011 (Fogh/Løkke) | In opposition; irrelevant to budget passage |
| 2012 (Thorning, FL 2012) | Voted **for** — first time ever; HB approved 15-9 |
| 2013 (FL 2013) | Voted **for** reluctantly ("nødtvungent") |
| 2014 (FL 2014) | Voted **against** — government went right instead |
| 2015 (FL 2015) | Voted **for** |
| 2019–2022 (Frederiksen I) | Voted **for** all three finanslove |

**EL's pattern shows a clear evolution** from principled opposition (1990s) through reluctant engagement (2012–2015) to consistent support under a forståelsespapir (2019–2022). The key variable is whether EL's hovedbestyrelse perceives sufficient concrete improvements to justify a "yes" vote. Internal EL votes on budget participation are frequently tight (15-9 type margins).

**Støtteparti stability varies enormously.** The VK+DF arrangement (2001–2011) was the most stable in Danish history — DF supported every budget for a decade. By contrast, EL's support for the Thorning government flickered on and off, with EL defecting on FL 2014. The general pattern is that støttepartier extract escalating concessions over time, but the government's ability to "go the other way" (seek support from the opposing bloc on specific issues) constrains this dynamic.

### Risk tolerance and budget passage

**Danish governments have been willing to accept significant risk.** Hartling governed with 22 seats; Løkke II governed with 34; the Nyrup IV government survived on 90 seats with the decisive mandate won by 176 Faroese votes. Formateurs do not generally "play it safe" — the modal Danish government has had a margin of 0–4 seats over the 90-seat threshold for its budget majority.

**No sitting government has simply failed to pass a budget** in the sense of being unable to find any majority. When EL refused FL 2014, the Thorning government pivoted to V and KF. When Schlüter couldn't get FL 1984 through (because S broke tradition and voted against while FrP voted against on principle), he called elections rather than accept defeat. The norm is that **a government will always find someone to pass a budget with**, even if the "someone" is unexpected.

**The genuinely uncertain moments** include Schlüter's 1983–84 finanslov crisis, Nyrup's razor-thin 1998 arithmetic, EL's 15-9 hovedbestyrelse vote on FL 2012, and Thorning's FL 2014 where the government had to suddenly reverse course and negotiate with the opposition. In none of these cases did a budget actually fail to pass — but the probability of passage was genuinely uncertain beforehand.

### Cross-bloc dynamics before and after SVM

**Before SVM (2022), cross-bloc government formation had one precedent: the AJ SV-government of 1978–79**, which lasted only 14 months and is universally remembered as a failure. However, **cross-bloc legislative agreements** were common and important: the Velfærdsforlig (2006), the efterløn reform (1998), and various Schlüter-era economic packages all involved parties from both blocs.

**SVM's reception was widely negative.** All three parties suffered sustained polling declines. The formation was considered norm-breaking — the first genuine cross-bloc government since 1978 and only the second since 1945. The immediate backlash focused on the abolition of Store Bededag (which many saw as imposed by a majority government that didn't need to compromise with anyone) and the perception that all three parties had abandoned their voters' mandates. SVM held 201 political agreements during its term, of which 182 crossed the political center — demonstrating that a broad government produces broad agreements but alienates partisan voters.

**SVM stayed together for the full term** but at enormous political cost. The combined SVM vote share fell from 50.1% (2022) to 39.7% (2026), losing approximately 19 seats between the three parties.

### What the 2026 formation tells us about Danish norms

The current formation puzzle — where rød blok has 84 seats, blå blok has 77, and Moderaterne holds 14 in between — is historically closest to the **1975 and 1988 formations**: situations where no obvious majority existed, multiple kongerunder were needed, and the eventual government was a configuration nobody predicted before the election.

**What the historical record suggests:**

- **Formation will be slow** — probably 3–6 weeks minimum, possibly longer. The record is 42 days (SVM 2022); the 2026 situation is at least as complex.
- **Multiple kongerunder are likely.** If Frederiksen cannot assemble a viable government with SF and RV, the process will restart. The historical pattern shows 2–4 rounds in difficult formations.
- **The kingmaker (Moderaterne/Løkke) will extract a high price.** Løkke's position is comparable to RV in the 1980s or DF in the 2000s — the indispensable swing party. The price will likely include cabinet seats and veto power over policy areas Løkke considers core (foreign policy, governance reform).
- **The EL question is the decisive variable.** Every plausible government including Moderaterne requires either active EL support (votes for the budget) or at minimum EL toleration (abstaining rather than voting against). Løkke's declared refusal to accept EL dependence collides with arithmetic reality. History suggests this kind of publicly stated red line gets quietly walked back during negotiations — as LA's topskat demands were abandoned in 2016.
- **A single-party S minority (Frederiksen repeating the 2019 model) is arithmetically possible but politically difficult** without the forståelsespapir partners willing to provide a reliable 90+ seat base. The 2019 model worked because rød blok had 91 seats; with only 84, the gap is much larger.

The ultimate lesson of 50 years of Danish government formation is captured in a paradox: **the system's stability comes from its tolerance of instability.** Negative parliamentarism means governments don't need to be strong — they just need to not be actively opposed. Budget votes are the annual stress test, but the norm of opposition abstention and cross-bloc deal-making means that determined governments almost always survive. Denmark has never had a government fall because it literally could not pass a budget. It has had governments fall because prime ministers chose to resign (AJ 1982), because scandals destroyed legitimacy (Schlüter 1993), because støttepartier issued ultimatums that triggered elections (RV to Frederiksen 2022), or because elections produced new arithmetic. The formation process is messy, slow, and often produces unexpected results — but it has never failed to produce a government.
