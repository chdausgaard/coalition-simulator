# Danmarksdemokraterne after the 2026 election: leverage, vetoes, and the path from here

Inger Støjberg's Danmarksdemokraterne enters government formation weakened but not irrelevant. DD lost four seats on 24 March 2026, dropping from 14 to approximately 10 mandates (~5.8%), overtaken by a resurgent Dansk Folkeparti (16 seats). Yet with the blue bloc at 77 seats and Moderaterne's 14 swing mandates as kingmaker, every blue party's cooperation matters — and DD's 10 seats are enough to make or break a blue-anchored majority. Støjberg's election night message was unambiguous: she pointed to Venstre's Troels Lund Poulsen as kongelig undersøger and "fuldt og fast" (fully and firmly) rejected pointing to Moderaterne. Mette Frederiksen was ultimately appointed kongelig undersøger, with negotiations beginning at Marienborg on 28 March. DD is likely headed for opposition, but its stance on Løkke, its relationship with Venstre, and its willingness to deal will shape whatever government emerges.

---

## How DD built its identity in opposition to SVM

Danmarksdemokraterne was founded in June 2022 after Støjberg's rigsret conviction and departure from Venstre, winning **14 seats at 8.1%** in November 2022. Its founding cadre was roughly one-third former Venstre members, one-third former Dansk Folkeparti members, and one-third political newcomers — a composition that defined its ideological positioning as a hybrid between Venstre's centre-right liberalism and DF's national-conservative welfare populism.

During the SVM government period (2022–2026), DD consolidated around four signature issues. The most defining was **absolute opposition to a CO2 tax on agriculture**, which Støjberg called "en tudetosset idé" (a completely idiotic idea). DD organized "CO2 rallies" in spring 2024 near Randers and Svinninge, complete with branded protest beer called "En Nejer" — attended by some 1,500 people. At the 2024 landsmøde in Herning, Støjberg launched a second rural identity campaign under the slogan "Nej til jernmarker, ja til kornmarker" (no to iron fields, yes to corn fields), opposing large-scale solar parks on agricultural land. Immigration remained a cornerstone issue, though the framing shifted from asylum flows toward enforcement — deportations, work requirements, and the failure to return Syrian refugees. The fourth pillar was a broader rural Denmark agenda, positioning DD as the party for "Produktionsdanmark" against Copenhagen-centric policymaking.

Berlingske called DD "an ideological riddle" as late as March 2023, and the description remained partially apt. The party's principprogram (August 2023) claims both liberalist and national-conservative elements. What clarified DD's identity was less ideology than posture: **it was consistently the most aggressive blue-bloc critic of the SVM model**, treating the broad coalition as a betrayal of bourgeois voters. Yet DD simultaneously demonstrated pragmatism by joining cross-party agreements on defence, kontanthjælp, psychiatry, and the 2024 elderly care reform — proving it could negotiate with the very government it opposed.

## The 2026 campaign: 12 billion for rural Denmark and a firm "no" to Løkke

DD's flagship 2026 campaign proposal was a **47-point rural districts plan worth 12 billion DKK**, presented on 17 March in Brønderslev. The plan included 6 billion DKK to reduce petrol and diesel levies (funded by cancelling the Vejle Fjord rail bridge project), abolition of the kilometerafgift for trucks (1.5 billion), elimination of the flyafgift (flight tax), a 50% increased commuter deduction for residents of 25 designated rural municipalities, relocation of 10,000 state jobs from cities, and an obligatory "rural districts filter" for all new legislation. Landdistrikternes Fællesråd praised it as "the most comprehensive rural policy plan in years."

On taxation, DD firmly opposed the formueskat (wealth tax) proposed by Socialdemokratiet and SF, aligning with the entire blue bloc. The party's own tax agenda centred on reducing levies rather than rates — abolishing fuel taxes, lowering food VAT, and extending the 2023 tax reform's bundfradrag increases. On climate, DD proposed **delaying the 2030 climate target by two years to 2032** and joined all five blue-bloc parties in a joint Jyllands-Posten kronik guaranteeing they would lift the nuclear power ban. DD's climate philosophy was "carrots, not sticks" — incentivise farmers rather than taxing them.

On immigration, Støjberg focused the 26 February partilederdebat on Frederiksen's failures: annual immigration from Muslim-majority countries had increased 124% under SVM, the Rwanda deportation centre never materialized, and Syrian refugees had not been sent home. DD's demands were consistent: work requirements from day one, more deportations of criminal foreigners, mandatory return when conflicts end, and stricter citizenship rules.

The most consequential campaign development for DD was the **"Borgerligt Fundament"** — a joint manifesto signed in January 2025 by Støjberg (DD), Alex Vanopslagh (LA), Mona Juul (K), and Morten Messerschmidt (DF). This document, followed by a "Borgerligt Konvent" in September 2025 and a "50 changes in 100 days" programme, represented DD's deepest institutional cooperation with the rest of blue bloc. It made DD a co-owner of a shared alternative governing vision — a significant legitimation step for a party barely three years old.

## Coalition signals: one ultimate demand and a nuanced veto

Støjberg articulated DD's negotiating position with a revealing formulation in a November 2023 Altinget interview with Erik Holstein: **"We have only ONE ultimate demand: we must have a blue prime minister."** Everything else was negotiable. This single demand — not a ministerial post, not a specific policy concession, but the colour of the statsminister — defined DD's coalition calculus.

On election night, Støjberg delivered precisely this message. She told DR: "Ja, det ser jo ud til at blive Venstre – med et mulehår. Og hvis det er sådan, så er det Troels Lund, der skal være vores kongelige undersøger." She fully rejected pointing to Moderaterne as formateur. But — and this distinction matters — **she did not completely reject M's presence in a government**. The DR analysis noted Støjberg "does not reject that Løkke could take part in that government," distinguishing between M as a leader (unacceptable) and M as a participant (conditionally tolerable). POV International captured the nuance on election eve: while Messerschmidt had "forsvoret" (sworn off) supporting any government with Løkke, "Det samme har Inger Støjberg. Næsten." ("The same has Inger Støjberg. Almost.")

DD drew several hard red lines during and after the campaign. The refusal to point to Mette Frederiksen as PM was categorical — Støjberg publicly demanded Troels Lund Poulsen make the same commitment and expressed frustration when he wouldn't. The opposition to Moderaterne leading government formation was firm. On policy, opposition to the CO2 tax on agriculture and to the formueskat functioned as non-negotiable positions. Softer signals included DD's evident willingness to support a V-led government and its participation in the Borgerligt Fundament framework — suggesting DD would be a reliable støtteparti for any pure-blue configuration.

## The formateur question and Løkke's kingmaker shadow

DD's formateur preference was straightforward: the largest blue-bloc party should lead. When the final count showed Venstre narrowly ahead of Liberal Alliance (10.1% vs. 9.4%), Støjberg endorsed Troels Lund Poulsen without hesitation — "med et mulehår" (by a whisker), she noted. Støjberg did not put herself forward as a PM candidate, nor did she endorse any other blue-bloc leader over TLP.

DD's relationship with Venstre is the most important bilateral dynamic for blue-bloc cohesion. Støjberg told Altinget: **"It's not far-fetched for me to work with Troels, if the politics are right."** She observed that TLP had "softened" and suggested he was more willing to return to the blue family than his predecessor Jakob Ellemann-Jensen had been. Yet friction persisted throughout the campaign. Støjberg was publicly "irriteret" (irritated) at TLP's refusal to unambiguously reject pegging on Frederiksen. When TLP proposed a "blå midterregering" (blue centre government) potentially including Moderaterne, Støjberg responded by calling Løkke "rød" — red — a cutting dismissal of M's bourgeois credentials.

On election night, Støjberg arrived at DD's valgfest and notably **dodged questions about who she had spoken to**, answering only "Flere" (several). She told supporters: "Det ser ud til, at danskerne gerne ser nogle ændringer. Det ser ud til, at danskerne ønsker sig en blå regering" — framing the result as a mandate for blue government despite DD's own losses. This positive spin was reinforced by spokesperson Susie Jessen, who characterised the seat loss as "consolidation" for a "still new party."

A revealing procedural detail: **DD's press office sent the email on behalf of multiple parties cancelling the traditional election night partilederdebat** on TV 2 and DR — breaking a 60-year tradition. Commentators interpreted this as a strategic move to avoid unscripted confrontations at a sensitive moment, suggesting coordinated blue-bloc messaging discipline.

## What experts see: pragmatism constrained by personal history

The expert consensus positions DD as more pragmatic than its rhetoric suggests but structurally constrained by the Løkke feud. Erik Holstein of Altinget described DD as having "proven more pragmatic than expected," noting Støjberg's successful pursuit of a **"skørhedsfaktor på nul"** (zero fragility factor) — meaning disciplined, reliable parliamentary operations with no internal defections. Christian Egander Skov, writing in Altinget, went further, arguing DD could become "bridge-builders and the guarantor for a folkelig-grounded borgerlig politics" and was "not unthinkable in a future broad government."

DD's actual parliamentary record supports the pragmatism thesis. During the 2022–2026 Folketing, the party participated in cross-party agreements on defence, cash benefits, psychiatry, research, the 2024 elderly care reform, and gang crime legislation — all negotiated with the SVM government it nominally opposed. DD also joined the 2023 tax reform agreement alongside the government, Konservative, Radikale, and Nye Borgerlige. This track record of deal-making contradicts any characterisation of DD as a purely destructive opposition force.

However, the mandate arithmetic severely limits DD's leverage in the current formation. Hans Engell, the veteran political commentator, predicted on election night that the most likely outcome was an S + SF + M + RV government with Enhedslisten and Alternativet as support parties — a scenario placing DD firmly in opposition. **Sune Steffen Hansen** of TV 2 identified Moderaterne and DF as "the two great winners" and "the poles that will define Danish politics going forward" — notably excluding DD from the decisive actors. With Mette Frederiksen appointed as kongelig undersøger and negotiations beginning at Marienborg, the trajectory favours a centre-left government in which DD has no role.

The credibility assessment of DD's red lines splits along two dimensions. The refusal to support Frederiksen as PM appears genuinely binding — Støjberg's entire political identity is built on this opposition. The anti-Løkke stance is harder to evaluate. It is **simultaneously personal and ideological**: personal because Støjberg believes Løkke "formed an entire party to keep her out of Danish politics," and ideological because M's immigration policy (which DD characterised as supporting "importing 50,000 Africans") is antithetical to DD's core platform. Yet the conditional openness to M's participation in a blue government — as opposed to M's leadership of it — suggests the veto is negotiable in practice, especially if immigration policy concessions were on offer.

## The Støjberg-Løkke fault line and what DD actually wants

The DD-M hostility is the single most consequential interpersonal constraint on Danish government formation. Its origins predate both parties' existence: Støjberg was forced out as Venstre vice-chair in December 2020, left V in February 2021, faced the rigsret in December 2021, and founded DD in June 2022. Løkke departed Venstre separately to form Moderaterne. Støjberg told Altinget: **"It almost seemed as if Løkke stood for election to prevent me from getting influence."** She called him "principløs" (unprincipled).

The critical analytical question is whether this feud is a binding constraint or a negotiable preference. The evidence suggests it is **binding at the leadership level but conditionally flexible at the institutional level**. DD will not point to Løkke. DD will not accept Løkke as PM. DD almost certainly will not accept Løkke as a minister on DD's mandates. But DD has signalled — through careful non-denial — that it could tolerate M's presence in a broader blue government where M participates on its own mandates rather than DD's. This positions DD between DF's absolute rejection (Messerschmidt vowed to "æde" — devour — Løkke) and LA's pragmatic acceptance.

DD's relationship with Venstre is warmer but watchful. The two parties share personnel (Mads Fuglede defected from V to DD in 2024) and policy overlap on rural issues and immigration. Støjberg's endorsement of TLP as kongelig undersøger was immediate and unequivocal. But the relationship carries a trust deficit rooted in Venstre's SVM participation. Støjberg's recurring frustration during the campaign — that TLP wouldn't clearly reject pegging on Frederiksen — reflects DD's core anxiety: that Venstre will again choose cross-bloc accommodation over blue-bloc loyalty.

DD's demands extend well beyond immigration, though immigration remains the non-negotiable entry point. The party's revealed priorities, based on its legislative participation and campaign emphasis, include **elderly care investment** (1 billion DKK annually, fixed care teams, dignity framework), **rural infrastructure** (the 12 billion DKK plan, decentralisation of state jobs, mobile network coverage), **agricultural protection** (no CO2 tax, no solar parks on farmland, delayed climate targets), and **tax relief for working Danes** (lower levies on fuel, food, and commuting). The foreign aid cuts DD proposes — 6.5 billion from development assistance — are both a financing mechanism and an ideological signal about prioritising domestic needs.

---

## Conclusion: a constructive partner waiting for a government that may never form

DD's post-election position is paradoxical. The party has demonstrated genuine pragmatism, legislative seriousness, and coalition discipline — qualities that would make it a reliable støtteparti or even coalition partner. Its demands are specific and achievable rather than maximalist. Its parliamentary track record shows constructive engagement across the aisle. Yet the government most likely to form — a centre-left coalition anchored by S, SF, and M — is precisely the configuration DD exists to oppose.

The most important signal from election night was not what Støjberg said but what she did not say. She did not categorically rule out M's participation in a blue government. She did not escalate the personal attacks on Løkke. She framed the result in terms of blue-bloc mandate rather than DD's specific losses. This suggests a leader preparing for a long opposition stretch while keeping the door ajar for influence — consistent with the "zero fragility factor" approach that has defined DD's parliamentary strategy since 2022. Whether that door leads anywhere depends almost entirely on Løkke's choice, and Løkke's incentives currently point left.
