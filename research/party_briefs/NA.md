# Four seats that won't show their hand

The two Greenlandic members elected to the Danish Folketing on March 24, 2026, have both **refused to declare who they will support as prime minister** — creating a small but potent wildcard in an already deadlocked parliament. The Faroese seats split predictably along bloc lines (one red, one blue), but the real story is in Nuuk: for the first time since 2001, both Greenlandic mandates belong to explicitly pro-independence parties, and neither will commit to a bloc. In a Folketing where **84 red seats face 77 blue seats with 14 Moderaterne in the middle**, those two undeclared Greenlandic votes could shape the arithmetic of government formation — even if they cannot, by themselves, resolve it.

---

## Who won: two new Greenlanders, two familiar Faroese faces

**Greenland — Seat 1: Qarsoq Høegh-Dam (Naleraq)**
Elected with **4,615 personal votes** (21.6% of all Greenlandic ballots), making him the election's biggest individual vote-getter in Greenland. He is the half-brother of outgoing MP Aki-Matilda Høegh-Dam, who chose not to run. This marks the **first time Naleraq has won a Folketing seat through an actual election** — the party previously held one only because Aki-Matilda switched from Siumut to Naleraq mid-term. Høegh-Dam is a party consultant with a background in Greenlandic domestic politics. He has demanded Danish soldiers be relocated away from populated areas in Greenland and called for massive public investment under a framework that preserves Greenland's right to subsequent independence.

**Greenland — Seat 2: Naaja Hjelholt Nathanielsen (Inuit Ataqatigiit / IA)**
A sitting Greenlandic government minister for business, raw materials, energy, justice, and equality, Nathanielsen won with **1,953 personal votes** — fewer than Demokraatit's Anna Wangenheim individually, but IA's **28.6% party vote share** (the highest of any Greenlandic party) secured the seat. She will vacate her ministerial posts to take up the Folketing seat. IA has traditionally leaned red — former leader Kuupik Kleist once said his "little finger points to the left" — but Nathanielsen has pointedly refused to pre-commit.

**Faroe Islands — Seat 1: Sjúrður Skaale (Javnaðarflokkurin / Social Democrats)**
Re-elected with a record-smashing **10,000+ personal votes** out of 29,110 valid Faroese ballots. TV2 called it a "Faroese super-success," noting that Skaale "works like his own unique planet in Faroese politics." He has served continuously since 2011, won Denmark's "Speech of the Year" in 2024, and aligns firmly with the **red bloc** through Javnaðarflokkurin's cooperation agreement with Socialdemokratiet.

**Faroe Islands — Seat 2: Anna Falkenberg (Sambandsflokkurin / Union Party)**
Re-elected with **5,304 personal votes** (also a party record), on 25.2% of the Faroese party vote. Born in 1996, first elected in 2022, Falkenberg represents Venstre's Faroese sister party and sits squarely in the **blue bloc**. She is the only major Faroese politician who opposes independence, arguing the Rigsfællesskab (the Danish Realm) should be developed rather than dismantled.

The headline shift is **Siumut's disappearance**. The party that had held a Greenlandic Folketing seat at every election since 2001 collapsed to 16.4% (down 21.3 percentage points), losing its mandate to Naleraq. Both Greenlandic seats now belong to parties whose primary ideological commitment is independence — not Danish domestic bloc politics.

## Bloc alignment is 1-1-2 with two unknowns

The Faroese split is a known quantity: **Skaale adds one to the red bloc's 84, bringing it to 85; Falkenberg adds one to the blue bloc's 77, bringing it to 78.** This pattern — one red, one blue from the Faroes — has recurred in election after election and was widely expected.

The Greenlandic seats are the live question. Neither Qarsoq Høegh-Dam nor Naaja Nathanielsen has said who they would point to (pege på) in the kongerunde, the royal consultation with King Frederik expected to begin on March 25. Nathanielsen told DR on election night: **"Det tager vi senere"** ("We'll take that later"). Høegh-Dam told Sermitsiaq he plans to consult with his party base before traveling to Copenhagen, and **would not name a preferred prime minister**.

Historically, Greenlandic representatives have leaned red. IA cooperates with SF; Siumut cooperated with Socialdemokratiet. But Naleraq is ideologically harder to classify — the party defines itself through Greenlandic sovereignty, not Danish left-right politics. Party leader Pele Broberg stated before the election: **"We are neither red nor blue, and we are not for sale. But the candidate who needs our mandates can always enter a dialogue with us."**

The historical record shows North Atlantic mandates have been decisive in **1971, 1998, 2007, 2011, and 2022**. In 2011, three red-leaning North Atlantic members signed a joint declaration of principles to leverage their collective support for Helle Thorning-Schmidt's government. In 2022, all four North Atlantic members supported the SVM coalition, giving it 93 mandates — a comfortable majority. Researcher Mette Marie Stæhr Harder of the University of Copenhagen confirmed to KNR on election night that tight Danish results historically activate the North Atlantic seats as kingmakers.

## Policy leverage centers on independence, the Self-Government Act, and Dansk Folkeparti

Both Greenlandic members will extract a price for their support, and the currency is sovereignty policy. Nathanielsen published a pre-election article on Altinget demanding that **any incoming government commit to revising the Self-Government Act and reinterpreting the Constitution** to establish genuine equality between Greenland, the Faroes, and Denmark. She even questioned whether Greenland should continue sending representatives to the Folketing at all. Høegh-Dam demanded Denmark fulfill its obligations under the Self-Government Act through massive investment — framed not as a gift but as a legal duty — with full preservation of Greenland's right to independence.

**One party poses a near-absolute obstacle to Greenlandic support for a blue-bloc government.** Dansk Folkeparti's Morten Messerschmidt proposed that Greenlandic independence require a referendum held in Denmark — effectively giving Danish voters a veto over Greenlandic self-determination. Høegh-Dam called this "very un-Danish" and an attack on democratic rights. With DF holding **16 blue-bloc seats** (up from just 4 in 2022), any blue-led government that includes or relies on DF becomes extremely difficult for Greenlandic members to support. This structural incompatibility nudges both Greenlandic mandates toward the red bloc or toward demanding explicit exclusion of DF from any governing arrangement they back.

For the Faroese members, the key issues are more economic and constitutional. Skaale supports the gradual phase-out of the Faroese block grant (reduced from 641.8 million DKK to **541.8 million DKK in 2026**), arguing the relationship should be "based on cooperation, not dependency." Falkenberg defended the controversial Faroese fisheries agreement with Russia and wants the Rigsfællesskab strengthened rather than dissolved. Five of six Faroese parliamentary parties now favor some form of statehood or independence, but since Skaale and Falkenberg are already locked into their respective blocs, their leverage in government formation is limited compared to the Greenlandic seats.

## The Trump crisis reshaped every calculation

The entire election unfolded in the shadow of the most acute geopolitical crisis in Danish post-war history. In January 2026, Trump declared the United States "needs" Greenland, threatened 10-25% tariffs on Denmark, and posted on social media demanding NATO allies force Denmark out. European allies deployed troops to Greenland in solidarity — soldiers from Sweden, Norway, Finland, Germany, and France joined Danish forces. Denmark reportedly prepared contingency plans to **destroy Greenland's airport runways** to prevent a U.S. military landing. The crisis de-escalated at Davos on January 21, when Trump pledged not to use force or tariffs, accepting a NATO framework that respects Danish sovereignty while updating the 1951 Greenland Defense Agreement.

This crisis produced three political consequences relevant to the North Atlantic mandates. First, it generated a **rally-around-the-flag effect** that boosted Frederiksen's standing, directly motivating her February 26 snap election call. Second, Denmark dramatically increased Greenland spending: the 2026-2029 financial bill included **400 million DKK annually** in new Greenland initiatives (nearly 9% on top of the existing ~4.5 billion DKK block grant), covering healthcare, child protection, and equal pay for Greenlandic-stationed police officers. Third, and most paradoxically, it **paused the independence movement** — when forced to choose between Washington and Copenhagen, Greenlandic leaders chose Copenhagen, and outgoing IA MP Aaja Chemnitz observed that "Greenland and Denmark are standing much closer together than we have ever done before in history."

Yet the crisis also raised the stakes for any government formation negotiation touching the Arctic. Greenlandic MPs now arrive in Copenhagen with heightened leverage: Denmark needs their loyalty not just for parliamentary arithmetic but for the geopolitical credibility of the Realm itself.

## How decisive are these four seats, really?

The arithmetic is instructive. With 175 Danish seats distributed as Red 84, Blue 77, and Moderaterne 14, the magic number of 90 (a majority in the 179-seat Folketing) is unreachable by either bloc without Lars Løkke Rasmussen's Moderaterne. Adding the North Atlantic seats changes the margins but not the fundamental reality:

| Scenario | Red total | Blue total | Red + Moderaterne | Blue + Moderaterne |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Both Greenland → red | 87 | 78 | 101 | 92 |
| Both Greenland → blue | 85 | 80 | 99 | 94 |
| IA red, Naleraq blue | 86 | 79 | 100 | 93 |
| Both abstain | 85 | 78 | 99 | 92 |

**In every scenario, Moderaterne remain indispensable.** The North Atlantic seats cannot independently deliver a majority to either bloc. TV2's Ask Rostrup acknowledged this while emphasizing that the seats still matter enormously: "Regardless of whether the North Atlantic votes fall on the red or blue side, their support will be far from free. They will pressure-test the Danish parties on what they want with the Realm, Greenland, and the Faroe Islands."

The seats become genuinely decisive only if a narrow coalition can be assembled to **exactly 89 seats**, where a single North Atlantic vote tips it to 90. This is unlikely given the current distribution but cannot be ruled out during complex multi-party negotiations. More realistically, the Greenlandic members' leverage lies in their ability to add **legitimacy and comfort margin** to whatever government emerges — a coalition with 93 mandates governs more securely than one with 90.

## The kongerunde begins with maximum uncertainty

As of March 25, King Frederik's first kongerunde is expected to begin today. The process is already complicated by three competing visions: Mette Frederiksen wants to lead a red or red-Moderaterne government. Venstre's Troels Lund Poulsen dropped a "bomb" on election night by declaring he would **only** enter a "blue centre government" and ruled out continuing in government with Socialdemokratiet — killing the prospect of a renewed SVM coalition. Lars Løkke Rasmussen, whose 14 Moderaterne seats make him the indispensable kingmaker, called on both sides to "climb down from those trees" and volunteered to serve as kongelig undersøger (royal investigator).

The Greenlandic members' silence is strategic. By withholding their preferences, Høegh-Dam and Nathanielsen maximize their negotiating position. Any formateur who wants their support will need to make concrete commitments on revising the Self-Government Act, constitutional reform, continued investment in Greenland, and — critically — keeping Dansk Folkeparti's sovereignty-restricting proposals off the table. DR's Jens Ringberg noted the Greenlandic mandates could be decisive **if** two conditions are met: the parties choose to participate in the decision, and the result is close enough to matter.

TV2's defense correspondent Anders Lomholt, reporting from Nuuk, offered a more skeptical assessment: "De har ikke så meget at handle med" ("They don't have much to bargain with") — because Moderaterne's 14 seats will resolve the government question regardless. But this underestimates the symbolic and political weight of Greenlandic support in the current geopolitical moment. A Danish government that lacks Greenlandic backing would face awkward questions about Realm unity at precisely the time when that unity is being tested by external pressure.

## Conclusion

The 2026 North Atlantic mandates represent a structural shift in Greenlandic representation — two pro-independence parties replacing the traditional IA-Siumut duopoly — embedded in a geopolitical context that amplifies their significance far beyond four votes. The Faroese split (Skaale red, Falkenberg blue) is stable and predictable. The real drama lies with Qarsoq Høegh-Dam and Naaja Nathanielsen, whose refusal to declare bloc allegiance is both a negotiating tactic and an ideological statement: Greenland's interests transcend Danish left-right politics.

The cold arithmetic says Moderaterne, not the North Atlantic, will determine who governs. But arithmetic and politics are different things. A government formed without Greenlandic consent — or worse, one dependent on Dansk Folkeparti's sovereignty-threatening proposals — would face a legitimacy problem that no mandate count can solve. The four North Atlantic members may not tip the balance, but they will shape the terms on which that balance is struck. Government formation talks could take weeks, and the signals from Nuuk will be watched as closely as those from Christiansborg.
