# Dansk Folkeparti's comeback and the coalition deadlock it created

Dansk Folkeparti tripled its mandate count from 5 to **16 seats** on 24 March 2026, completing one of the most dramatic resurrections in Danish electoral history — yet the party's own iron-clad vetoes may have locked it out of power entirely. Under Morten Messerschmidt's combative leadership, DF won 9.1% of the vote by running the hardest-line immigration platform in Danish history and waging rhetorical war on Lars Løkke Rasmussen, only to discover that the arithmetic of the new Folketing makes Løkke's Moderaterne the indispensable kingmaker. The result is a structural paradox: DF is too big to ignore but too hostile to Løkke to participate in any government that can actually command a majority. This tension — between electoral triumph and political isolation — will define DF's role in the coming parliamentary period.

## From near-extinction to euphoria in four years

DF's trajectory from 2022 to 2026 is a story of institutional near-death followed by ideological reinvention. When Messerschmidt won a contested leadership election in January 2022 — the first in DF history — **11 of the party's 16 MPs defected** within months, many joining Inger Støjberg's Danmarksdemokraterne (DD). The November 2022 election yielded DF's worst-ever result: 2.6% and 5 seats, barely clearing the 2% threshold. Political scientist Peter Nedergaard declared in 2022 that DF was "a party in deep, deep crisis, fighting for its survival."

The turnaround began with Messerschmidt's acquittal in the MELD/FELD EU fraud case in December 2022, which removed a major credibility burden. Two Nye Borgerlige MPs defected *to* DF in February 2023, raising the group to 7. By 2024, internal turmoil had subsided as dissidents had already left. Former DF press chief Søren Søndergaard observed that "those who couldn't stand Messerschmidt have left — the remaining feel a sense of shared betrayal by former colleagues." DF secured one European Parliament seat in June 2024 (6.4%) and joined the new Patriots for Europe group alongside Fidesz, National Rally, and FPÖ. Jordan Bardella visited Copenhagen for DF's September 2024 conference to formalize a "working partnership."

The 2026 election confirmed the comeback. DF's 9.1% came overwhelmingly at DD's expense — **19% of 2022 DD voters switched to DF**, according to Epinion polling. DD fell from 14 to 10 seats while DF rose from 5 to 16. DR analyst Pia Glud Munksgaard concluded: "DF is simply snatching voters back from Danmarksdemokraterne." At DF's election party in a tent at Christiansborg's Indre Slotsgård, Messerschmidt opened with "Dansk Folkeparti er tilbage" as supporters celebrated what he called "a really, really historic night for DF and thus for Denmark." The evening also marked founder Pia Kjærsgaard's farewell after 42 years in parliament.

## Messerschmidt's national-conservative reinvention

Under Messerschmidt, DF has undergone a decisive ideological sharpening — not a pivot but an intensification. Professor Peter Nedergaard of Copenhagen University characterized the shift as a "return to the party's conservative roots," specifically its 1995 founding ideology emphasizing national sovereignty, cultural preservation, and welfare nationalism. Where predecessor Kristian Thulesen Dahl maintained a pragmatic ambiguity — sometimes flirting with Socialdemokratiet, sometimes softening EU criticism — Messerschmidt has planted DF unambiguously on the national-conservative right.

The shift manifests across every major policy dimension. On **immigration**, DF's signature issue, Messerschmidt has moved from restriction to reversal. His campaign's "ultimativt krav" demands **net emigration of Muslims** — more people with Muslim backgrounds leaving Denmark than arriving — as a non-negotiable condition for supporting any blue government. The party's March 19 platform, "Dansk Fortid, Dansk Fremtid," proposed reviewing all citizenships granted in the past 20 years, capping new citizenships at 250 per year, moving all asylum processing to third countries, and what it explicitly called "de-Islamization." Messerschmidt stated: "We don't just want to stop Islamization — we want to roll it back."

On **climate and energy**, DF withdrew from the 2019 climate agreement in summer 2024 after the Green Tripartite Agreement introduced a CO2 tax on agriculture. Messerschmidt declared he wanted to "end climate fanaticism." The party made cheap fuel its campaign centerpiece, partnering with Go'on gas stations to sell petrol at **9.50 DKK/liter** (reflecting EU minimum tax levels) at 200 stations on election day. DF demands cutting fuel taxes by 2.68 DKK/liter for petrol and 1.80 DKK/liter for diesel — a measure the climate think tank Concito estimated would increase Danish CO2 emissions by up to 2.5 million tons annually. DF also supports continuing North Sea oil and gas extraction beyond the 2050 phase-out and joined the blue bloc agreement to lift Denmark's ban on nuclear power.

On **EU policy**, Messerschmidt executed perhaps his most surprising maneuver. In a Berlingske interview timed with his campaign book "Dansk Forår," he announced DF would no longer pursue Danish EU exit or a referendum on membership — a dramatic reversal of the party's longstanding position. His formulation: **"I'm more interested in getting the EU out of Denmark than getting Denmark out of the EU."** The shift was pragmatically motivated; the EU's strong backing of Denmark during Trump's Greenland threats made anti-EU positions electorally toxic. Socialdemokratiet's Christian Rabjerg Madsen called the reversal "completely incredible."

On **welfare and taxation**, DF continued branding itself as "blå bloks bløde hjerte" (the blue bloc's soft heart). Its 2030 fiscal plan included **1,000 DKK monthly extra for pensioners** (7.2 billion DKK annually), 10 billion for healthcare, 6.5 billion for eldercare, abolition of the inheritance tax (4.35 billion DKK), and a medicine co-payment cap of 1,100 DKK/month. DF opposes the wealth tax proposed by Socialdemokratiet and rejects raising the pension age to 70. Financing comes primarily from abolishing all development aid (~20 billion DKK) and bureaucracy cuts (~13 billion DKK).

## The double veto that defines the deadlock

DF's post-election positioning is dominated by a single structural problem: the party's **categorical rejection of both Mette Frederiksen and Lars Løkke Rasmussen** as government ministers. On election night, Messerschmidt stated without qualification: "DF's mandater kommer aldrig nogensinde til at gøre hverken Mette Frederiksen eller Lars Løkke til minister" (DF's mandates will never ever make either Frederiksen or Løkke a minister). He then escalated with a remark that became the most quoted line of election night: **"We will devour him and his people every single day"** — referring to Løkke.

This veto is framed in policy terms, not merely personal ones. Messerschmidt called Løkke "synonymous with free immigration, more money to the EU, and higher food prices." The substantive anchoring makes it harder to walk back than a purely personal grievance. The veto is also reciprocal: at Moderaterne's March 7 congress in Aarhus, Løkke called DF "unfit to sit in government" and said he would not join any government "dependent on Dansk Folkeparti." After Messerschmidt's "devour" speech, Moderaterne's Jakob Engel-Schmidt remarked dryly on March 25: "That doesn't sound like an obvious cooperation metaphor."

DF points to **Troels Lund Poulsen (Venstre) as kongelig undersøger** (royal investigator/formateur), but attaches a critical condition: the formateur must "beforehand exclude forming a government with Frederiksen and Løkke." If Troels Lund Poulsen cannot provide this guarantee, Messerschmidt indicated he would instead recommend himself, Inger Støjberg, or Alex Vanopslagh. The acceptable PMs for DF are Troels Lund Poulsen (preferred), Vanopslagh, or Messerschmidt himself — and no one else.

There is, however, **one narrow opening**. Messerschmidt has not explicitly ruled out being a support party for a blue government where Moderaterne is *also* a support party — that is, a configuration where neither Løkke nor any Moderaterne member holds a ministerial portfolio. His precise formulation was: "Lars Løkke Rasmussen will not become a minister on my mandates." This leaves a theoretical scenario in which V, LA, K, and DD form a government, with both DF and M providing external support. Whether this slender distinction survives the pressures of actual negotiation remains to be seen.

## What experts say about DF's real leverage

The commentator consensus is that DF's 16 mandates give it significant but paradoxically constrained influence. The fundamental problem is arithmetic: blue bloc totals just 77 seats, well short of the 90 needed for a majority. Without Moderaterne's 14 seats, no blue government is viable. But DF's Løkke veto blocks the obvious path to incorporating those seats.

Veteran commentator **Hans Engell** predicted on the morning of March 25 that the likely government would be **S + SF + M + RV**, with Enhedslisten and Alternativet as support parties — a formation that bypasses DF and the entire blue bloc. Former Løkke adviser Jacob Bruun described Moderaterne's position as a "dream result" — "Løkke has now gotten what he dreamed of since 2019: no government can be formed based on either the red or blue far flank." TV2 commentator Lawand Hiwa Namo offered the most incisive pre-election observation: **"We have yet to pressure-test Messerschmidt on how ultimative he actually is."**

Assessments of Messerschmidt's negotiating style converge on **confrontational rather than pragmatic**. His former mentor Thulesen Dahl called him a "bridge-burner" on DR's P1, comparing his tactics to Trump's and calling his approach "hopeless" and "crazy." Altinget's John Wagner reviewed Messerschmidt's campaign book and concluded his "political visions are incompatible with a possible future blue government." TV2 analysts framed the coming period as defined by "the two great poles: Lars Løkke Rasmussen versus Messerschmidt — that is where the energy is."

The credibility of DF's red lines divides opinion. On one hand, Messerschmidt has invested enormous political capital in the Løkke veto — walking it back would devastate his brand. On the other hand, DF has a 20-year track record as the loyal blue bloc support party, and the immigration ultimatum contains built-in flexibility. DF's Anders Vistisen clarified that "the formulation in the government program needn't say that more Muslims should leave than arrive, but there must be initiatives that achieve that consequence." DR's Magtens Morgenbrief noted: **"DF is ultimative on the goal, but not on how to get there, which leaves room for negotiation."** The morning after the election, Messerschmidt opened the door for Løkke to be *støtteparti* for a blue government (without the Moderates or Social Democrats in it). 

## DF and DD — rivals who need each other

DF's relationship with Danmarksdemokraterne is defined by competitive rivalry, not alliance. DD was founded in 2022 from DF's own defectors — 11 of DD's 14 original MPs had DF backgrounds. The parties compete for the same voter pool of culturally conservative, immigration-skeptical Danes. In 2026, DF won that competition decisively: DF gained 11 seats while DD lost 4, with Epinion data showing a direct voter transfer. Geographic analysis by Kasper Møller Hansen (Copenhagen University) found a territorial split: "Messerschmidt has conquered Zealand; Støjberg dominates Jutland."

The two parties are broadly aligned on policy — both demand restrictive immigration, both back Troels Lund Poulsen as PM, both reject Løkke — but they are **not coordinating strategy**. Messerschmidt dismissed questions about the relationship on March 9: "I don't think anything has fundamentally changed between our parties." The lack of coordination reflects genuine rivalry: DF needs to outflank DD to justify its existence, and Messerschmidt's more extreme positions (net Muslim emigration, EU exit rhetoric) serve partly to differentiate DF from DD's slightly softer profile.

In coalition talks, DF and DD function as parallel but independent actors. Both add to the blue bloc's 77-seat total but neither can deliver a majority alone or together without Moderaterne. Their combined weight (26 seats) theoretically gives the nationalist right significant voice, but the mutual Løkke veto from both DF and DD makes this academic unless the formateur process produces an innovative configuration.

## The government participation question

Perhaps the most consequential long-term question is whether DF will finally cross the threshold from support party to governing party. DF has **never held government office** despite decades of influence — a distinction unique among major Danish parties. In 2015, when DF won a record 37 seats and was the largest party in blue bloc, it chose to remain outside government. That decision contributed to voter disillusionment and the party's subsequent decline.

Messerschmidt appears determined not to repeat the 2015 mistake. On election day he declared his goal was "a blue government with a strong Dansk Folkeparti," and Kristeligt Dagblad reported: "DF has never been a government party, but now it shall be, if Messerschmidt has his way." Yet his actual demands emphasize *policy outcomes* (net Muslim emigration, lower fuel taxes) rather than *ministerial posts*. This ambiguity may be strategic — keeping options open — but it also reflects a party culture built around influence-without-responsibility.

The deeper problem is that Altinget commentator John Wagner's assessment appears accurate: Messerschmidt's political visions, as articulated in "Dansk Forår," are fundamentally incompatible with the compromises required for coalition government. A party demanding net Muslim emigration, abolition of development aid, withdrawal from climate agreements, and EU reform from within would struggle to find coalition partners willing to accept even a fraction of this platform. DF's historical role as the party that shapes the immigration debate from the outside may prove more durable than Messerschmidt's stated ambitions.

## What happens next

The kongerunde — the first under Kong Frederik, replacing the traditional "dronningerunde" — was expected to begin on March 25. DF's instruction to the King will be to appoint Troels Lund Poulsen as royal investigator, conditional on excluding Frederiksen and Løkke. If this condition is unmet, DF will recommend an alternative blue leader.

The most probable scenarios, ranked by expert assessment:

- **S + SF + M + RV government** (Engell's prediction), with EL and Alternativet as support parties — DF goes to opposition, potentially as the blue bloc's most vocal critic of both the government and Løkke's participation in it.
- **A blue minority government** (V + LA + K, possibly DD) requiring both DF and M as parallel external support parties — the narrow opening where Løkke is not a minister but his party's mandates still support the government. This is mathematically possible but politically fragile.
- **Extended negotiations and a "messy" government formation** (TV2's assessment: "Rodet regeringsdannelse venter"), with multiple rounds as parties test each other's red lines and discover which vetoes are real and which bend under pressure.

What is clear is that Messerschmidt has positioned DF not as a constructive coalition partner but as a **permanent opposition force to centrism** — the anti-Løkke pole in Danish politics. Whether this is a path to power or a formula for continued marginalization depends entirely on whether the Løkke veto holds, and whether the rest of blue bloc is willing to sacrifice Moderaterne's 14 seats to accommodate DF's 16. As of the morning of March 25, 2026, no one in Danish politics appeared ready to make that trade.
