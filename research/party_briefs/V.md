# Venstre's historic gamble: blue centre dream meets brutal arithmetic

Venstre emerged from the March 24, 2026 Danish general election with **18 seats and 10.1% of the vote** — the worst result in the party's 156-year history — yet leader Troels Lund Poulsen staked everything on a bold election-night ultimatum: a "blå midterregering" (blue centre government) or opposition, nothing else. This binary framing shut the door on rejoining a government with Socialdemokratiet and Mette Frederiksen, marking a decisive break from the cross-bloc SVM model that defined Danish politics from 2022 to 2026. The problem is that virtually every major commentator — Ask Rostrup, Hans Redder, Hans Engell, Pia Glud Munksgaard — considers the blue centre government scenario arithmetically and politically unworkable, given that Dansk Folkeparti's Morten Messerschmidt has sworn to "devour" Lars Løkke Rasmussen's Moderaterne if they enter government together. Venstre's actual trajectory appears to be opposition, while the most likely government formation is **S + SF + M + RV** — a configuration that excludes Venstre entirely.

## Three years inside SVM wore Venstre down to its bones

The SVM government (Socialdemokratiet–Venstre–Moderaterne), formed on **15 December 2022** after a record 42-day negotiation, was the first S-V coalition in over four decades. Venstre entered wounded — its 13.3% and 23 seats in the November 2022 election were already poor — and leader Jakob Ellemann-Jensen admitted the decision to make Frederiksen PM was a "løftebrud" (broken promise). The party received **7 of 23 ministerial posts** and secured genuine policy wins: a **10 billion DKK personal income tax reform** in December 2023 (the largest in a decade, halving the top tax rate bracket), a **10-year defence agreement** reaching NATO's 2% GDP target by 2030, and liberalisation initiatives in eldercare and municipal services.

But the costs were severe. The abolition of **Store Bededag** (Great Prayer Day) as a public holiday in 2024 generated the largest petition in Danish history and poisoned voter sentiment toward all three government parties. The **CO2 tax on agriculture** — the world's first, at 300 DKK per ton CO2-equivalent from 2030 — cut against Venstre's agrarian DNA. The party hemorrhaged voters in every direction: **14% to Konservative, 10% to Dansk Folkeparti, 9% to Liberal Alliance**, leaving Venstre retaining barely half of its 2022 voters by 2026 according to YouGov analysis.

The internal turbulence was extraordinary. Ellemann-Jensen was **sick-listed with stress** in February 2023, just two months into government, and Troels Lund Poulsen stepped in as acting Defence Minister. After Ellemann's return in August 2023, he shuffled portfolios but never recovered politically, ultimately **resigning on 23 October 2023** with the admission that "my person overshadows Venstre's results." Troels Lund Poulsen was elected party leader on **18 November 2023** at the Herning congress as the sole candidate. The most dramatic rupture came on **19 March 2024** when MP Mads Fuglede — immigration spokesperson and West Jutland representative — defected to Inger Støjberg's Danmarksdemokraterne, citing opposition to the SVM experiment and the CO2 agriculture tax. This cost the government its domestic majority and symbolised the tension between Venstre's liberal-centrist wing (comfortable governing with S) and its right-wing agrarian faction.

Despite this, Venstre **stayed in the SVM government until the election**. The gradual rightward recalibration under Poulsen was strategic, not schismatic: he defended the government's record on tax reform and defence while increasingly distancing Venstre from Socialdemokratiet's policy direction, particularly on pensions, foreign aid, and immigration. By November 2025, his party congress speech explicitly set Venstre apart from S's welfare expansion agenda.

## The 2026 campaign: defence hawk, wealth tax slayer, immigration hardliner

Troels Lund Poulsen declared himself **statsministerkandidat** (PM candidate) for the blue bloc on **26 February 2026**, the day the election was called in the wake of the Greenland crisis triggered by Trump's push to acquire Greenland. His campaign rested on four pillars.

**On taxation**, Venstre was the blue bloc's most vocal opponent of Socialdemokratiet's proposed **formueskat** (wealth tax on the wealthiest ~1% of Danes). Poulsen argued it would destroy liquidity and jobs: "Løsningen er ikke nye skatter, højere skatter eller skatter på dem, der skaber værdien for samfundet" ("The solution is not new taxes, higher taxes, or taxes on those who create value for society"). Venstre's counter-proposal was to **cut corporate tax from 22% to 20%** and reduce business regulatory burdens by 25% by 2030. All five blue bloc parties, plus Moderaterne and Radikale Venstre, opposed the wealth tax — and post-election, TV2 commentator Noa Redington declared it "stendød" (stone dead).

**On defence** — Poulsen's personal brand as outgoing Defence Minister — he proposed **doubling conscripts to 13,000 by 2035** (from the existing 6,500 agreement), cutting development aid below 0.7% of GNI, and accelerating military investments. This became a flashpoint in the TV2 statsministerduel, where Poulsen accused Frederiksen of "speaking untruth" about whether these numbers had been discussed in government. The think tank Kraka calculated the conscription proposal would reduce the private workforce by nearly 6,000 full-time equivalents.

**On immigration**, Poulsen announced in **December 2025** that Venstre now supported **withdrawal from the European Convention on Nationality** (statsborgerretskonventionen) — a significant shift. He acknowledged it was "not a step Venstre has previously committed to" but argued "the sum of challenges means we must now be willing to take the extra step." The platform also included stricter criminal deportation and a crackdown on "familieklan" criminality.

**On climate**, Venstre championed a market-oriented, technology-driven approach — "incitamenter, ikke forbud" (incentives, not bans) — and joined all five blue bloc parties in a **joint pledge to lift Denmark's ban on nuclear power**. Venstre was caught on the defensive, however, on the **drikkevand** (drinking water contamination) issue, with SF's Pia Olsen Dyhr accusing Poulsen of hypocrisy for opposing a blanket pesticide spraying ban after Venstre had blocked exactly this in SVM government negotiations.

## Election night: the two-option ultimatum that defined everything

The drama at Venstre's valgfest in Landstingssalen, Christiansborg, unfolded in three acts. When DR's exit poll hit at 20:00 showing just 9.3%, there was "stilhed og nedslåede blikke" (silence and dejected looks). By 22:00, improved forecasts showing 10.5% lifted spirits. The final result of 10.1% — still historically terrible — was greeted with palpable relief, especially because Venstre had held off Liberal Alliance (9.4%) for the title of **largest blue party**.

Around midnight, Poulsen took the stage and delivered what Kristeligt Dagblad called a "bombe" (bombshell). After acknowledging the result — "Jeg har gjort alt, hvad jeg kunne, jeg har givet alt, hvad jeg havde" ("I have done everything I could, I have given everything I had") — he pivoted to the mandate distribution: "Meget tyder på, at der ikke er et rent rødt flertal i Danmark. Så langt så godt. Det er en kæmpe sejr" ("Much suggests there is not a pure red majority in Denmark. So far so good. That is a huge victory").

Then came the critical passage, addressed directly to Lars Løkke Rasmussen: **"Vi skal prøve at lave en blå midterregering, som jeg foreslog i søndags. Muligheden er der, Lars"** ("We should try to form a blue centre government, as I suggested on Sunday. The opportunity is there, Lars"). He then presented his binary framing: **"Et: Vi får en blå midterregering. To: Vi går i opposition"** ("One: We get a blue centre government. Two: We go into opposition"). The crowd erupted, alternately cheering and chanting "Troels, han er for lækker" ("Troels, he's too cool").

This was a dramatic shift. Throughout the campaign, Poulsen had hedged — never fully closing the door on pointing to Frederiksen as PM. DR's Magten analysts noted that even his March 22 "blå midterregering" proposal "doesn't actually rule out governing with S." But the election-night speech essentially slammed that door. The reaction at Socialdemokratiet's party was immediate: jubilant chanting of "Danmark har brug for en socialdemokratisk regering."

## The blue centre government runs into a wall of contradictions

Venstre's preferred constellation — V + LA + KF + M, with DF and DD as potential partners — faces interlocking obstacles that virtually every major analyst considers insurmountable.

The **core arithmetic** is the first problem. V (18) + LA (16) + KF (13) = only **47 seats**. Adding Moderaterne (14) brings the total to 61 — still 29 short of a majority. You need DF (16) + DD (10) + Borgernes Parti (4) to reach **91 seats**, the barest possible majority. But this configuration requires parties that fundamentally refuse to coexist in the same political space.

**Morten Messerschmidt's veto on Løkke** is the critical blockage. DF's leader has been explicit: "Lars Løkke Rasmussen kommer ikke til på mine mandater at blive minister" ("Lars Løkke Rasmussen will not become minister on my mandates") and promised to "æde ham og hans folk hver eneste dag" ("devour him and his people every single day"). Borgernes Parti's Lars Boje Mathiesen holds a similar position. Meanwhile, Løkke himself refuses to participate in a government "dependent on extremist flanks" — meaning DF and Borgernes Parti. As TV2's Hans Redder put it: "Det, Troels Lund Poulsen forestiller sig, vil kræve, at Lars Boje Mathiesen og Borgernes Parti kan arbejde sammen med Lars Løkke Rasmussen. Og Morten Messerschmidt, hvis erklærede mål er at ødelægge Lars Løkke Rasmussen" ("What Troels Lund Poulsen imagines would require Lars Boje Mathiesen and Borgernes Parti to cooperate with Lars Løkke Rasmussen. And Morten Messerschmidt, whose declared goal is to destroy Lars Løkke Rasmussen").

DR's Magtens Morgenbrief was equally blunt: "Lavpraktisk taler han om en regering med Venstre, De Konservative, Liberal Alliance og Moderaterne. Tillykke med det — bortset fra, at de partier overhovedet ikke har flertal" ("Practically speaking, he's talking about a government with Venstre, Conservatives, Liberal Alliance, and Moderaterne. Congratulations with that — except those parties don't have a majority at all").

Venstre also issued a **soft veto on Radikale Venstre**, with Poulsen citing their abandonment of an agreed coalition deal in 2022 and their softer immigration stance. No blue-bloc party was explicitly vetoed, but the internal contradictions within the blue bloc make any comprehensive configuration unworkable.

## Commentators see opposition as Venstre's most likely destination

The expert consensus is striking in its unanimity. **Ask Rostrup** (TV2 political analyst) and **Hans Redder** (TV2 political editor) both judged the blue centre government scenario unrealistic. Veteran commentator **Hans Engell** — a former Conservative leader — predicted the outcome will be a government of **S + SF + M + RV**, with Enhedslisten and Alternativet as support parties, completely excluding Venstre.

**DR's Pia Glud Munksgaard** characterised Poulsen's pre-election "blå midterregering" proposal as "panik før lukketid" (panic before closing time) — a desperate move driven by polls showing Venstre at a catastrophic 8.6% just days before the vote. The concept emerged only on **March 22**, two days before election day, reinforcing the perception of improvisation rather than strategy.

**NordiskPost's** post-election analysis captured Venstre's paradox precisely: "The party can claim symbolic primacy on the right, but it has done so from a much weaker electoral base than before. In practical terms, its room for manoeuvre is narrower, not wider." Former Venstre tax minister **Karsten Lauritzen** — now outside parliament — shifted the frame entirely, identifying "de helt store poler i dansk politik fremadrettet: Det er Lars Løkke Rasmussen mod Messerschmidt" ("the major poles in Danish politics going forward: It's Lars Løkke Rasmussen vs. Messerschmidt") — a formulation that marginalises Venstre from the central axis of power.

Former Enhedslisten leader **Pernille Skipper**, now a TV2 commentator, offered the most nuanced assessment: "Det er for tidligt at udråbe valgets taber... Det er stadig så åbent" ("It is too early to declare the election's loser... It is still so open"). She noted that the Hans Engell scenario requires Løkke to "swallow the camel" of governing on far-left mandates — something he explicitly pledged not to do. This suggests protracted negotiations with no easy resolution.

## The V-LA rivalry, the Løkke problem, and unresolved contradictions

The competition with **Liberal Alliance** for blue bloc primacy was one of the election's subplots. When the exit poll briefly showed LA ahead of V (10.5% to 9.3%), DR's Jens Ringberg called it a potential "skifte i det borgerlige Danmark" (shift in conservative Denmark). The final result restored Venstre's slim lead (18 vs 16 seats), and Alex Vanopslagh deferred to Poulsen as negotiation leader. But the margin is razor-thin, and **three blue parties now cluster at 16-18 seats each** (V, LA, DF), creating an unprecedented fragmentation of the right. Poulsen's claim to the blue PM candidacy rests on a "mulehår" (whisker), as DD's Inger Støjberg noted.

On the kongerunde, the situation as of March 25 was what DR's Christine Cordsen called "meget usædvanligt" — it was entirely unclear who would be named kongelig undersøger. DF, KF, DD, and eventually LA pointed to Poulsen. Moderaterne pointed to Løkke himself. Red bloc parties pointed to Frederiksen. According to subsequent reporting, **Mette Frederiksen was ultimately chosen as kongelig undersøger**, with government formation negotiations beginning March 27 — confirming that Venstre's blue centre government scenario failed to gain sufficient mandate support.

Several **contradictions** in Venstre's position deserve flagging. First, Poulsen ran for a blue PM while most analysts — and his own party's polls — showed this as unrealistic; DR's Munksgaard noted he himself had acknowledged a year earlier that "Venstre ligger omkring 10 procent." Second, the "blå midterregering" emerged only 48 hours before the vote, after catastrophic polling, yet was presented as a principled strategic position. Third, Venstre attacked Frederiksen on the drikkevand pesticide issue despite having blocked the same protective measures inside the SVM government. Fourth, Poulsen throughout the campaign maintained a "helgardering" (hedge) on governing with S, then dramatically abandoned it on election night — a shift that read as responsive to the crowd's energy rather than a pre-planned strategic commitment.

## Where Venstre stands as negotiations begin

Venstre's negotiating position is structurally weak but not irrelevant. The party holds **18 seats** in a fragmented parliament where 12 parties share 175 Danish mandates. Its leverage depends entirely on whether Frederiksen's negotiation with M, SF, and RV encounters obstacles — particularly Løkke's stated reluctance to govern on far-left mandates, and Enhedslisten's demand that Frederiksen guarantee a red government before receiving their support. If those negotiations stall, a broader constellation could re-emerge where Venstre's seats become relevant.

Poulsen's personal position appears secure for now — he told journalists on election morning that this would not be his last election as formand, and the party rallied around him on election night. But the historical trajectory is sobering: Venstre has fallen from **34.2% in 2001** (its modern peak under Anders Fogh Rasmussen) to 10.1% in 24 years, while its former voters have scattered across LA, KF, DF, DD, and Borgernes Parti. The fragmentation of the Danish right into six competing parties, each with 4-18 seats, has made the classic Venstre-led government model nearly impossible to replicate.

The most likely immediate outcome is a **centre-left government of S + SF + M + RV** that sends Venstre to opposition. From there, Poulsen faces a familiar Danish opposition leader's challenge: rebuilding from a historic low while competitors on every flank claim overlapping pieces of Venstre's traditional electorate. His election-night ultimatum may have been politically necessary to hold the party together, but it also narrowed his future options in ways that the coming months of opposition may test severely.
