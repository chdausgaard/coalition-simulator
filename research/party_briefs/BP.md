# Borgernes Parti: Denmark's wildcard four seats that could reshape government formation

**Borgernes Parti (BP), the one-man insurgency led by Lars Boje Mathiesen, barely cleared Denmark's 2% electoral threshold on March 24, 2026 — yet its four seats may determine whether Denmark gets a blue or red government.** The arithmetic is stark: blue bloc plus Moderaterne equals exactly 91 seats (one more than needed), but remove BP's four mandates and that coalition falls to 87 — dead on arrival. This gives a protest party that most blue leaders openly disdain an outsized veto over the entire government formation process. Mathiesen, who spent election night declaring he "doesn't owe anyone anything" and pointing to himself as prime minister, appears intent on extracting maximum leverage from a position most commentators thought he would never reach.

---

## A party born from expulsion and grievance

Borgernes Parti did not emerge from ideological ferment or grassroots organizing. It emerged from a firing. In February 2023, Lars Boje Mathiesen became leader of Nye Borgerlige (New Right) after founder Pernille Vermund stepped down. Within one month — on March 9, 2023 — the party's national executive board unanimously expelled him. The trigger: Mathiesen had demanded a **four-year irrevocable contract at 55,000 DKK/month** plus a **350,000 DKK lump sum** transferred to his personal account for a "Boje på Borgen" branding campaign. Internal documents later revealed he had also charged a tuxedo, underwear, a MacBook, and hardware-store purchases totaling around 36,000 DKK to the party. Nye Borgerlige co-founder Peter Seier Christensen called it "the most grotesque situation I can recall in Danish politics."

Mathiesen sat as an independent MP for over a year before announcing in April 2024 that he would found a new "power-critical" party. His first proposed name — "Danmarks Bedste Parti" (Denmark's Best Party) — was rejected by the Election Board as self-promotional on the ballot. "Borgernes Parti" alone was rejected due to similarity with the existing Borgernes Folkeparti. The final approved name: **Borgernes Parti – Lars Boje Mathiesen**, assigned party letter **H**, formally approved on January 15, 2025. The party spent approximately **885,000 DKK on social media advertising** to collect the required 20,195 voter declarations, representing roughly 10% of all party political advertising on Facebook and Instagram nationally. The funding sources remain undisclosed.

By November 2025, BP had approximately 2,000 members and its first prominent candidate — millionaire Allan Feldt — had already withdrawn, citing poor polling and suggesting BP merge with Dansk Folkeparti. In December 2025, Mathiesen declared himself a candidate for prime minister. No other party supported this candidacy.

## Ideological DNA: libertarianism meets anti-Islam nationalism

BP officially rejects ideological labels. Its website states: "Vi er ikke bygget op omkring en ideologi, men er opstået på baggrund af tusindvis af samtaler med danskere" (We are not built around an ideology, but emerged from thousands of conversations with Danes). The party claims to operate on two principles: "Does it make sense?" and "Is it common sense?"

In practice, BP occupies a distinctive ideological space that fuses four strands:

**Economic libertarianism.** Lower income taxes, removal of VAT on food, abolition of vehicle registration tax entirely, reduction of fuel and electricity duties to EU minimums, opposition to wealth taxes, and aggressive deregulation ("for every new law, three others should be removed"). The party explicitly targets motorists as a constituency, branding itself "Denmark's best party for car owners."

**Anti-Islam nationalism.** The party's second pillar is literally titled "Befri Danmark fra Islam" (Free Denmark from Islam). Proposals include a total stop on asylum and immigration from MENAPT countries (Middle East, North Africa, Pakistan, Turkey), expulsion of criminal foreigners after first conviction, "remigration," and the stripping of Danish citizenship from those who committed serious crimes. Most provocatively, in November 2025 Mathiesen proposed a **monthly levy of 4,000–5,000 DKK per adult and 2,000 DKK per child** for citizens from MENA countries to remain on Danish soil. Investigative outlet Frihedsbrevet documented that this idea originated from radical far-right movements influencing BP behind the scenes.

**Climate skepticism.** BP's third pillar is "Et opgør med klimahysteriet" (A showdown with climate hysteria). The party insists Denmark faces an energy crisis, not a climate crisis. It supports nuclear power, opposes heavy wind and solar subsidies, defends North Sea oil and gas extraction, and wants to remove CO2 taxes on agriculture.

**Anti-establishment populism.** Constitutional court with initiative power to investigate government lawbreaking, digital referendums, term limits for politicians, abolition of public party funding, dismantling of public broadcaster DR, and voluntary foreign aid. Mathiesen boycotted all mainstream media during the 2026 campaign, running it almost entirely through Facebook.

### Where BP sits relative to its neighbors

Compared to **Liberal Alliance**, BP shares the economic liberalism but goes far further on immigration, rejects "climate alarmism" more aggressively, and substitutes LA's technocratic policy approach with populist rhetoric. TV 2 reported spending four days trying to get BP to provide financial details for its proposals and being stonewalled — a sharp contrast with LA's costed platforms.

Compared to **Dansk Folkeparti**, BP shares the anti-immigration stance but is far more economically liberal. DF is a welfare-state protector; BP wants to shrink the state. When DF proposed a merger, Mathiesen dismissed it: "Nej, vi er ikke socialdemokrater i Borgernes Parti."

Compared to **Danmarksdemokraterne**, BP is more economically libertarian and arguably more extreme on anti-Islam rhetoric. The key structural difference: DD participated in Borgerligt Konvent (the coordinating platform for blue-bloc opposition parties); BP was excluded or chose not to participate.

BP essentially occupies the intersection where Nye Borgerlige used to sit — its direct ideological ancestor — combining elements that no single other party bundles together. Lex.dk classifies it as "to the right on both the distributive and values-political dimensions." English Wikipedia labels it "far-right populist."

## The 2026 campaign: social media insurgency meets policy opacity

BP's 2026 campaign was unlike any other party's. Mathiesen's media boycott meant the campaign was conducted almost entirely on Facebook, bypassing traditional journalistic scrutiny. The party's platform centered on its three pillars — lower taxes, anti-Islam immigration policy, and climate skepticism — alongside populist governance reforms.

Key policy specifics included raising the personal tax allowance to give each parent 1,000 DKK more per month after tax, abolishing vehicle registration tax, making development aid voluntary, making ATP pension membership optional, stopping state rewilding projects (called "animal abuse disguised as nature conservation"), and mandatory digital pre-nuptial agreements. The party was EU-skeptic but strongly pro-NATO, advocating meeting a **5% of GDP defense spending target** and closer Nordic military cooperation.

The result on election night: **2.1% of the vote, yielding 4 seats** — the party's first election ever and a razor-thin margin above the 2.0% threshold. This was historically notable: it marked the first time since the threshold was introduced in 1953 that all 12 parties on the ballot cleared it. Polls had swung wildly — from 1.9% in January 2025 to 3.3% in mid-March 2026 to a final 2.1%. BP was the smallest party in the new Folketing.

## Post-election signals reveal a party maximizing leverage

The most informative data comes from election night and the morning after — the period when campaign rhetoric gives way to negotiating positions.

**Election night, Christiansborg.** Mathiesen arrived last among all party leaders. His first statement to TV 2 set the tone: **"Jeg skylder ikke nogen noget som helst"** (I don't owe anyone anything). His victory speech at 00:28 on March 25 was notably emotional and personal: "Det er en sejr til alle dem derude, der bliver mobbet. Alle dem, der får at vide, at de ikke er gode nok" (This is a victory for all those out there who are bullied, all those who are told they're not good enough). He dedicated it to children and young people who feel excluded: "I skal aldrig lade jer tryne. I skal aldrig nogensinde give op" (Never let them push you around. Never ever give up).

The combative edge surfaced immediately after. Mathiesen expressed open bitterness toward the blue bloc: **"Jeg har ikke sagt et eneste dårligt ord om dem overhovedet. De har bevidst valgt at ignorere mig som en eller anden dårlig udgave af 'Paradise Hotel'. Jeg skylder ikke nogen noget som helst."** (I haven't said a single bad word about them. They deliberately chose to ignore me like some bad version of 'Paradise Hotel'. I don't owe anyone anything.) He was the only party leader still at his party at 01:45, surrounded by supporters seeking selfies and hugs.

**The kongerunde stance.** Mathiesen refused to name a kongelig undersøger (royal investigator). Instead, he pointed to himself as statsminister — a maximalist negotiating position that commits to nothing while preserving all options. When pressed pre-election, he deflected: "Det finder vi først ud af den 24. marts."

**Day-after debate, March 25.** At the Publicistklubben debate hosted by Martin Krasnik, Mathiesen delivered a pointed message to Lars Løkke Rasmussen: **"Vi går kun en gang til buffeten"** (We only go to the buffet once) — signaling that BP will demand its full price upfront with no second chances. He also struck a statesmanlike note: "Pas nu på det her folkestyre. Folk har ikke så stor tillid til os" (Take care of this democracy. People don't have that much trust in us).

A crucial detail: Mathiesen does not define his party as blue-bloc. He calls it a **"midterparti"** (center party), even though media and bloc arithmetic count it firmly in the blue column. This rhetorical positioning preserves ambiguity and negotiating space.

## The arithmetic that makes four seats matter enormously

The mandate distribution creates a situation where BP punches far above its weight:

- **Red bloc** (S, SF, EL, ALT, RV): **84 seats** — 6 short of majority
- **Blue bloc** (V, LA, KF, DD, DF, BP): **77 seats** — 13 short
- **Moderaterne** (M): **14 seats** — the kingmaker
- **Blue + M = 91** — bare majority, but requires ALL blue parties including BP
- **Blue minus BP + M = 87** — no majority
- **Red + M = 98** — comfortable majority without needing BP

This arithmetic means BP's four seats are **necessary for any blue-leaning government configuration to reach 90 mandates**. Remove them and the math collapses. However, the leverage is asymmetric: BP can only play within a blue-leaning scenario that already depends on Moderaterne choosing the blue side. If Løkke chooses red, BP's seats become irrelevant.

TV 2's political editor Hans Redder captured the tension on election night: a blue center government "would require Lars Boje Mathiesen and Borgernes Parti to cooperate with Lars Løkke Rasmussen — and Morten Messerschmidt, whose stated goal is to destroy Lars Løkke Rasmussen and Moderaterne this parliamentary term."

Under Denmark's system of negative parliamentarism, BP would not need to actively support a government — it would merely need to not vote against it. Mathiesen would be unlikely to bring down a blue-leaning government since that would empower Mette Frederiksen, whom he has called for impeaching. But this creates a "free rider" dynamic where he can extract concessions without bearing government responsibility.

The most likely government scenario, according to veteran commentator **Hans Engell**, is an **S + SF + M + RV coalition** — in which BP plays no role whatsoever. Moderaterne's Jakob Engel-Schmidt stated on March 25: "Der er ikke nogen partier i det danske Folketing, som Moderaterne ikke vil samarbejde med" — but Løkke's stated principle of not relying on "den yderste venstrefløj eller den yderste højrefløj" creates clear tension with depending on BP votes.

## How experts and mainstream parties view the outsider

The consensus among Danish political commentators is that BP is a legitimate democratic actor but a deeply difficult partner — and widely considered to be on the far right.

**The "impossible to cooperate with" reputation.** TV 2 summarized it bluntly: "Lars Boje Mathiesen bliver ofte beskrevet som en dygtig kommunikatør. Men også som en politiker, der er noget nær umulig at samarbejde med" (Often described as a skilled communicator, but also as a politician who is nearly impossible to cooperate with). Blue parties privately wished BP hadn't cleared the threshold. TV 2 reported: "Når mikrofonerne er slukket, er det helt tydeligt, at partierne i blå blok håber, at Borgernes Parti ikke kommer over spærregrænsen."

**Explicit exclusions.** Troels Lund Poulsen (Venstre) stated on March 2: "At invitere Lars Boje i regering kommer ikke til at ske" (Inviting Lars Boje into government is not going to happen). Alex Vanopslagh (LA) said he did not think Mathiesen would make a good prime minister and that on some areas he would prefer Mette Frederiksen. Inger Støjberg (DD) and Mona Juul (KF) both said they would need to "draw a breath" before saying they could see Mathiesen as a government colleague.

**The extremism question.** DR researcher Kasper Grotle Rasmussen (SDU) found BP is **"konspirationsteoretikernes yndlingspolitiker"** (conspiracy theorists' favorite politician), with the party actively engaging in Facebook groups associated with deep state, Great Replacement, anti-WEF, and chemtrails theories. Information reported BP collaborated with Kent Ivan Nielsen, a controversial activist who was former leader of Stram Kurs (classified as far-right extremist), had convictions for weapons violations and harassment of a government minister, and was linked to a Russian influence campaign against Denmark. Frihedsbrevet documented that radical far-right movements influenced BP's policy positions behind the scenes.

These associations go beyond normal right-wing politics in the Danish context. However, BP's economic platform — lower taxes, less regulation, smaller government — is entirely mainstream in Danish bourgeois politics. The party occupies a strange dual position: conventional on economics, extreme on immigration and political style, and linked to conspiratorial networks in ways that distinguish it from even Dansk Folkeparti or Danmarksdemokraterne.

## Lars Boje Mathiesen: the bourgeois energy drink

Mathiesen's profile explains much about BP's character. Born May 21, 1975, in Skive, he grew up on a disused farm near Ribe in what he describes as a hippie household — his father was a musician with cannabis plants on the property, his parents drank, and he has spoken publicly about a difficult childhood where he "didn't always dare invite friends home." He studied in the United States at Southeast Missouri State University in 1999, an experience he credits with transforming his views on personal freedom. He trained as a primary school teacher, working variously as a teacher, welder, freelance musician, and hotel/café operator.

His political trajectory reveals a pattern of joining organizations, rising fast, and departing acrimoniously. He joined Liberal Alliance in 2013, won a seat on Aarhus City Council, but left in 2016 when LA wouldn't nominate him for the Folketing. He joined Nye Borgerlige immediately, was elected to parliament in 2019, became party leader in February 2023, and was expelled in March 2023 — **a leadership tenure of roughly one month**.

JydskeVestkysten called him **"en borgerlig energidrik"** (a bourgeois energy drink). Information described him in 2020 as a "viral sensation" and one of Denmark's most effective Facebook communicators. Atlas Magasin compared him to a "kandestøber" — a reference to Holberg's comedy about a political amateur in over his head. His self-assessment is characteristically defiant: "Så må du hjertens gerne kalde mig populist" (Then you're very welcome to call me populist), he told Zetland, adding: "Jeg har hørt mere sandhed på en bodega fredag eftermiddag med en kølig og en sidevogn, end jeg har hørt på Christiansborg" (I've heard more truth at a pub on a Friday afternoon with a cold beer and a chaser than I've heard at Christiansborg).

His communication strategy is genuinely formidable. He built a party from scratch, collected 20,000+ voter declarations through social media alone, boycotted all mainstream media during the campaign, and still crossed the threshold. But the same combativeness that makes him effective on Facebook makes him toxic as a coalition partner. His track record of institutional relationships — LA, Nye Borgerlige, and now his cold relationship with the entire blue bloc — suggests a figure who thrives in opposition and outsider status but struggles with the compromises of collaboration.

## Conclusion: a veto player with nowhere to go

The central paradox of Borgernes Parti's position is that its four seats are arithmetically essential for the only scenario in which they matter — a blue center government — but the party's reputation, its leader's relationships, and Moderaterne's stated red lines all work against that scenario materializing.

Three novel insights emerge from the post-election signals. First, Mathiesen's refusal to name a kongelig undersøger and his self-nomination as statsminister is not mere vanity — it is a **rational maximalist negotiating strategy** that keeps all doors open while committing to nothing. Second, his description of BP as a "midterparti" rather than a blue-bloc party is a deliberate signal that he considers himself unbound by bloc discipline — a warning to blue leaders that his four votes cannot be taken for granted. Third, the "buffet" metaphor directed at Løkke ("we only go once") reveals a transactional mindset: BP will price its support high, and Mathiesen expects payment upfront.

The most likely outcome is that BP's leverage proves theoretical. If Løkke steers toward a center-left coalition (S+SF+M+RV, totaling 98 seats), BP's four mandates become irrelevant to government formation entirely. Mathiesen would then spend the parliamentary term as a vocal, social-media-driven opposition figure — a role that suits his temperament and his party's DNA far more than the quiet compromises of supporting a government. The party's long-term viability, hovering at 2.1% with a one-man brand and no institutional infrastructure, remains deeply precarious.
