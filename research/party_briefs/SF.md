# SF's path back to power after Denmark's fractured 2026 election

SF — Socialistisk Folkeparti — emerged from the 24 March 2026 Danish general election as **the second-largest party in the Folketing** with 20 seats and 11.6% of the vote, its best result in modern history. The election produced a hung parliament with no viable bloc majority: the red bloc holds 84 seats, the blue bloc 77, and Lars Løkke Rasmussen's Moderaterne commands 14 kingmaker seats. **Pia Olsen Dyhr has staked SF's future on a binary ultimatum — government or opposition, nothing in between** — while simultaneously opening the door to the very centrist party (Moderaterne) that many in her base despise. This tension defines everything about SF's negotiating position. Mette Frederiksen was named kongelig undersøger on 25 March and began formal negotiations at Marienborg on 27 March, with the emerging consensus pointing toward an S+SF+M+RV four-party government — a coalition that would require everyone to abandon at least one campaign promise.

---

## Twelve years of rebuilding transformed SF into a governing force

SF's trajectory from 2022 to 2026 is a story of disciplined opposition converted into electoral momentum. When Mette Frederiksen formed the cross-bloc SVM government (S+V+M) in December 2022, SF walked away from the formation talks, refusing to accept the abolition of Store Bededag, regressive tax cuts, and weakened drinking-water protections. That decision — staying in opposition rather than swallowing unacceptable terms — became the foundational narrative of Pia Olsen Dyhr's leadership.

The SVM government proved deeply unpopular almost immediately. Its first major act — abolishing the centuries-old Store Bededag public holiday — triggered what SF's Karsten Hønge called **"bededagsrøveriet"** (the prayer-day robbery), shouting "Stop tyven!" from the Folketing podium during the February 2023 vote. Dyhr weaponized the issue relentlessly, calling the abolition "magtfuldkomment" (despotic) and demanding reinstatement as "the first act of a new red government."

SF's growth fed on voter migration from the Social Democrats. Megafon analysis before the 2026 election found that **32% of intended SF voters had supported Socialdemokratiet in 2022**, and 15% of S's 2022 electorate had migrated to SF. The electoral cascade was striking: SF became Denmark's largest party in the June 2024 European Parliament election (17.4%, three MEPs), peaked at 18.8% in a June 2024 Voxmeter poll, won the Copenhagen overborgmester post in November 2025's municipal elections — ending over a century of Social Democratic control — and then secured 20 parliamentary seats in March 2026.

Under Dyhr, SF's ideological profile shifted on several fronts. The party **abandoned its historically pacifist defence stance**, co-signing the March 2022 National Compromise and the 10-year Defence Agreement 2024–2033 (approximately DKK 143 billion), fully committing to NATO's 2% GDP target. It moved toward a more centrist immigration posture while maintaining its core green and welfare identity. In March 2022, the party congress rebranded SF's English name to "Green Left," with Dyhr explaining the old literal translation was "reminiscent of the communist parties in Russia and China. And we have no interest in that."

## SF's 2026 campaign built around welfare, climate, and inequality

SF entered the 2026 campaign — called on 26 February with voting on 24 March — with a dense policy platform spanning approximately 50 proposals with financial consequences.

**The wealth tax (formueskat)** was the campaign's signature economic proposal: a **0.5% annual tax on net wealth exceeding DKK 10 million** per person (DKK 20 million for couples), projected to generate **DKK 9.3 billion annually**. This was significantly more ambitious than S's proposal of the same rate on wealth above DKK 25 million (yielding roughly DKK 6.5 billion). SF intended the revenue to finance expanded parental leave and welfare investments. Notably, the DKK 10 million threshold actually represented a softening from SF's 2019 proposal, which targeted fortunes above DKK 5 million — Radio4 reported on this shift on 10 March 2026. When pressed on whether the wealth tax was an ultimatum, Dyhr explicitly told Information: **"Det er et af vores krav. Men nej, det er ikke ultimativt. For jeg tror ikke på ultimative krav"** ("It's one of our demands. But no, it's not an ultimatum. I don't believe in ultimatums").

**On climate**, SF positioned as Denmark's green party, demanding climate neutrality by 2040, a 90% CO₂ reduction by 2035 (versus the SVM government's proposed 82%), a total ban on pesticides in vulnerable groundwater areas, and a binding Nature and Biodiversity Law requiring 30% protected nature on land and sea with 10% strictly protected. This nature law became a **joint red bloc demand** — SF, Enhedslisten, Radikale Venstre, and Alternativet announced it together on 25 March, the day after the election.

**Education became SF's flagship welfare proposal**: "Nu er det folkeskolens tur" (Now it's the school's turn) included DKK 6.2 billion annually plus a DKK 15 billion one-time transformation fund, a class-size cap of 24 students, and a push for more two-teacher classrooms. Elder care received a proposed DKK 3 billion quality uplift, and child poverty a "Mulighedsreform" targeting Denmark's approximately 50,000 children in poverty.

**On immigration**, SF carved a distinctive niche: more humane than S (resume quota refugees, better conditions at departure centres) while maintaining firmness on parallel societies, negative social control, and criminal foreigners. On defence, SF endorsed the government's 2025 decision to raise spending to 3.5% of GDP by 2030, while advocating a broader security concept encompassing cyber defence, climate security, and Arctic low-tension preservation.

## The "aldrig igen" rhetoric targets Venstre and Konservative, not Moderaterne

The user's question about SF's "aldrig igen" (never again) stance on Moderaterne reveals a critical finding that inverts the expected narrative. **SF's absolute veto applies to Venstre and De Konservative — not to Moderaterne.** Pia Olsen Dyhr has been actively opening the door to governing with Lars Løkke Rasmussen since early 2024.

The timeline is instructive. In a February 2024 Politiken interview, Dyhr first indicated willingness to consider an S-SF-M government, saying "Jeg vil ikke udelukke noget på forhånd" ("I won't exclude anything in advance"). By March 2024, she told Altinget: **"Du tænker på Løkke som i gamle dage. Løkke er faktisk blevet en anden som formand for Moderaterne"** ("You're thinking of Løkke as he was in the old days. Løkke has actually become a different person as leader of Moderaterne"). She pointed to policy overlap on self-governance (selvforvaltning), Løkke's position on Gaza, and even his top-top tax — noting that "when SF proposed the millionærskat, we were laughed at, but that's exactly what Løkke introduced."

By February 2025, she told Kristeligt Dagblad unambiguously: **"SF kommer ikke i regering med Venstre eller De Konservative. Men vi er klar til at indgå i en rød regering... Det kan sagtens være med Moderaterne."** At SF's landsmøde on 21 March 2026, three days before the election, she confirmed: "Politik er sådan, at så længe, at ingen partier har et flertal selv, så skal man samarbejde. Vi har sagt, at vi sådan set godt vil se på at lave et samarbejde med Moderaterne eller Radikale Venstre, som for mig at se er to midterpartier af samme type."

The "aldrig igen" language, when it appears, refers specifically to economic policy — not to Moderaterne as a party. At the landsmøde, Dyhr declared: **"Vi kommer aldrig — aldrig — igen til at sige ja til at føre borgerlig økonomisk politik"** ("We will never — never — again agree to pursue bourgeois economic policy"). This is a reference to the traumatic 2011–2014 government, when SF governed under conditions largely dictated by Radikale Venstre's Margrethe Vestager, ultimately leaving the coalition in disgrace.

However, **significant grassroots resistance exists within SF**. DR surveyed local party chairs and found deep scepticism. Jan Kjældgaard, SF Furesø chairman (46 years in the party), said: "Det er helt usandsynligt, at vi får så meget politik igennem, at vi kan leve med Moderaterne... Lars Løkke Rasmussen er ikke til at stole på over en dørtærskel." Other local leaders called Løkke "en centrum-højre-mand" whose politics are not "specielt spiselig for SF." Dyhr herself acknowledged the tension with a characteristically pragmatic framing: "Jeg er god til at tælle alle mine fingre, inden jeg går ind i et forhandlingslokale med Lars Løkke Rasmussen."

Intriguingly, Dyhr has at times signalled that **Moderaterne may be more compatible than Radikale Venstre**: "Hvis man ser på Radikales økonomiske politik, synes jeg nærmest, de er mere højreorienterede end Moderaterne." This also serves a strategic function — pressuring RV by reminding them they are not the only possible centrist coalition partner.

## Election night and kongerunde crystallised SF's negotiating stance

On the evening of 24 March, Pia Olsen Dyhr described the exit poll as "en smule bøvlet" (a bit messy) and delivered her election-night speech at Johan Borups Højskole with a clear framing of the three possible outcomes: "Vi kan få en blå regering lige om lidt. Vi kan også få en magenta-farvet, men venner — vi kan også få en rød regering om lidt." The crowd booed at the mention of a blue government.

Her most consequential statement was the binary ultimatum: **"Enten er SF i regering, eller også er vi i opposition."** This was not new — she had been saying it throughout the campaign — but its repetition on election night, with the actual mandate numbers known, elevated it from campaign rhetoric to a binding commitment. SF will not accept a støtteparti role. It demands cabinet seats.

At the kongerunde on 25 March — the first under King Frederik X — Dyhr pointed "unequivocally" to Mette Frederiksen as formateur, "but with the demand to form a multi-party government." Crucially, she did not exclude Lars Løkke Rasmussen from a potential government, while explicitly ruling out Venstre and Konservative. She also did not seek the statsminister post for herself; SF's political spokesperson Signe Munk had earlier told DR: "Jeg har svært ved at se for mig, at det ender der, hvor det er SF, der skal sidde for bordenden som statsministerparti." This despite Enhedslisten's Pelle Dragsted having publicly floated Dyhr as PM: "Set fra Enhedslistens synspunkt, så vil jeg da hellere have en statsminister fra SF end fra Socialdemokratiet."

On election day morning, voting at Skolen i Sydhavnen, Dyhr delivered perhaps her most revealing line: **"Jeg drømmer ikke om en ministerbil, men politikken bliver bedre med SF i regeringen. Mette Frederiksen er nødt til at vælge mellem Troels og mig"** — framing the choice as binary: Troels Lund Poulsen (Venstre) or her.

## Commentators see SF as essential but predict painful compromises

The expert consensus converges on an **S+SF+M+RV four-party government**, with Enhedslisten and Alternativet as external support parties. Veteran political commentator Hans Engell stated on election night: "Jeg tror, det ender med, at vi får en regering bestående af Socialdemokratiet, SF, Løkke og de radikale. Enhedslisten og Alternativet bliver de to støttepartier." NordiskPost's 25 March analysis described this as "the clearest viable formula inside the new Folketing."

But this formula contains deep structural tensions. Former Enhedslisten leader Pernille Skipper, now a TV2 commentator, identified the core problem: "Hvis man sammenholder aftenens resultater med alt, hvad partierne har sagt, så er der bare ikke 90 mandater bag noget som helst." Løkke has repeatedly stated he will not sit in a government dependent on Enhedslisten's mandates — yet the S+SF+M+RV constellation needs precisely that external support to reach 90 seats. Political scientist **Rune Stubager** described the overall dynamic as "an extended version of the game of chicken."

The wealth tax — SF's flagship economic proposal — appears to be the first casualty. TV2 commentator **Noa Redington declared the formueskat "stendød" (stone dead)** on election night, noting opposition not only from the blue bloc but also from Moderaterne and Radikale Venstre. Since Dyhr herself said the wealth tax is "not an ultimatum," this concession appears pre-baked into SF's negotiating strategy.

DR's election-night analysis was blunt: the arithmetic is **"faktisk næsten umuligt"** (actually almost impossible) if all party leaders hold to their campaign positions. "Nogen skal give sig og opgive valgkampens ultimatummer" ("Someone has to give up the campaign's ultimatums"). Information's analysis identified SF's core paradox: Dyhr has "spent 12 long years rebuilding SF from the ruins" and is "desperate to return to government but terrified of repeating the 2011 mistake of governing on someone else's terms."

SF's leverage is real but bounded. It is **indispensable to any left-leaning government** — no viable red-tinted coalition exists without SF's 20 seats. Its best-ever electoral result gives it a strong democratic mandate. But 84 red-bloc seats still fall short of 90, meaning SF cannot dictate terms; it must accommodate Moderaterne, whose centrist economic agenda conflicts directly with SF's welfare-first, progressive-taxation platform.

## The coalition arithmetic forces SF through a narrow needle

A pure S+SF government commands only approximately 58 seats — far from a majority. Dyhr has acknowledged this reality: "Jeg ser ikke, at vi kan få en regering kun bestående af S og SF. Jeg ville ønske det, men det er jeg for realistisk til." Even the full red bloc (S 38 + SF 20 + EL 11 + RV 10 + ALT 5 = 84) cannot govern alone. Moderaterne's 14 seats are mathematically essential.

SF's relationships with its potential partners reveal a complex web. With **Enhedslisten**, cooperation is deep — joint post-election demands on nature policy, ideological sympathy, and Dragsted's public preference for Dyhr as PM. With **Radikale Venstre**, there is overlap on climate but suspicion of RV's blue-bloc flirtation; Dyhr has warned that "Det Radikale Venstre leger med blå blok." With **Alternativet**, SF coordinates within the red-bloc framework but treats it as a minor partner.

The relationship with **Socialdemokratiet** is the most consequential and the most fraught. SF's entire growth story is built on absorbing disaffected S voters. At the landsmøde, Dyhr directly attacked Frederiksen: "Socialdemokratiets kampagne har været fyldt med politik, der kun kan blive til virkelighed i samarbejde med os. Og alligevel holdes alle døre, kattelemme, sprækker og smuthuller åbne for at kunne fortsætte med højrefløjen. Stop det nu." SF took Copenhagen's mayoralty from S. S recorded its worst result since 1903. The power dynamics within any S-SF-M-RV government would reflect this shift, with SF demanding — and likely receiving — significant ministerial representation. Dyhr has openly aspired to the Finance Ministry (finansminister), which would give SF direct control over fiscal policy.

Moderaterne's Jakob Engel-Schmidt framed the situation from the other side: "Jeg ser en masse små partier, der har fået middelstor opbakning fra danskerne, i et meget fragmenteret Folketing, hvor der kun er ét mellemstort parti tilbage. Det kalder på et samarbejde, der er bredere end normalt." Løkke's strategy, per Information's analysis, is transparent: "At presse næste regering ind i et midterkompromis" — to press the next government into a centrist compromise.

## What SF will likely sacrifice, and what it will fight to keep

The gap between SF's campaign positions and what it can realistically achieve in government negotiations reveals the true hierarchy of its priorities.

**Likely concessions**: The formueskat is almost certainly dead in its proposed form. Moderaterne and RV both oppose it, and Dyhr pre-emptively declined to call it an ultimatum. Store Bededag reinstatement faces uncertain prospects — Frederiksen's preferred single-party S government would not prioritise it, and Moderaterne may resist. The 90% CO₂ reduction target by 2035 will likely be watered down to something between 82% and 90%.

**Core demands SF will fight hardest to preserve**: The nature and biodiversity law with binding 30%/10% targets — presented as a joint red-bloc condition — represents SF's strongest negotiating card because it has unified support from all four red-bloc opposition parties. The pesticide ban on vulnerable groundwater areas, which Dyhr framed as "valgets tale" (the election's message), benefits from broad public support. The welfare-first economic orientation — "aldrig borgerlig økonomisk politik" — is existential for SF's identity but will be tested by Moderaterne's centrist fiscal preferences.

**The structural demand — cabinet seats — is non-negotiable.** Dyhr has nailed this down with "syvtommersøm" (seven-inch nails), per TV2's characterisation. The 2011 trauma makes the støtteparti role impossible for SF to accept without triggering a devastating internal revolt.

## Conclusion: SF holds strong cards in a game where everyone must fold something

SF enters government negotiations from a position of unprecedented strength — 20 seats, the second-largest party, the best result in its history, and an unbroken streak of electoral victories across EU, municipal, and parliamentary elections. Pia Olsen Dyhr has spent twelve years preparing for exactly this moment, rebuilding a party from near-extinction after the 2014 catastrophe.

The critical insight is that **SF's "aldrig igen" rhetoric has been strategically misdirected in public perception**. It does not target Moderaterne — it targets Venstre, Konservative, and the structural error of governing on someone else's economic terms. Dyhr has been quietly building the case for an S+SF+M+RV coalition since early 2024, reframing Løkke as a changed political figure while maintaining the red-line theatrics that satisfy her base. The wealth tax was never an ultimatum; it was a negotiating chip. The binary government-or-opposition stance, by contrast, is genuine — rooted in institutional memory and grassroots fury at the 2011 experience.

The fundamental question is whether four parties with genuinely different economic philosophies — S's pragmatic centrism, SF's democratic socialism, M's centrist liberalism, and RV's social liberalism — can write a government platform that all can claim as a victory. DR's assessment that someone must "give up the campaign's ultimatums" is correct. SF's negotiating art will lie in ensuring that what it gives up (the wealth tax, perhaps some climate ambition) is less visible than what it gains (cabinet seats, the nature law, welfare investment, and the narrative that the red-green direction prevailed). The negotiations that began at Marienborg on 27 March 2026 will test whether Dyhr's twelve-year gamble — patience, rebuilding, strategic pragmatism — finally pays off.
