# Løkke holds the key — and nobody knows which door he'll open

**Moderaterne's 14 seats make Lars Løkke Rasmussen the undisputed kingmaker of the 2026 Danish election**, with neither bloc able to reach the 90-seat majority threshold without him. On election night (24 March 2026), Løkke declined to tip his hand, instead telling Mette Frederiksen and Troels Lund Poulsen to "climb down from their trees" and "come play with us in the center." His core demand is not the prime ministership but the role of *kongelig undersøger* — royal investigator — positioning himself as architect of whatever government emerges. Yet every viable coalition path runs into at least one of Løkke's stated red lines, creating a genuine deadlock that analysts predict will produce "unprecedentedly long and difficult negotiations." As DR analyst Jens Ringberg put it on election night: *"Han har vundet nøglen til magten i Danmark"* — he has won the key to power in Denmark.

---

## The party born to break blocs (2022–2026)

Moderaterne emerged from Løkke's bitter departure from Venstre in 2021, after his cross-bloc government idea was rejected by both S and his own party during the 2019 campaign. Founded formally on Constitution Day 2022, M pitched itself as purple politics — literally mixing red and blue — with a pragmatic, "un-dogmatic and solution-oriented" centrism targeting voters exhausted by bloc warfare. The party's debut at the November 2022 election was spectacular: **9.3% and 16 seats**, making it the third-largest party and the pivot that produced Denmark's first S-V grand coalition since 1972.

The SVM government (Socialdemokratiet–Venstre–Moderaterne) gave M disproportionate influence. With just 16 seats, M secured five ministerial portfolios, including Løkke as Foreign Minister. The coalition's signature achievements bear M's fingerprints: the **Green Tripartite Agreement** (June 2024), making Denmark the first country to tax agricultural CO₂ emissions; the abolition of Store Bededag to fund defense spending; and accelerated NATO 2% GDP commitments. Lars Aagaard (M) led the climate portfolio as minister; Løkke leveraged his PM experience on the world stage.

But SVM ground the party down. Poll numbers cratered from 9.3% to a terrifying **~1.3% at the November 2025 local elections**, where M won just 6 municipal council seats. Three MPs defected between 2023 and 2024 — including co-founder Jeppe Søe, whose departure over a workplace environment scandal was symbolically devastating. The party competed with Radikale Venstre for the same centrist space, and a Megafon analysis found less than half of M's 2022 voters planned to stick with the party.

Then came the Greenland crisis. Løkke's high-profile handling of the Trump administration's territorial provocations as Foreign Minister — appearing on US media, rallying European allies — produced a **dramatic rehabilitation**, catapulting M from existential irrelevance to ~7-8% in weeks. The party entered election night on **7.7%, winning 14 seats** — down 2 from 2022, but once again positioned exactly where Løkke designed it to be: in the center, holding the decisive votes.

## A detailed tax reform and targeted welfare expansion defined the campaign

Moderaterne ran on an ambitious economic platform that positioned the party between Socialdemokratiet's redistributive instincts and Venstre's supply-side orthodoxy. The most consequential policy stance was the **flat rejection of S's proposed 0.5% wealth tax (formueskat) on fortunes above 25 million DKK**. Løkke called this a "red line" — and TV2 commentator Noa Redington declared the wealth tax *"stendød"* (stone dead) on election night, since M, RV, and the entire blue bloc oppose it.

M's own tax blueprint was intricate. The party proposed **restructuring the topskat** — which Løkke called "probably the most growth-inhibiting tax we have" — replacing it with a payroll levy system: 3.2% on salaries above 352,000 DKK paid by employers, with a **negative levy of -5.8%** on the lowest salaries, creating a progressive structure via employer contributions. As a first step, M would raise the top tax threshold from 544,800 to 670,000 DKK. Other proposals included a **flat 35% equity tax** (replacing the 27-42% progressive aktieskat), corporate tax cuts from 22% to 15%, and a novel **tax on future home sale profits** — currently untaxed in Denmark. The Arbejderbevægelsens Erhvervsråd (AE) criticized the equity tax proposal as benefiting the richest 1% (average gain of **89,300 DKK/year**), while nine out of ten income groups would lose.

On welfare, M's flagship was a **universal deltidspension** (part-time pension) beginning three years before the standard pension age, with escalating payments. The party upheld the 2006 Velfærdsforlig with its automatic pension age increases — contrasting with S's proposal to slow them — and proposed replacing the Arne-pension with a unified system for worn-out workers. Free childcare for second and subsequent children, healthcare equalization across regions, and a *lokalgaranti* giving communities the right to take over schools before closure rounded out the domestic agenda.

On climate, M supported existing targets and claimed credit for the Green Tripartite Agreement, while backing **nuclear power** as part of Denmark's energy mix. On immigration, M was **notably more liberal** than both S and V — supporting EU migration pact participation (potentially accepting up to 7,000 refugees/year through redistribution), more openness to foreign workers, and an integration philosophy of "open to all who can and will." This softer immigration stance is one of M's sharpest divergences from potential blue coalition partners.

## Løkke demands the architect's chair, not the corner office

The most critical variable for coalition modeling is what Løkke actually demands. The evidence, drawn from his Politiken interview on 23 March and post-election statements, points to a three-layered posture:

**mDemandPM = No (stated).** Løkke explicitly declared: *"Jeg er ikke statsministerkandidat... jeg skal ikke være statsminister. Det har jeg prøvet"* ("I am not a PM candidate... I'm not going to be PM. I've tried that"). Jakob Engel-Schmidt reinforced this on 25 March: *"Jeg tror ikke, det ligger lige for... et statsministerparti har en vis tyngde"* ("I don't think it's obvious... a PM party should have a certain weight"). However, **skepticism is universal**. Frederiksen said bluntly: *"Jeg tror mest af alt, at det er et udtryk for, at han gerne vil være statsminister igen"* ("I believe most of all that he wants to be PM again"). DR's analysis noted: *"der ikke mange på Christiansborg, der stoler på, at han ikke ville forsøge at bruge den mulighed til selv at rykke ind i Statsministeriet for tredje gang"* ("not many at Christiansborg trust that he wouldn't try to move into the PM's office a third time"). The PM denial should be coded as **credible for first-round negotiations** but **soft for later rounds** — if both Frederiksen and TLP fail, Løkke's offer as kongelig undersøger could morph into a PM bid.

**mDemandGov = Yes (implicit but strong).** Every signal indicates M wants ministerial seats, not merely a support party role. Engel-Schmidt said he'd *"utroligt gerne"* continue as culture minister. Mohammad Rona (M, MF) called the result *"en drømmeposition"* for getting influence. The party's campaign repeatedly framed governance participation — not outside support — as its objective.

**Kongelig undersøger = The primary demand.** Løkke's central strategic play is to be the person who *leads* government formation negotiations: *"Ikke for at danne en regering, men for at undersøge, om der kan skabes et regeringsgrundlag"* ("Not to form a government, but to investigate whether a government platform can be created"). Former Løkke advisor Jacob Bruun explained the logic: *"Han ved godt, at han ikke bliver statsminister, men han kan sætte tempoet i forhandlingerne, han sidder for bordenden, han bestemmer, hvem der må snakke med hvem"* ("He knows he won't become PM, but he can set the pace, sit at the head of the table, decide who gets to talk to whom"). DR's Christine Cordsen assessed this was "not immediately very likely" in a first kongerunde, as bloc parties would nominate their own candidates — but Løkke could emerge in a second or third round if neither succeeds.

## Equidistant rhetoric masks an asymmetric structural reality

Løkke's orientation — the mPmPref parameter — is the most carefully guarded secret in Danish politics. His election night speech was a masterclass in strategic ambiguity. He addressed both rivals by first name — *"Derfor vil jeg sige til Troels og Mette: Prøv nu at kravle ned fra de træer"* — and criticized both: Frederiksen as someone who "can only cooperate if she sits at the head of the table," and Troels Lund Poulsen as wanting to "pull Denmark away from the world." He then left the party without speaking to a single journalist — DR noted the waiting press corps *"måtte stå tilbage med en lang næse"* ("were left with a long nose").

On 25 March, when pressed on TV2 about who he would point to as kongelig undersøger, Løkke deflected sharply: *"Det brugte I også hele sidste valgkamp på at spørge mig om. Jeg synes, det er ekstremt fattigt"* ("You spent the entire last campaign asking me that too. I think this is extremely poor"). He refused to reveal his card: *"Det får vi at se i morgen"* ("We'll see that tomorrow").

Yet **the arithmetic creates an asymmetry** that Løkke's rhetoric cannot fully obscure. Red bloc (84) + M (14) = **98 seats**, a comfortable majority. Blue bloc (77) + M (14) = 91, a bare majority — but only if DF's seats (which Løkke rejects cooperating with) don't actively torpedo legislation. YouGov's pre-election model found that adding M to the red bloc produced a majority **over 98% of the time**, while adding M to the blue bloc succeeded only **~25% of the time**. Information's analysis concluded: *"Umiddelbart kunne man tro, at Løkke reelt kun har én mulighed — at støtte Mette Frederiksen"* ("At first glance, one might think Løkke really only has one option — supporting Frederiksen").

## Every viable path collides with at least one red line

Løkke's stated red lines are clear: no government dependent on **Enhedslisten/Alternativet** (far left), no government including or dependent on **Dansk Folkeparti** (far right), no **wealth tax**, and the government must be *"hen over midten"* (across the center). The problem is that these red lines collectively block every mathematically viable configuration.

**Scenario 1: Centre-left (S + SF + M + RV).** This is the scenario most commentators consider likeliest. Hans Engell predicted on election night: *"Jeg tror, det ender med, at vi får en regering bestående af Socialdemokratiet, SF, Løkke og de radikale. Enhedslisten og Alternativet bliver de to støttepartier."* The problem: S (38) + SF (22) + M (14) + RV (10) = 84 seats — **short of 90**. This government would need Enhedslisten (11) and Alternativet (4) as support parties. That directly contradicts Løkke's red line against far-left dependency. Commentator Pernille Skipper flagged the contradiction: *"Løkke skal sluge den kamel"* ("Løkke will have to swallow that camel").

**Scenario 2: Blue centre government (V + M + KF + LA).** Troels Lund Poulsen proposed this on election night: *"Jeg synes, vi skal prøve at lave en blå midterregering. Muligheden er der, Lars!"* But V (18) + M (14) + KF (?) + LA (?) likely falls short of 90 without DF and DD support. DF's Messerschmidt has declared war: *"Vi kommer til at æde ham og hans folk hver eneste dag."* TV2 analysts Hans Redder and Ask Rostrup both dismissed the blue centre path as "unrealistic" given DF's explicit goal to destroy Moderaterne.

**Scenario 3: New SVM (S + V + M).** Løkke's stated ideal. But Troels Lund Poulsen ruled it out: *"Det har slidt meget på Venstre at være i regering med Socialdemokratiet"* and called any repetition *"historiens største løftebrud."*

**Scenario 4: Løkke as PM of a broad centre government.** Theoretically possible if both Frederiksen and TLP fail. DR's analysis warned that *"trods Løkkes besværgelser, er der ikke mange på Christiansborg, der stoler på, at han ikke ville forsøge at bruge den mulighed til selv at rykke ind i Statsministeriet for tredje gang."* This is the wild card scenario, and one Løkke has not definitively closed.

## Party-by-party compatibility reveals a fragmented landscape

**M ↔ Socialdemokratiet (S):** Three years of SVM partnership created a working relationship, but Løkke's wealth tax rejection and more liberal immigration stance create friction. The TV2 candidate test showed Løkke and S leader Nicolai Wammen disagreeing on **8 of 24 questions**. Frederiksen publicly doubts Løkke's motives. Nevertheless, this is probably the **most functional partnership** available.

**M ↔ SF:** The highest policy distance among potential partners — Løkke and Pia Olsen Dyhr disagreed on **11 of 24 candidate test questions** (economic inequality, euthanasia, nuclear power, pension age). SF grassroots members expressed deep unease. But Olsen Dyhr offered a pragmatic assessment: *"Lars Løkke er Lars Løkke. Når man laver aftaler med ham, så holder han dem"* ("When you make agreements with him, he keeps them").

**M ↔ Venstre (V):** The most emotionally complex relationship. Løkke founded V's rival after 40 years as a member. TLP directly appealed to Løkke on election night, using his first name. Despite the split, there's a personal bond. But V's categorical rejection of new SVM cooperation limits options.

**M ↔ Liberal Alliance (LA):** Significant ideological gap (LA's libertarian economics vs. M's pragmatic centrism), but LA is even considering pointing to Løkke as forhandlingsleder — a significant signal of openness.

**M ↔ Konservative (KF):** Relatively compatible; both centrist-pragmatic. KF's Mette Abildgaard explicitly cautioned against premature exclusions: *"Jeg tror, danskerne har en forventning om, at vi er voksne mennesker, der søger hinanden for Danmarks bedste."*

**M ↔ Dansk Folkeparti (DF):** Toxic and mutual. Messerschmidt's "devour" threat and Løkke's rejection of DF dependency make cooperation functionally impossible. They disagreed on **14 of 24 candidate test questions** — the maximum divergence.

**M ↔ Radikale Venstre (RV):** Natural allies in the centrist space but electoral competitors. Both oppose the wealth tax. RV's Lidegaard refused to reveal his kongerunde card on election night, needing to "sleep on it" — suggesting he may be weighing whether to point to Løkke rather than Frederiksen.

**M ↔ Enhedslisten (EL):** Ideologically incompatible (14 of 24 disagreements). EL demands a "red government" as precondition for supporting any PM. The paradox is complete: the most likely government formation (S+SF+M+RV) requires EL tolerance, but Løkke has sworn against exactly this dependency.

## Denmark's most pragmatic politician faces his defining test

Commentators consistently describe Løkke as Denmark's ultimate deal-maker. His career arc supports this: he formed a pure Venstre minority government with just **34 seats** in 2015; he engineered the SVM grand coalition in 2022 despite the red bloc's technical majority; and he navigated Denmark's 2008 bank bailout and 2009 tax reform as Finance Minister. His negotiating personality is characterized by strategic ambiguity, patience, and willingness to take unconventional paths.

Crucially, M's organizational weakness is paradoxically a negotiating strength. As TV2 commentator Lawand Hiwa Namo observed: *"Moderaterne står mere frit og har i deres egne øjne alle de snusfornuftige løsninger. Og de har ikke et stort bagland, de skal slås med"* ("M stands more freely... they don't have a big grassroots base to fight with, unlike S and V"). Løkke can pivot without internal revolt. Information captured the strategic essence: *"At ingen helt ved, hvor de har Lars Løkke Rasmussen, er hans største trumf"* ("That nobody quite knows where they have Løkke is his greatest trump card").

The evidence strongly suggests **Løkke will ultimately swallow the camel** on the far-left dependency question. His red lines are best understood as **negotiating positions, not binding commitments** — tools for extracting maximum policy concessions. The most likely endgame remains a Frederiksen-led government with S, SF, M, and RV, where Løkke trades his far-left red line for substantive policy wins: blocking the wealth tax, securing tax reform elements, retaining a senior ministerial portfolio (likely Foreign Affairs or Finance), and shaping the government platform through his kongelig undersøger bid. But the path there will be winding. Multiple kongerunder are expected, and DR called the uncertainty "very unusual" — *"Det er meget usædvanligt, at vi ikke ved, hvem der på et tidspunkt i morgen ser ud til at blive udpeget som kongelig undersøger."*

## Conclusion

Three findings reshape the standard coalition calculus. First, **Løkke's primary demand is procedural, not positional** — he wants to lead negotiations (kongelig undersøger) rather than hold the PM title, giving him leverage that outlasts any single formation attempt. Second, **his red lines are strategically contradictory by design**: by vetoing both far-left dependency and DF, he ensures that *no government can form without directly negotiating with him*, effectively forcing both blocs to accept M as a governing partner rather than a mere support party. Third, M's **institutional thinness** — a leader-dependent party with negligible grassroots organization — means Løkke can negotiate with near-total personal flexibility, unburdened by internal democratic constraints that slow S and V. The most probable outcome is a centre-left coalition (S+SF+M+RV) with Enhedslisten tolerance, but the timeline will be measured in weeks, not days — and the final configuration will bear Løkke's fingerprints whether or not he sits at the head of the table.
