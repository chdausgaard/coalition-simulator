# Alternativet's 5 seats and the green lever in Danish government formation

**Alternativet (Å) enters post-election negotiations with 5 seats, a single non-negotiable demand — the "Svinepagt" on pig welfare — and conditional support for Mette Frederiksen that hinges on whether she commits to forming a genuinely red government rather than a centrist one.** The party has not directly pointed to Frederiksen as statsminister, aligning with Enhedslisten in making support contingent on the government's ideological direction. This matters because the red bloc's 84 seats — 6 short of a majority — need Moderaterne's 14 swing seats, creating a structural tension between Alternativet's green-left demands and Lars Løkke Rasmussen's refusal to join a government "dependent on the far left." Alternativet's leverage is real but secondary to Enhedslisten's, and its ultimate role will likely be determined by the EL-Moderaterne standoff rather than by anything ALT itself does.

---

## Rosenkilde rebuilt a jester party into a focused green force

**Franciska Rosenkilde** has led Alternativet since February 7, 2021, when she was elected at an extraordinary national meeting with 260 of 471 votes. A geographer by training and former Mayor of Culture and Leisure in Copenhagen (2018–2021), she describes Alternativet as a "green, progressive party" — explicitly distinguishing it from socialism. On DR's *Middag med magten*, she stated: "Although we in Alternativet see ourselves as a green progressive party and not a socialist party, the red bloc is without doubt the bloc we share the greatest value community with."

Rosenkilde's leadership project has been one of disciplined reinvention. She acknowledged having "pulled the party out of the party" (a play on the Danish word *fest* meaning both party and celebration), working to shed what critics called the "gøglerparti" (jester party) image that plagued ALT during its chaotic 2019–2021 period — when founder Uffe Elbæk departed, four MPs defected to form Frie Grønne, and the short-lived Josephine Fock leadership collapsed after nine months. The lex.dk encyclopedia entry credits Rosenkilde with "creating calm and stability and bringing more seriousness into the project." She was re-elected unopposed at the 2025 Vejle party congress.

The party's ideological substance has remained consistent — climate, green transition, new political culture — but its **style and strategic focus sharpened markedly** during 2022–2026. Three developments drove this. First, ALT absorbed **Grøn Alliance** (September 2022) and **Klimapartiet Momentum** (October 2022) before the November 2022 election, consolidating Denmark's fragmented green-party landscape and importing members with strong animal-rights and radical climate positions. Second, as commentator Hans Engell observed, other parties "stole" Alternativet's green agenda, forcing the party to differentiate by pushing further — proposing **100% climate neutrality by 2035** and **halving Danish livestock production within four years**. Third, the Gaza conflict after October 7, 2023 added a human rights/international solidarity dimension. Rosenkilde repeatedly used parliamentary question time to press PM Frederiksen on the war, calling it "in my view — and many others' view — a genocide" and criticizing Frederiksen's statement that "Denmark stands unconditionally on Israel's side" as placing the country "on the wrong side of history."

The most significant internal rupture came on **September 17, 2023**, when **Theresa Scavenius** was expelled from the parliamentary group by unanimous decision of the remaining members. The official explanation cited "unwillingness to cooperate," "harsh treatment of employees," and behavior that "damaged Alternativet's cooperation with other parties and ministries." Scavenius offered a different account, claiming the real conflict was political — she had broken with the party line on a Koran-burning ban and represented an uncompromising, principled approach that clashed with Rosenkilde's cooperative strategy. Scavenius left the party on September 21, 2023, continuing as an independent MP.

Throughout the SVM government era (December 2022–March 2026), Alternativet sat in **clear opposition**. Rosenkilde called the SVM government a "centrum-højre-regering" (centre-right government) and "Danmark A/S" (Denmark Inc.) — "a working community not bound by values and visions, but a power community" focused on "purely technocratic management of our country as a business, not a society." ALT was not part of the broad 10-party defense agreement of June 2023, though it did join the broadly agreed 2024 finance law — making it one of the few broad agreements ALT participated in.

## The Svinepagt became ALT's single ultimatum for 2026

Alternativet's 2026 campaign was dominated by a single demand elevated to the status of **ultimative krav**: the "Svinepagt" (Pig Pact). Announced at the party congress and formalized during the campaign, it contains four specific requirements — stop extreme breeding, end fixation of sows, halt routine tail-docking, and give pigs more space. Rosenkilde framed it around a striking fact: Denmark produces **40 million pigs annually** and the country is "far too small for this violent swine industry." TV2 described the election as a "svinevalg" (pig election). No other party signed the Pig Pact because of its absolute character — Kristeligt Dagblad noted that "no other parties have been willing to sign the demand, because it is ultimate."

Beyond the Svinepagt, the party's campaign platform was structured around an ambitious green agenda. ALT proposed an **86% reduction in meat production**, replacing conventional farming with pesticide-free and regenerative methods, with the goal of producing only as much meat as Danes themselves consume rather than exporting. The party positioned itself as "Danmarks nye landbrugsparti" (Denmark's new agricultural party).

On **climate targets**, ALT called for **100% climate neutrality by 2035** — the most ambitious target of any Danish party. Rosenkilde called anything less "simply unserious and irresponsible." ALT joined SF, Enhedslisten, and Radikale Venstre in a formal joint demand for **90% CO₂ reduction by 2035**. Additional climate proposals included a high uniform CO₂ tax, a permanent national Climate Citizens' Assembly (Klimaborgerting), a stop to fossil "prestige projects" (new motorways, artificial islands, airport expansions), and green public procurement requirements.

On **taxation**, ALT's platform supports shifting tax from labor to wealth and introducing green levies, including a green VAT (grøn moms) making organic food cheaper. The party has "generally supported the concept of a wealth tax," according to Dansk Industri's analysis, but — crucially — **formueskat was not ALT's ultimatum**. TV2's political commentator Noa Redington declared on election night that "formueskatten er stendød" (the wealth tax is stone dead) and that "it was not a big demand from either Enhedslisten or Alternativet."

On **immigration**, ALT maintained its consistently humanitarian profile — full compliance with the UN Refugee Convention, right to work from day one for asylum seekers, children out of deportation centers, safe legal routes to asylum. Rosenkilde previously labeled Denmark's special law for Ukrainian refugees as "statsracisme" (state racism) for creating a two-tier system based on nationality. However, **immigration was not a primary campaign focus** in 2026; the party campaigned overwhelmingly on climate, agriculture, and animal welfare.

On **fiscal policy**, ALT rejects GDP as the primary measure of progress, advocating a triple bottom line of social, environmental, and economic sustainability. The party supports universal basic income (borgerløn) as a long-term vision, free green organic school meals, mental health houses in all municipalities, and removing the anlægsloft (construction ceiling) to allow municipal green investment. CARE Denmark and the Blogsbjerg analysis blog noted that ALT has "the most ambitious climate policies" among Danish parties but "lacks clear proposals and models for how the various measures can be financed."

Green issues surged unexpectedly in the final two weeks of the campaign, driven by a Miljøministeriet report on pesticide contamination of drinking water and media coverage of pig farming conditions. On March 2, 2026, Enhedslisten, Alternativet, SF, and Radikale Venstre jointly demanded a **total ban on pesticides** in areas where drinking water is formed. Rosenkilde said on election night: "I got a shock that climate was not being discussed when the election was called, but I can tell you that climate, environment, animal welfare, and clean drinking water are back in the campaign."

## Election night: conditional support and no direct endorsement of Frederiksen

Alternativet held its election party in **Kødbyen** (Copenhagen's Meatpacking District) — a characteristically on-brand choice. The party won **5 seats on 2.6% of the vote**, down from 6 seats (3.3%) in 2022, but clearing the 2% threshold that had been in doubt throughout the campaign. DR's exit poll initially projected 4.1%/7 seats, but the final count was lower. The loss of one mandate came partly from the Nordsjælland constituency, where Helene Brydensholt lost her seat.

Rosenkilde's election-night statements were carefully calibrated. Her key quote to TV2: **"Forhåbentligt skal de bruges til at trække en rød regering i en grøn, grøn retning"** ("Hopefully they will be used to pull a red government in a green, green direction"). To DR: "Vi vil trække en rød regering i en meget grønnere retning og kræve noget mere klimapolitisk lederskab og en plan for omstillingen af det danske landbrug" ("We want to pull a red government in a much greener direction and demand more climate leadership and a plan for agricultural transition").

The critical signal: **ALT did not directly point to Mette Frederiksen as statsminister.** DR explicitly noted: "Alternativet, der trods tvivl formåede at komme over spærregrænsen, har ikke direkte peget på Mette Frederiksen som statsminister." The condition, stated previously during the campaign and unchanged post-election, is that Frederiksen must commit to forming a "rød regering" — not a centrist government. This mirrors Enhedslisten's identical position; Pelle Dragsted stated: "Vi peger kun på en forhandlingsleder, der ønsker en rød regering."

On the question of whether a red government based on ALT and EL mandates is even feasible, Rosenkilde herself acknowledged profound uncertainty. In a March 11 TV2 interview: "Much of the politics Frederiksen has come up with in the campaign has been quite red. But I sense she wants a centre government... **Whether it is realistic to imagine a government with Frederiksen at the helm using Enhedslisten's and Alternativet's mandates — I honestly don't quite know.**"

Post-election, on **March 25**, four red-bloc parties — SF, Radikale Venstre, Enhedslisten, and Alternativet — jointly demanded a **binding nature and biodiversity law** with **30% protected nature on land and sea, 10% strictly protected**, as a condition for supporting any government. This joint demand signal represents the most concrete post-election coordination among the left-flank parties.

## The structural paradox constraining ALT's leverage

Alternativet's negotiating position is defined by a **structural paradox**: its seats are necessary for the red bloc's numerical advantage (84 vs. 77 for the blue bloc), but the red bloc cannot govern without Moderaterne's 14 seats, and Moderaterne's ideological direction conflicts with ALT's demands.

The arithmetic is unforgiving. A centre-left government of S+SF+M+RV would command approximately **82 seats** — still 8 short of the 90-seat majority. Such a government needs at least EL's 11 seats (bringing the total to 93) and likely ALT's 5 as well (to 87 or 98). But Lars Løkke Rasmussen has repeatedly stated he will not sit in a government "based on mandates from either the far left or the far right." As former EL leader **Pernille Skipper** noted on TV2: "That means Løkke has to swallow the camel" — a formulation capturing the irreconcilable tension.

Veteran commentator **Hans Engell** (former Conservative leader) offered the most concrete prediction on election night: "I believe we'll end up with a government consisting of Socialdemokratiet, SF, Løkke (Moderaterne), and the Radicals. Enhedslisten and Alternativet become the two support parties." This is the scenario that best accommodates all constraints: Frederiksen leads, Løkke participates in government, and the left flank provides external support without formal government responsibility.

**DR political correspondent Christine Cordsen** called the situation "extremely unusual" — for the first time in recent memory, it was unclear on election night who would be named royal investigator. **Pia Glud Munksgaard** (DR political analyst) emphasized: "It could become extremely decisive for the traditional red bloc whether Alternativet gets in or not, when everything is this close." Former Venstre tax minister **Karsten Lauritzen** assessed that Løkke is in a "dream position" (ønskeposition) despite losing votes, because he holds the decisive mandates.

The fundamental constraint on ALT's green demands is clear: any government including Moderaterne will be less green than what ALT wants. Moderaterne have called for a "realistic and technology-enthusiastic green transition" tied to economic responsibility — directly conflicting with ALT's call for degrowth-oriented agricultural transformation. DR's political editor noted that "climate minister Lars Aagaard (M) wants the parties to cool down and wait for his ministry's own forecast in April" — signaling pushback on green ambitions even before negotiations begin.

## How ALT compares to Enhedslisten as a left-flank support party

The comparison between Alternativet and Enhedslisten is essential for understanding left-flank dynamics, and the differences are more significant than the similarities.

**Enhedslisten has roughly double ALT's leverage** — 11 seats versus 5. In the critical arithmetic, a centre-left government (S+SF+M+RV ≈ 82 seats) could theoretically survive with EL support alone (82+11=93) but not with ALT support alone (82+5=87). This means **a government can potentially function without ALT's votes, but not without EL's**. ALT's leverage is therefore derivative — it matters most when combined with EL's, and its bargaining power is structurally subordinate.

The parties' demands differ in scope. EL takes a **broad, systemic approach** — wealth tax on fortunes above 35 million DKK, a formal forståelsespapir (written agreement), inequality reduction, compliance with international conventions, and softer immigration policy. ALT's demands are **narrowly focused on green and animal welfare issues** — the Svinepagt, climate neutrality by 2035, agricultural transformation, nature protection. EL demands a written understanding paper before supporting any government; ALT has not made this a formal requirement.

On coordination, the two parties have issued **multiple joint demands** together with SF and Radikale Venstre — the nature/biodiversity law, the pesticide ban, the 90% climate target. Both have taken the identical position on government formation: support only for a "rød regering." But this coordination is issue-specific rather than a formal alliance, and their different emphases mean they create distinct pressure points. EL pushes on economic redistribution; ALT pushes on agricultural and environmental transformation.

Commentators consistently treat the two parties as a pair. Engell refers to "Enhedslisten and Alternativet" as the two support parties in his predicted government scenario. Redington groups them together on the wealth tax. But **NordiskPost's analysis focuses on EL as the more problematic partner** for Løkke, noting that "the Moderates and the Social Liberals are politically far from Enhedslisten on major economic and welfare questions." ALT's green profile is less threatening to centrist partners than EL's economic radicalism — giving ALT potentially slightly more room in negotiations, though less structural weight.

The most likely scenario in which ALT and EL both play a role is Engell's: an **S+SF+M+RV government with EL and ALT as external support parties**. In this configuration, ALT would condition its annual budget support (finanslov) on green concessions — primarily agricultural reform and climate ambition — while EL would press on economic and welfare issues. The scenario in which ALT opposes while EL supports (or vice versa) is theoretically possible but unlikely given their aligned positions. The more realistic risk is that **both refuse to support a government they deem insufficiently red**, creating a gridlock that forces either Frederiksen or Løkke to make concessions.

## Conclusion: green leverage in a centrist vise

Alternativet enters government formation with clear demands, a focused green identity, and a structural position that gives it influence without decisive power. The Svinepagt is a distinctive and media-effective ultimatum, but its absolute character — no other party has signed it — means Rosenkilde will eventually face a choice between standing firm and being marginalized, or accepting partial concessions on animal welfare in exchange for broader green gains.

The party's most significant post-election move is its **alignment with Enhedslisten and the other red-green parties on joint demands** — the nature/biodiversity law, climate targets, and pesticide bans. This coordination amplifies ALT's 5 seats by embedding them in a 16-seat left-flank bloc (EL+ALT) that is mathematically necessary for most viable government formations. The question is whether Rosenkilde's acknowledged pragmatism — "politics is cooperation and negotiation... if you say we only participate if we get everything point by point, you get no influence" — will ultimately lead ALT to accept a centrist government including Moderaterne, provided it makes sufficient green concessions.

The kongerunde has not yet taken place as of March 25, 2026. ALT's formal recommendation to the King remains pending, and concrete negotiations have not begun. The party's stated condition — a "rød regering" — may prove to be an opening position rather than a hard veto, particularly if the only alternative is opposition irrelevance. The coming weeks will test whether Alternativet's green lever is strong enough to extract real policy concessions from a government that will, by mathematical necessity, include partners far less green than Alternativet itself.
