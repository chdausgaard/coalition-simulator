# Radikale Venstre's bid for relevance in Denmark's fractured parliament

**Radikale Venstre enters government formation negotiations in its strongest position since 2022, yet faces a central paradox: the party's traditional kingmaker role has been usurped by Moderaterne.** With roughly 10 mandates after the March 24, 2026 election — a gain of 3 seats — RV is a near-certain component of the most widely predicted coalition formula (S+SF+M+RV), but it is not the decisive swing player. Lars Løkke Rasmussen's 14 Moderaterne seats hold that power. Martin Lidegaard has responded with a strategy of disciplined ambiguity: no ultimatums, no burned bridges, but a clear conditional demand that Mette Frederiksen commit to building "the greenest government Denmark has ever had" before RV will point to her as formateur. As of March 25, the kongerunde had not yet begun, and RV had deliberately withheld its recommendation — a tactical delay designed to maximize leverage in the opening hours of negotiation.

---

## Lidegaard rebuilt RV from rubble after 2022

Martin Lidegaard took over Radikale Venstre on **November 3, 2022**, inheriting a party in crisis. The 2022 election had slashed RV from 16 seats to just 7 — its worst result in a generation. His predecessor Sofie Carsten Nielsen resigned immediately. Lidegaard, a former Climate Minister and Foreign Minister in the Thorning-Schmidt government, had lost an internal leadership contest to Nielsen in 2020 and was now the party's last credible option.

His first and most consequential decision was to **walk away from the SVM government negotiations** in December 2022. After five weeks at Marienborg, Lidegaard concluded that RV would have too little influence inside a three-party government dominated by Socialdemokratiet, Venstre, and Moderaterne. "As a small party, we would get too small a role in government and would have to swallow too large camels," he told DR. The decision proved deeply divisive internally. **Christian Friis Bach**, a senior MP and former development minister, publicly stated he "would really have liked to have been in that government" and described the crisis as the most serious in his 40 years in the party. The tension culminated in **August 2024** when Friis Bach left RV entirely and joined Venstre — an event the party's youth wing called a "moment of liberation."

Under Lidegaard, RV's ideological profile shifted in three ways. First, the party moved from its traditional posture as a **centrist kingmaker** toward a firmer red-bloc alignment, while retaining centrist economic credentials. Second, Lidegaard introduced the **"generationskontrakt"** (generation contract) as the party's overarching narrative — framing politics as a conflict between present consumption and future generations' interests in climate, education, and clean water. Third, he deliberately **downgraded immigration** as a defining issue, arguing that "immigration policy had become the only thing the party was known for." Climate and education replaced it as RV's signature terrain.

The opposition years produced a mixed record. RV was the **only opposition party** to vote for abolishing Store Bededag (Great Prayer Day) in February 2023, drawing fierce internal criticism — former party chair Søren Bald called RV "useful idiots for the government." Yet in April 2023, RV formed the **KLAR reform alliance** with Liberal Alliance and Det Konservative Folkeparti, an unprecedented cross-bloc partnership on economic reform including abolishing efterløn and lowering income tax. The alliance demonstrated RV's willingness to work across bloc boundaries even while formally sitting in opposition. RV's **7.1% result** in the June 2024 European Parliament election — well above domestic polls — validated Lidegaard's strategy and elected 29-year-old Sigrid Friis, reinforcing the generational brand.

## "Plan B" — RV's campaign platform prioritized climate and children

RV ran under the banner **"Plan B for Danmark,"** explicitly positioning itself as the alternative to the SVM era. The platform rested on two pillars that Lidegaard identified as his "hovedkrav" (main demands) for government negotiations: green transition and education.

**On climate**, RV staked out the most ambitious targets in Danish politics: **90% CO₂ reduction by 2035** (versus the government's 82%), climate neutrality by 2040, and **110% reduction (net-negative) by 2045** — requiring revision of the Climate Act. Specific proposals included a full deployment of onshore wind and solar within five years, a "svineloft" halving pig production over a decade, a total pesticide ban on vulnerable groundwater areas, and a ban on trawl fishing. On **March 2**, RV joined SF, Enhedslisten, and Alternativet in a formal joint demand for the 90% target and the pesticide ban — the first coordinated red-green policy platform of the campaign. Lidegaard called the lack of climate debate in the first party leader debate "depressing" and described a "massive political failure."

**On taxation**, RV broke sharply with Socialdemokratiet by declaring itself **explicitly against the wealth tax (formueskat)**. Lidegaard called S's proposal something that "approaches nationally damaging activity." TV2 commentator Noa Redington declared the wealth tax "stone dead" after the election, noting that "it's not just the blue parties that have been against it — it's also Moderaterne and Radikale Venstre." RV instead advocated progressive income taxation, abolishing VAT on fruit and vegetables, higher taxes on cigarettes, alcohol, and sugar, and a CO₂ tax of 800 kr/ton.

**On welfare**, RV proposed **DKK 15 billion annually** for children's services — daycares, schools, free school meals — alongside raising kontanthjælp so no children live below the poverty line and reversing SVM-era university and education cuts. **On immigration**, RV maintained its liberal tradition, with Lidegaard declaring "remigration — a word from the most extreme far-right in Europe — has no place in Denmark." The party defended international conventions, family reunification rights, and a common EU asylum system. **On defense**, RV broke new ground by advocating for a **European Defense Union**, with Lidegaard telling TV2: "I wouldn't mind seeing an EU flag on the shoulder of our deployed soldiers alongside Dannebrog." The party called for democratic oversight of the DKK 195 billion defense spending agreed through 2033.

## Coalition signals reveal strategic ambiguity with a red tilt

RV's coalition positioning combined deliberate openness with unmistakable preferences. Lidegaard's core formula, stated at the Plan B launch on February 27: **"If it is a centre-left government that works across the middle, we are open to the two parties that are most obvious: Socialdemokratiet and SF."** He identified Frederiksen as "the most likely" statsminister and declared unambiguously: **"It is a clear wish, intention, and will to enter government"** — meaning RV wants ministerial seats, not a mere support-party role.

The party's stance toward potential partners breaks down as follows:

- **Socialdemokratiet**: Natural senior partner. Deep historical precedent (Thorning government, 2011-2015). But friction exists over the wealth tax and trust issues from 2022.
- **SF**: Close ally on climate and education. The two parties have governed together before and share overlapping voter bases on green issues.
- **Moderaterne**: Pragmatic openness. Lidegaard acknowledged mutual dependency during a debate: "As the polls stand, we are probably a bit dependent on each other, Lars Løkke and I." Both oppose the wealth tax. SF's Pia Olsen Dyhr described RV and M as "two centre parties of the same type."
- **Venstre**: Practically blocked. Troels Lund Poulsen explicitly vetoed RV on TV2's "Højskolen" on March 17, citing the 2022 walkout: "It'll be difficult with De Radikale — they ran from an agreed government platform last time I participated in government negotiations." Lidegaard took it calmly, noting "it's been seen before that Venstre changes its mind after an election."
- **Enhedslisten**: RV is "ideologically far from Enhedslisten on economic issues," creating structural tension in any arrangement requiring EL support.

The most critical departure from RV predecessors is Lidegaard's **explicit rejection of ultimative demands**. He told journalists: "We have no ultimative demands. But our position is clear." This contrasts sharply with Morten Østergaard's 2019 ultimatum threatening to topple the S government over immigration, and Sofie Carsten Nielsen's rigid approach. Lidegaard instead identified three areas where "something absolutely must happen" — climate, education, and retirement — while avoiding the language of red lines. The party joined SF, EL, and Alternativet in a post-election joint demand for a **nature and biodiversity law** with binding targets of 30% protected nature as a condition for lending mandates.

On whether RV would repeat a cross-bloc SVM-style government: the entire "Plan B" campaign was a rejection of SVM's record. Lidegaard charged that under SVM "the green transition ground to a halt" and Denmark had "for the first time in history a government that with open eyes bets on lowering the education level." Combined with Venstre's explicit veto of RV, a new SVM-with-RV configuration is effectively dead.

## The kongerunde: Lidegaard's calculated silence

RV's formateur strategy on election night was striking for its **deliberate withholding**. When pressed on who he would recommend as kongelig undersøger, Lidegaard said: "I understand the question well, but it's a question you'll first get an answer to tomorrow. We just need to sleep on it." This was not indecision — it was tactical. DR's political correspondent Christine Cordsen called it "very unusual that we don't know who appears likely to be appointed."

Lidegaard's **conditional backing of Frederiksen** was the key signal. He told TV2 on March 25: "That is at least the precondition for me to point to her as negotiation leader after the election" — referring to his demand that Frederiksen commit to a red government, not another SVM-style arrangement. When asked about Troels Lund Poulsen as an alternative: "That would require a blue majority. And I really hope there isn't one." He added a revealing contingency: **"If we end up in a situation where there's a blue majority without us, I would feel obligated to see if Radikale Venstre could replace Dansk Folkeparti in such a constellation"** — preserving the bridge-party option in theory while clearly preferring the red path.

The party's posture toward Løkke's kingmaker role has been notably restrained. RV neither explicitly welcomed nor resisted it. **Samira Nawa**, RV's deputy group leader, said on March 25 she found it "strange" that no kongelig undersøger had been named yet — suggesting frustration with the uncertainty but stopping short of criticizing Løkke directly. As of March 25, three candidates were in play for kongelig undersøger (Frederiksen, Troels Lund Poulsen, Løkke), multiple kongerunder were expected, and RV had still not formally announced its recommendation.

## Experts see RV as likely in government but not driving the process

The dominant commentator consensus points toward an **S+SF+M+RV four-party government** — a prediction made most explicitly by veteran analyst Hans Engell on TV2 election night: "I think it ends with a government consisting of Socialdemokratiet, SF, Løkke (Moderaterne), and the Radikals. Enhedslisten and Alternativet become the two support parties." NordiskPost called this "the clearest viable formula inside the new Folketing."

However, analysts identify **three structural problems** with this formula. First, Løkke has repeatedly said he will not sit in a government dependent on mandates from the far left (Enhedslisten) or far right — yet any S+SF+M+RV government would require exactly that external support. TV2's Pernille Skipper challenged Engell directly: "That means Løkke has to swallow the camel that he said he won't." Second, trust remains fragile: the 2022 walkout left lasting scars, with Troels Lund Poulsen and elements within S still suspicious of RV's reliability. Third, RV's roughly **10 mandates give it relevance but not indispensability** — an S+SF+M government without RV would still command the same external support structure.

**Ask Rostrup** (TV2) directly assessed RV's kingmaker potential and found it wanting: "There are two parties that can bring themselves into play as kingmaker: Radikale Venstre and Moderaterne." But he concluded that Moderaterne can do this "without problems" while RV's leftward tilt limits its bridge function. Moderaterne's 14 seats and ideological flexibility simply outclass RV's 10 seats in the pivotal-center game. **Netavisen Pio** drew the parallel starkly: "In 2011, Radikale Venstre showed you could rule Denmark with just 9.5% of the votes. Now the ghost of 2011 is back in the form of Lars Løkke Rasmussen and Moderaterne."

On Lidegaard's negotiating style, the assessment is **more pragmatic than predecessors but untested under real pressure**. Former RV campaign chief Jakob Gejl Møller wrote in Altinget that Lidegaard marks "a significant break" from Jelved, Vestager, Østergaard, and Carsten Nielsen by narrowing the party into an "interest party" focused on climate and education rather than the traditional "whole-of-society" approach. Lidegaard himself told DR in the pre-election program "Middag med magten": **"I'm a power person. I belong in a government."** He explicitly said the 2022 walkout was "probably the hardest decision I've made in my life" and vowed not to repeat it, adding: "I should be measured on whether we are in government." This self-awareness suggests he will be significantly more willing to compromise than in 2022 — but the question, as DR's analysis noted, is whether "the S leadership can trust that Martin Lidegaard has control over the radical group."

## Conclusion: the price of a seat at the table

Radikale Venstre's trajectory from 2022 to 2026 tells a story of deliberate reinvention under Lidegaard — from a devastated centrist party to a green-progressive force with a coherent generational narrative and a recovered electoral base. The party's strategic position is clear: it almost certainly enters government in the most likely S+SF+M+RV configuration, contributing mandates and green-policy credibility. Its wealth tax opposition aligns it with Moderaterne and gives it a centrist economic veto that Socialdemokratiet must accommodate. Its climate demands — the 90% target, pesticide ban, revised Climate Act — represent the firmest policy commitments it brings to negotiations, backed by coordination with the broader red-green bloc.

But RV's leverage has structural limits that campaign rhetoric cannot overcome. Moderaterne, not RV, holds the true kingmaker position. Lidegaard's no-ultimatum strategy is wise given the 2022 credibility damage, but it also means RV has fewer hard bargaining chips than parties willing to threaten walkouts. The deepest tension lies in the gap between Lidegaard's stated ambition — "the greenest government ever" — and the mathematical reality that any viable government requires accommodating Løkke, who has shown little appetite for radical green policy. Whether Lidegaard can thread this needle — securing enough climate policy to justify entering government without repeating 2022's last-minute retreat — is the defining test of his leadership in the days ahead.
