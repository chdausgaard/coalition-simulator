# Liberal Alliance in 2026: pragmatic ambition meets parliamentary arithmetic

**Liberal Alliance emerged from the 24 March 2026 election with its best result ever — 16 seats on 9.4% of the vote — yet faces the paradox of historic strength amid structural powerlessness.** The blue bloc's 77 seats fall 13 short of a governing majority, Moderaterne's 14 kingmaker mandates sit in Lars Løkke Rasmussen's hands, and veteran commentator Hans Engell predicted on election night that the likeliest government (S + SF + M + RV) would exclude LA entirely. Alex Vanopslagh's response to this arithmetic has been the defining signal of the post-election period: within hours of the final count showing Venstre at 18 seats versus LA's 16, he dropped his conditional PM claim, endorsed Troels Lund Poulsen as negotiation leader, and pivoted to courting Løkke — a sequence that reveals a leader playing a longer game than one election cycle.

---

## From near-death to record result: LA's transformation under Vanopslagh

Liberal Alliance's trajectory from 2022 to 2026 represents one of Danish politics' more remarkable rehabilitations. After collapsing to 4 seats (2.3%) in 2019 under Anders Samuelsen's disastrous ultimatum strategy, the party surged to **14 seats in November 2022** and then **16 seats in March 2026**, building a fundamentally different political identity along the way.

The ideological shift is best understood as a move from **classical libertarianism toward pragmatic, culturally conservative national liberalism**. Vanopslagh's 2023 manifesto *Vejen til ansvar* (The Road to Responsibility) explicitly rejected the old LA image of "topskat og billige biler" (top tax and cheap cars), instead grounding the party in Grundtvigian freedom concepts and Hayekian rule-of-law principles. The party embraced personal responsibility, duty, and community values alongside market economics. Former LA minister Simon Emil Ammitzbøll-Bille observed critically that "freedom and open-mindedness have been replaced by raised fingers and a hint of moralism."

Three concrete shifts illustrate this evolution. On **social issues**, LA moved from being a lonely blue-bloc champion of LGBTQ+ rights to expressing skepticism toward transgender surgery for minors and expanded abortion access — influenced partly by Jordan Peterson's cultural conservatism. On **immigration**, the party abandoned its founding "open borders, closed coffers" principle for a hard line: ending spontaneous asylum, individual screening of citizenship applicants for anti-democratic attitudes, and expulsion of criminal foreigners. Pernille Vermund's January 2024 defection from Nye Borgerlige to LA cemented this rightward turn. On **European alignment**, LA joined the EPP group rather than liberal Renew Europe at the 2024 European Parliament elections, signaling a broader center-right identity.

The **April 2024 landsmøde** marked the pivotal strategic shift. Vanopslagh scrapped the party's long-term 2035 plan and the ambition to fully abolish topskat in one term, replacing it with a concrete four-year plan to halve it. TV2 reported this as a deliberate repositioning to make LA credible as a "regeringsbærende parti" — a core governing party rather than an influence or protest vehicle. This was the clearest break from the Samuelsen era's maximalist demands.

During the **SVM government period** (2022–2026), LA positioned itself as the authentic liberal-bourgeois alternative to Venstre, which LA argued was compromised by sitting in a cross-bloc government with Socialdemokratiet. Key opposition battlegrounds included the abolition of Store Bededag (the Great Prayer Day holiday), which LA opposed on principle; the new "toptopskat" (an additional 5% tax on incomes above ~2.59 million DKK), which LA made a dealbreaker; and what Vanopslagh called the SVM's "lukewarm compromises" that combined "the worst of populism and technocracy." LA attracted an estimated 50,000 former Venstre voters by 2023 and frequently polled ahead of V through 2023–2025, positioning Vanopslagh as the de facto leader of the liberal-conservative opposition.

## LA's 2026 platform: 47.7 billion kroner in tax cuts and nuclear reactors

LA's election campaign ran under the slogan **"En frisk start"** (A Fresh Start), built around a fully financed four-year economic plan that was the most fiscally ambitious proposal from any party.

**Taxation** remained the party's core identity. The flagship proposal made the first **5,000 DKK per month tax-free** for all workers, at a cost of roughly 20 billion DKK. Beyond that, LA proposed abolishing the toptopskat and mellemskat (middle bracket tax), reducing corporate tax from 22% to 18%, cutting the high-rate capital gains tax from 42% to 34%, and capping marginal tax at 50%. The total package: **47.7 billion DKK in tax relief** (net cost 40.3 billion after dynamic effects), financed through eliminating 19,000 state administration positions, converting graduate-level SU to loans, cutting development aid, and scrapping various tax deductions. Arbejderbevægelsens Erhvervsråd calculated the plan would deliver average relief of 30,801 DKK in wealthy Hørsholm versus 2,728 DKK in Lolland — a distributional profile LA did not contest but framed as the natural result of cutting marginal rates.

On **wealth tax** (formueskat), LA drew its hardest policy line. Vanopslagh called Socialdemokratiet's proposal to reintroduce it an attempt to "build a country on pettiness and envy," citing a Dansk Industri analysis claiming it could cost the economy 16 billion DKK — though DR fact-checkers and Princeton professor Henrik Kleven questioned the evidence base. Together with V's Troels Lund Poulsen and K's Mona Juul, Vanopslagh ran a coordinated blue-bloc campaign against the wealth tax.

**Nuclear energy** became LA's most distinctive policy. The party proposed building **6 to 8 Small Modular Reactors** within 10–15 years at a cost of **120 billion DKK**, with proposed sites at Studstrup (near Aarhus) and Asnæs (near Kalundborg). The controversial financing plan redirected 55 billion DKK from offshore wind tenders and 61 billion from the Bornholm Energy Island project. LA also proposed allowing nuclear weapons on Danish soil as part of a European nuclear deterrence umbrella — a position aligned with its broader defense plan to raise military spending to **3.5% of GDP**, financed primarily through welfare cuts including halving the dagpenge (unemployment benefit) period from two years to one.

On **immigration**, LA's 2026 platform proposed ending spontaneous asylum entirely, with protection offered only through quota refugees. The party called for banning prayer rooms in educational institutions and expelling criminal foreigners on temporary permits for "any criminal offense." Legal experts from Aarhus and Aalborg universities warned these proposals would violate the European Convention on Human Rights and the UN Refugee Convention; LA's immigration spokesperson Steffen Larsen acknowledged this, saying the party sought to reform the conventions.

No formal pre-election cooperation agreement (forståelsespapir) existed between LA and any other party. The fragmented blue bloc landscape precluded such arrangements.

## Election night revealed a pragmatist behind the rhetoric

The hours between the 20:00 exit polls and the final overnight count on 24 March 2026 compressed what might normally take days of political recalibration into a single dramatic evening. The shift in LA's positioning was swift and revealing.

At 20:00, both DR and TV2 exit polls showed LA as the **largest blue bloc party** (DR: 10.5%/18 seats; TV2: 10%/17 seats). Jubilation erupted at LA's valgfest at Moltkes Palæ. LA's political spokesperson Sólbjørg Jakobsen declared it "completely natural that Alex is kongelig undersøger" if LA was the largest blue party. Ole Birk Olesen was "speechless." But by the early morning hours, the actual count reversed the picture: Venstre edged ahead at **10.1% (18 seats)** to LA's **9.4% (16 seats)**. The cocaine scandal — Vanopslagh's admission during the campaign of having used cocaine "1–2 times" while party leader — likely cost the party the 1–2 percentage points that would have made the symbolic difference.

Vanopslagh's response was immediate and disciplined. He dropped the PM claim without drama, stating: "No, I cannot become PM on red mandates. What is important now is to explore the possibility of a center-right government." He endorsed Troels Lund Poulsen to lead negotiations and pivoted to his core post-election message: building a "bred fælles front mod Mette Frederiksen" (broad common front against Mette Frederiksen).

The most significant post-election signal was LA's **active courtship of Moderaterne**. Vanopslagh declared on election night: "I can offer Lars Løkke that we won't have a Social Democratic prime minister." He sharply attacked DF's Morten Messerschmidt for refusing to cooperate with Løkke, arguing: "If Morten Messerschmidt is not ready to cooperate with Lars Løkke Rasmussen, then he gives four more years to Mette Frederiksen." This positioned LA as the most constructive force in blue bloc — the party willing to bridge the gap between traditional blue parties and Løkke's centrist project.

By the morning of 25 March, Jakobsen confirmed on TV2 that LA was **deliberating internally** whether to point to Troels Lund Poulsen or Lars Løkke Rasmussen as kongelig undersøger in the forthcoming kongerunde. This deliberation itself was significant: it signaled LA was open to Løkke leading government formation, not just participating in a blue-led process. The fact that this would be the first kongerunde under King Frederik (replacing the dronningerunde under Queen Margrethe) added procedural novelty to an already complex political puzzle.

| Dimension | Pre-election position | Post-election signal |
|---|---|---|
| PM candidacy | "If LA is largest blue party, I'm ready" | Dropped; backed Troels Lund Poulsen |
| Moderaterne | Open to "blå midterregering" concept | Actively courting Løkke; criticized DF for blocking him |
| Kongelig undersøger | Largest blue party should lead | Deliberating between TLP and Løkke |

## Coalition math and the question nobody can answer

LA's coalition preferences follow a clear hierarchy, but the parliamentary arithmetic frustrates every option. The party's **first preference** is a pure blue government (V + LA + KF + DD + DF + BP), a position the party website articulated as: "Vi er blå før valget. Vi er blå under valget. Og vi er blå efter valget." But blue bloc's 77 seats are 13 short. LA's **second preference** — embraced enthusiastically on election night — is a "blå midterregering" adding Moderaterne's 14 seats to a blue core, which would give exactly 91 mandates. Vanopslagh endorsed this concept on Facebook in response to Troels Lund Poulsen's proposal: "Det lyder godt! Vi er helt på linje her. Alt for at komme videre fra Mette. Vi kører på!"

The problem is internal blue bloc coherence. DF's Messerschmidt absolutely refuses Løkke in government ("we will eat him and his people every single day") and demands net Muslim emigration — conditions Vanopslagh declined to match. Even if DF were bypassed, V + LA + KF + M totals only **61 seats**, far short of 90. Adding DD (10) brings it to 71 — still insufficient without DF's 16. The arithmetic points stubbornly toward either a very broad and unstable blue-centre constellation or a red-centrist alternative.

LA's **hard veto** is governing with Socialdemokratiet. Vanopslagh has never signaled any flexibility on this point, and it was maintained explicitly on election night. This rules out any SVM-style cross-bloc government including LA. On **policy red lines**, the wealth tax (formueskat) is a firm dealbreaker, as is any arrangement preserving the toptopskat. These positions are central to LA's identity and would be extremely costly to abandon.

The question of whether LA would accept **Løkke as prime minister** remains deliberately ambiguous. When asked directly before the election, Vanopslagh deflected: "Mit udenomssvar er jo også et svar" (my non-answer is also an answer). He refused to offer the PM post to Løkke but also refused to rule it out. Post-election, LA's deliberation on whether to point to Løkke or TLP as formateur suggests this remains a live option — though publicly, LA prefers to frame any Løkke involvement as M joining a blue-led government rather than the reverse.

## What experts say: pragmatist playing the 2030 game

The expert consensus on Vanopslagh is remarkably unified: **he is substantially more pragmatic than his rhetoric suggests**, and his strategic horizon extends beyond 2026. DR political analyst Jens Ringberg described LA's positioning as that of an "indflydelsesparti" — "a party that raises the liberal banner very high — but not so high that you can't get through the door to the negotiating rooms at Slotsholmen." Altinget senior consultant Malene Møller Hall characterized the transformation under Vanopslagh as moving "from tree-climbing and threats to vote against their own finance bill to pragmatism, cooperativeness, and forbearance toward friends in blue bloc."

The election night behavior confirmed this assessment. **Three moments** revealed the pragmatist behind the principled exterior. First, the instant dropping of the PM claim when results shifted — no sulking, no ultimatums, just a clean pivot. Second, the active embrace of Moderaterne cooperation, positioning LA as the bridge between traditional blue parties and Løkke's centre. Third, the directed attack on Messerschmidt rather than on other blue competitors — Vanopslagh chose to be the unity candidate, not the factional fighter.

Hans Engell's election-night prediction of an **S + SF + M + RV government** (supported by Enhedslisten and Alternativet) represents the most prominent scenario in which LA would be excluded entirely. However, other analysts noted that this scenario requires Løkke to accept dependence on Enhedslisten mandates — something he has explicitly rejected. Pernille Skipper, political commentator on TV2, flagged this as the "camel" Løkke would have to "swallow." NordiskPost's Riccardo Sala concluded on 25 March that "the next government will only emerge through difficult negotiations at the centre of parliament," with no easy path for any constellation.

On **red line credibility**, the evidence is nuanced. LA's veto on governing with Socialdemokratiet appears genuinely binding — it is so central to the party's identity that breaking it would be electorally catastrophic. The wealth tax opposition is similarly firm. But the PM candidacy claim was transparently conditional, and LA's tax demands — while ambitious — were deliberately designed in 2024 to be "achievable in one term" rather than maximalist. This suggests **LA's economic red lines are negotiating positions with room for movement**, while its coalition vetoes are structural constraints.

Netavisen Pio's pre-election analysis argued Vanopslagh's real target is **2030**: "lay the foundation for next time — get Venstre back in the conservative bloc and become clearly the largest conservative party." At 34, Vanopslagh has time. His 2026 performance — record result, disciplined post-election behavior, cooperative posture — builds credibility for an eventual PM candidacy even if the immediate arithmetic keeps LA in opposition.

## The 16-versus-18 question and what comes next

The slim gap between LA's 16 seats and Venstre's 18 is **symbolically significant but strategically temporary**. Venstre's 10.1% was its worst-ever result; LA's 9.4% was its best ever. The trendlines point toward convergence or reversal. During the campaign, Claus Hjort Frederiksen (former V defense minister) publicly named Vanopslagh as blue bloc's natural PM candidate — a remarkable endorsement from within Venstre itself. Konservative's Mona Juul lamented that blue bloc couldn't unite behind a single candidate, with Vanopslagh, Troels Lund Poulsen, and Lars Boje Mathiesen all declaring.

Vanopslagh handled the PM question with calculated ambiguity, always hedging: "It would be rather absurd if I said only I would do as PM candidate." His pragmatic concession on election night — backing TLP immediately once V was confirmed as largest — earned goodwill rather than burning bridges. A Voxmeter poll from 5–7 March had shown Vanopslagh as the **second-most-preferred PM** at 10.1%, ahead of Troels Lund Poulsen at 8.6% (though well behind Frederiksen's 29.9%), suggesting his PM ambitions had broader public resonance than the final vote share reflected.

The party's internal discipline remains a competitive advantage. Unlike Venstre's serial leadership crises or Moderaterne's scandals, LA has maintained remarkable cohesion. The only visible crack was candidate Martin Mickey Pedersen's public criticism of Vanopslagh during the cocaine scandal, which resulted in swift expulsion — a signal that dissent carries consequences.

## Conclusion

Liberal Alliance in March 2026 sits at the intersection of its strongest-ever electoral position and a parliamentary arithmetic that may render that strength irrelevant. The party's evolution from libertarian protest vehicle to pragmatic, culturally conservative governing aspirant is Vanopslagh's central achievement — but the math demands either Løkke's cooperation (which requires tolerating M's centrism) or opposition under a red-led government. The critical post-election signal is not what LA demands but what it has stopped demanding: the PM post, a pure blue majority, and the exclusion of Moderaterne. What remains firm — no Socialdemokratiet in government, no wealth tax, no toptopskat — defines the space within which Vanopslagh will negotiate. Whether that space is large enough to build a government depends almost entirely on Lars Løkke Rasmussen, who holds the 14 mandates that neither bloc can govern without. Vanopslagh appears to understand this reality clearly, and his post-election behavior suggests a leader willing to pay the price of pragmatism today for the credibility it buys tomorrow.
