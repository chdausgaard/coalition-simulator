# Socialdemokratiet in the 2026 government formation: qualitative positioning brief

*Revised 25 March 2026. Corrections applied to kongelig undersøger timeline, 2022/2026 disambiguation, and étpartiregering statement sequencing.*

---

## Overview

Mette Frederiksen enters the post-election period from a paradoxical position: **Socialdemokratiet posted its worst result since 1903** (21.9%, 38 seats — down 12 from 50 in 2022), yet she is widely expected to be appointed *kongelig undersøger* — the person tasked with leading government formation negotiations.

**Status as of 25 March 2026 (morning/midday):** The kongerunde has not yet concluded and no kongelig undersøger has been appointed. DR's political correspondent Christine Cordsen described it as "very unusual" that no clear undersøger candidate had emerged by the morning after the election, and RV's Samira Nawa called the situation "strange." Parties are expected to visit King Frederik X at Amalienborg in the course of today to deliver their sedler indicating whom they support as forhandlingsleder. All three former SVM party leaders have put themselves forward: Frederiksen, Venstre's Troels Lund Poulsen (who wants the PM post), and Moderaterne's Lars Løkke Rasmussen (who declared his candidacy for the undersøger role in a Politiken interview on 23 March, while acknowledging the PM post itself was unrealistic for him).

Based on election-night declarations, the mandate arithmetic strongly favors Frederiksen: S, SF, and (conditionally) Enhedslisten have indicated support for her; the entire blue bloc points to Troels Lund Poulsen; and Moderaterne's position remains to be formalized. But the outcome is **not yet confirmed**, and some parties (notably Radikale Venstre and Enhedslisten) were still deliberating as of election night, with Dragsted (EL) setting conditions and Lidegaard (RV) saying he'd answer "tomorrow."

**A note on 2022 vs. 2026:** Frederiksen was *kongelig undersøger* after the November 2022 election, when she formed the SVM government after Moderaterne and the red parties pointed to her (103 mandates vs. 58 for Ellemann-Jensen). Several background sources on the institution's procedures reference the 2022 process as their primary illustration — the Lex.dk entry on "kongelig undersøger" uses a photo of Løkke leaving Amalienborg after the 2022 kongerunde. The 2026 process is distinct: it is the first conducted under King Frederik X (following the January 2024 tronskifte), and the situation is far more uncertain than 2022 given the fragmented result and three competing candidates.

**Why Frederiksen is the likely outcome regardless:** The red bloc's 84-seat lead over the blue bloc's 77 makes Moderaterne's 14 seats sufficient to give a Frederiksen-led coalition a comfortable majority of 98, while a blue-bloc-plus-M arrangement would barely scratch 91. Structural arithmetic, not electoral strength, is her leverage. If appointed, formal negotiations would likely begin Friday 27 March, with parties invited in order of electoral size. The days ahead will test whether Frederiksen can translate procedural advantage into a durable government — and whether the scars of three years governing with Venstre have permanently reshaped her party.

## 1. Ideological positioning during the SVM years (2022–2026)

The cross-bloc SVM government (S + Venstre + Moderaterne), formed in December 2022, was a historically unusual experiment — the first S-V coalition in over 40 years. It delivered an extraordinary legislative output: **201 political agreements**, 182 of which crossed the political center. Major achievements included the world's first CO₂ tax on agricultural emissions (the Green Tripartite, June 2024), a transformative healthcare reform merging two regions (November 2024), and a defense buildup that pushed Denmark's military spending commitment to **3.5% of GDP by 2030**, well ahead of NATO's timeline.

Yet voters punished all three governing parties. The defining wound was the abolition of Store Bededag (Great Prayer Day), voted through in February 2023, which generated Denmark's largest-ever petition (477,441 signatures) and prompted trade union branches in Jutland to boycott Socialdemokratiet from May Day celebrations — an unprecedented rupture in the century-old party-union alliance. Each coalition partner denied proposing the holiday's removal; Finance Ministry documents strongly implicated S. **70% of Danes opposed the abolition**, and it remained toxic through the 2026 campaign, with all parties eventually signaling willingness to revisit it.

The electoral cost cascaded through subsequent elections. In the June 2024 European elections, S collapsed from 27.5% to **15.6%**, losing half its support. SF surged past them. In November 2025 municipal elections, S lost control of **18 of 44 municipalities**, including Copenhagen, where the party managed just 12.7%. By December 2025, S polled at roughly 17%. The Greenland crisis — Trump's renewed claims in January 2025 and Frederiksen's resolute response — temporarily restored her "strong leader" image and boosted polls to ~21%, creating the window for a snap election call on 26 February 2026.

Ideologically, the SVM period pulled S toward the economic center. Store Bededag was fundamentally a supply-side labor market reform. Tax reform and public-sector deregulation reflected compromise with Venstre. But S maintained hard-line positions on immigration (pursuing third-country asylum processing) and defense (donating "our entire artillery" to Ukraine, per Frederiksen at Munich 2024). The net effect, as Jacobin assessed in November 2025, was that the left-nationalist formula that won in 2019 and 2022 was diluted by governing with Venstre. The party hemorrhaged left-flank voters to SF without proportional gains from the center.

## 2. Election campaign positions

### Taxation (formueskat)

Frederiksen launched the 2026 campaign with a sharp leftward pivot on economics, clearly designed to recapture voters lost to SF. The centerpiece was a **formueskat (wealth tax)**: a 0.5% annual levy on individual wealth exceeding 25 million DKK (50 million for couples), affecting approximately 22,000 Danes — the richest ~1%. Revenue was projected at 6–7 billion DKK annually and earmarked for a flagship education proposal dubbed "Lilleskolen": a cap of **14 students per class** in grades 0–3, funded by 5 billion DKK and 4,000 new teachers. A Jyllands-Posten analyst called it a tactical stroke of genius.

The business community revolted. Fifteen industry organizations launched a joint campaign against it. Dansk Industri estimated a **16-billion-DKK GDP hit**. Vestas CEO Henrik Andersen threatened to leave Denmark. Moderaterne's Løkke dismissed it as driven by envy. But S's flexibility was signaled early: Frederiksen told Jyllands-Posten mid-campaign that "it is not a goal in itself to get a wealth tax" — a clear indicator this was a negotiating chip, not a red line. DR also reported that S took weeks into the campaign to clarify the proposal's details, reinforcing the impression it was more positioning than policy.

### Pensions

S attempted to replicate the success of the 2019 "Arne-pension" campaign by proposing to **slow the automatic rise in the retirement age** (from 1 year per 5 years to 0.5 years per 5), enhance early-retirement benefits by 3,000 DKK/month, and freeze the minimum early pension age at 66. The employers' confederation DA called it an attack on the economy's foundation, but S argued unused Arne-pension allocations made it fiscally sustainable. AE (Arbejderbevægelsens Erhvervsråd) published a supportive analysis.

### Welfare: ældrecheck and lilleskole

A joint S+SF *ældrecheck* proposal on 12 March raised asset thresholds and boosted the poorest pensioners' income by 1,000 DKK/month. This served as both policy and signal, marking the **first formal S+SF cooperation of the campaign** and foreshadowing a potential governing partnership. The lilleskole proposal (smaller class sizes) attracted support from Danmarks Lærerforening, who endorsed the direction while questioning whether adequate funding would follow.

### Immigration

S continued and escalated its longstanding strict line. An 18-point *udlændingeudspil* included proposals for administrative (non-judicial) deportation of criminal foreigners, healthcare bans for those who assault medical staff, and tighter restrictions on third-country labor immigration. Frederiksen declared immigration policy *ultimativt* — literally non-negotiable. This drew legal criticism (Professor Eva Smith warned of European Convention on Human Rights violations) and political attacks from Enhedslisten's Pelle Dragsted, who argued Danish Social Democrats needlessly copy the right's immigration playbook. An Altinget commentary noted that the Norwegian election showed social democrats could win without adopting far-right immigration positions. Yet it positioned S to compete with Dansk Folkeparti, which had surged from 2.6% to 9.1% on a "remigration" platform.

### Green/climate policy and the sprøjteforbud

The drinking-water crisis emerged as an unexpected defining issue. Environment Minister Magnus Heunicke (S) accused coalition partner Venstre of obstructing groundwater protection during SVM, and Frederiksen made a national pesticide ban (*sprøjteforbud*) over vulnerable aquifer zones an **"ultimativt krav"** in the final TV2 debate on 23 March — the evening before the election. This was one of very few issues where she used explicitly ultimative language.

## 3. Coalition preferences and collaboration signals

### Pre-election: maximum ambiguity

At the valgudskrivelse on 26 February, Frederiksen stated from the Folketingets talerstol that she could imagine both a classic "red" government and a government across the center. This kept every configuration on the table throughout the campaign.

### Election night (24–25 March): narrowing the field

Frederiksen's election night speech, delivered around 1 AM on 25 March to chanting supporters at Christiansborg, struck a deliberately open tone. She acknowledged the SVM government "was never popular" and thanked departing coalition partners. Her key message: that forming a government would not be easy, and that parties would need to bend toward each other.

She then narrowed the field with a critical observation: she noted that there appeared to be no path to a blue government with Løkke, and that the question now was what the red parties and Løkke would say. This framing — red parties *plus* Løkke — already pointed toward her preferred architecture.

### The étpartiregering anchor

Frederiksen has stated her aim to form a **socialdemokratisk étpartiregering** (single-party S minority government), calling it "the most maneuverable government Denmark can have." This represented a notable narrowing from her pre-election openness to multiple configurations. TV2 reported her as foreseeing difficult negotiations ahead. *(Note: the exact timing of this statement — whether it came during or after the kongerunde — should be confirmed as events unfold.)*

The *étpartiregering* framing is best understood as a tactical anchor rather than an expected outcome. A single-party S government of 38 seats would need extensive support, and no configuration of potential backers aligns cleanly. The anchor allows Frederiksen to negotiate from a position of apparent modesty — "I'm not asking for much" — while keeping every coalition format on the table.

### Red lines and vetoes

S has not publicly vetoed any party from potential support arrangements. Frederiksen's stated "ultimative" issues are immigration and the sprøjteforbud — but these are areas where broad parliamentary support exists or where S's position aligns with potential partners anyway.

### Stance on repeating SVM

Venstre's Troels Lund Poulsen firmly refused to re-enter government with Frederiksen, stating his options are a blue centre-right government or opposition. This effectively kills SVM Mark II from V's side — a convenient constraint that allows Frederiksen to claim she "tried broad" without actually having to deliver it.

## 4. Formateur dynamics

### The statsminister question: non-negotiable

Frederiksen has made exactly one absolute commitment: **she will not join any government she does not lead as statsminister**. This was stated on the DR Genstart podcast during the campaign and is universally assessed by commentators as credible and binding. Bookmakers priced her as heavy favorite (odds 1.15–1.30) on election eve.

### Løkke's role

Her relationship with the kingmaker — Løkke and Moderaterne — is the negotiation's central axis. Løkke's pre-election bid to serve as *kongelig undersøger* himself was swatted down by Frederiksen (she characterized it as an expression of his desire to be PM again) and by S finance minister Nicolai Wammen's pointed Facebook critique. Yet on election night, **Moderaterne ultimately pointed to Frederiksen**, not Løkke — a pragmatic concession that simultaneously acknowledged arithmetic reality and preserved M's negotiating position.

Løkke's election-night appeal — urging parties to come down from their positions — and his insistence that M will not sit in a government dependent on the far left or far right sets up the core tension. Any S-led government including M that relies on Enhedslisten's 11 seats for its majority would violate Løkke's stated condition.

M's Jakob Engel-Schmidt acknowledged that M lacks the weight to claim the PM post, saying a PM party should have "a certain weight." But M's 14 seats are indispensable. The price of M's participation will likely be substantive policy concessions (no wealth tax, pension moderation) and possibly a prominent ministerial portfolio for Løkke.

## 5. Commentator and expert assessments

### Most likely scenario

Veteran commentator Hans Engell assessed on election night that the most probable outcome is a government consisting of **S + SF + Moderaterne + Radikale Venstre**, with Enhedslisten and Alternativet as external support parties. This configuration would place roughly 81 seats in government with ~96–98 in total support. Oddset (the state bookmaker) priced this scenario at **odds 2.40**, making it the clear favorite.

### The Løkke contradiction

Former Enhedslisten leader Pernille Skipper, now a TV2 commentator, identified the core obstacle: Løkke's stated refusal to sit in a government dependent on far-left mandates is directly violated by this scenario, since the government would structurally depend on Enhedslisten's tolerance for its majority. Whether this contradiction is resolved through creative institutional design (a formal *støtteparti* agreement with EL), through Løkke quietly abandoning his stated position, or through an entirely different configuration will define the negotiations.

### Pressure from the left

Enhedslisten and SF are applying divergent but complementary pressure. Pelle Dragsted (EL) stated that Enhedslisten would only point to a forhandlingsleder who wants a red government, and that he hadn't heard Frederiksen say she wanted that. Enhedslisten demanded S explore a "red government" as a precondition for support. SF's Pia Olsen Dyhr went further on institutional terms: demanding formal coalition membership, not just *støtteparti* status — "Either we're in government, or we're in opposition." The left parties' 2026 electoral success (SF at ~20 seats, its best result in decades) gives this pressure real weight.

### Frederiksen's negotiating style

DR's pre-election analysis described her as maintaining "top-down control" for a decade, with a willingness to make surprising ideological pivots — SVM itself being the most dramatic example. Altinget's coverage suggested S's campaign for a broad government was partly motivated by the procedural advantage it confers: as long as Frederiksen avoids a fresh kongerunde, she sits at the head of the table.

### Red line credibility assessment

- **PM demand:** Ironclad. No analyst questions it.
- **Immigration ultimatum:** Genuine but largely untested, since potential partners mostly agree or accept S's line as the price of government.
- **Sprøjteforbud:** Broad popular support. Likely to survive negotiations.
- **Formueskat:** Negotiable. Frederiksen signaled this herself.
- **Pension reform:** Faces strong opposition from Moderaterne (Løkke flatly disagreed) and will likely be scaled back significantly.

## 6. Party-specific addendum: Frederiksen's positioning

### The statsminister question: framed as non-negotiable

Frederiksen has framed the PM question as a settled matter, not a bargaining chip. She is running for a third term and has not signaled any willingness to serve under another PM or step aside. This is treated as the single binding constraint in her negotiating position.

### Red government vs. broad midterregering

Frederiksen has kept her options open, but the SVM experience and the post-election sequence reveal a clear preference hierarchy:

1. **Optimal:** An S-led minority government (possibly with SF and/or RV as formal coalition partners), with Moderaterne providing external support. This gives her a left-wing governing identity while retaining centrist votes through M's involvement.
2. **Acceptable:** A broader S+SF+M+RV coalition — the scenario most commentators consider likeliest, but one that requires managing the Løkke contradiction described above.
3. **Worst:** Another SVM-style arrangement with Venstre, which the party base and trade unions would view as a betrayal of the election's message. Venstre's refusal makes this near-impossible anyway.

### How SVM shaped her approach

The three-year SVM experiment delivered policy substance but electoral devastation. Frederiksen's public framing is that "it wears you out to take responsibility" — positioning S's losses as the inevitable cost of governing through crises. The private lesson, visible in her post-election *étpartiregering* preference, is that **formal coalition with ideologically distant parties destroys the party's brand**. S lost roughly 15–16% of its 2022 voters to SF alone (the single largest voter shift, per YouGov), driven by welfare and climate concerns that the SVM framework prevented S from addressing with sufficient left-wing credibility.

### Longer-term question

TV2 political editor Hans Redder raised whether this may be Frederiksen's final election cycle, comparing her trajectory to Anders Fogh Rasmussen's mid-term departure for NATO. After nearly seven years as PM, a historic electoral decline, and the physical toll she referenced on election night, a scenario in which Frederiksen secures a government, serves two to three years, and passes the baton to a successor (Wammen, Heunicke, or another) cannot be dismissed.

---

## Conclusion: arithmetic trumps mandate, but the coalition's shape remains contested

The central insight is the **disconnect between Socialdemokratiet's electoral weakness and its structural strength**. The 84-77-14 mandate distribution gives Frederiksen leverage that her 21.9% vote share alone would not justify. She controls the formation process, commands the largest single party, and faces a blue bloc that cannot assemble a majority even with Moderaterne's support unless every seat holds.

The most likely trajectory leads toward an **S+SF+M(+RV) government**, but the path there requires resolving a genuine contradiction: Løkke's refusal to depend on far-left mandates versus the mathematical reality that any government including both S and M will, by definition, rely on Enhedslisten's tolerance. Whether this contradiction is resolved through creative institutional design, through Løkke quietly abandoning his stated position, or through an entirely different configuration will determine the shape of Denmark's next government. Frederiksen has positioned herself to be the one asking the questions rather than answering them — which, in Danish government formation, is most of the battle.
