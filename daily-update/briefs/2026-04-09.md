# Denmark's coalition talks resume with Løkke holding all the cards

Two weeks after Denmark's March 24 election, the government formation entered a decisive phase as negotiations resumed post-Easter with **no viable coalition in sight**. The April 6–9 window revealed a pattern of asymmetric concessions: the red bloc parties are systematically softening their positions to court Moderaterne's **14 kingmaker seats**, while Lars Løkke Rasmussen maintains his demands largely intact and keeps both doors open. The most consequential signals from this period are SF's retreat from formueskat, Frederiksen's reduction of her red lines to just two issues, and Løkke's continued insistence that a center-left government cannot deliver sufficiently "offensive" economic policy. Meanwhile, Trump's renewed Greenland rhetoric, blue-bloc fragmentation, and Venstre leader Troels Lund Poulsen's emergency hospitalization are all tilting leverage toward Frederiksen — even as Løkke publicly declares that things "still look heavy."

---

## April 6: Easter ends and the pressure builds at Marienborg

**Negotiations resumed on Easter Monday** after a four-day pause (April 2–5). Mette Frederiksen convened meetings at Marienborg with SF (Pia Olsen Dyhr), Radikale Venstre (Martin Lidegaard, Samira Nawa), and Moderaterne (Lars Løkke Rasmussen, Jakob Engel-Schmidt). Crucially, **Venstre and Konservative confirmed they were not invited**, signaling Frederiksen's continued focus on a center-left-plus-Moderaterne track.

**Martin Lidegaard** (RV) delivered the most substantive public remarks of the day, framing urgency around geopolitics: *"At alle partier — der har tænkt sig at være en del af den kommende regering og det flertal, der skal stå bag — skynder os så meget, vi kan, så vi kan komme på plads."* He added a line that neatly summarized the bilateral challenge at the heart of the formation: *"Jeg tror, at det er lettere at gøre de røde økonomisk ansvarlige end at gøre de blå grønt ansvarlige."* He described the day's talks as having stretches of agreement (*"i lange stræk har der været enighed"*) and expressed hope the process would not take "many weeks."

**Pia Olsen Dyhr** (SF) characterized the discussions as **"positive"** but offered no specifics. Løkke avoided the press entirely — a notable contrast to Olsen Dyhr and Lidegaard, who both spoke briefly to journalists.

**Frederiksen posted on Instagram**, striking a pragmatic tone: *"Ingen har et ønske om at gøre Danmark fattigere. Tværtimod. Men ideen om, hvordan vi fastholder vores sunde økonomi og høje beskæftigelse har forskellige ansigter."* She urged parties to meet each other *"pragmatisk og med det klare mål at håndtere både problemerne her og nu."*

Three contextual developments reshaped the landscape around the resumed talks:

**Troels Lund Poulsen's emergency surgery.** The Venstre leader announced via Facebook on April 6 that he had undergone acute eye surgery on Saturday evening (April 4): *"Lørdag tog min påske desværre noget af en drejning. Jeg blev ramt af en øjensygdom, som gjorde, at jeg blev akut opereret lørdag aften... operationen betyder, at jeg skal holde mig i ro de næste syv til ti dage."* Venstre confirmed he would not participate in any negotiations during this period. With V's leader physically sidelined — and his party reeling from its worst-ever result (**10.1%**) — Venstre's capacity to shape events during the critical post-Easter week was severely diminished.

**Blue bloc fragmentation continued.** Borgernes Parti's Emilie Schytte departed on April 4, the second BP member to leave in 11 days (after Jacob Harris's expulsion March 28). Schytte's Facebook statement was scathing: *"Det, der blev præsenteret som gennemsigtighed, ærlighed og et opgør med magtcentrering, viser sig i praksis at være et pyramidespil af hykleri og magtfuldkommenhed."* Combined with Cecilie Liv Hansen's earlier expulsion from Liberal Alliance, blue bloc had now lost **three mandates to independents**, making a blue-bloc majority arithmetically impossible.

**Polling reinforced the center-gravity pull.** A Voxmeter poll showed DF surging to **10.3%**, making it the largest blue party — a development that further toxified blue-bloc arithmetic, since both DF and Moderaterne refuse to cooperate with each other.

TV 2 political commentator Helle Ib captured the strategic picture: *"De eksempler, som vi har set med dramatik og eksklusioner fra LA og Borgernes Parti, alt andet lige taler for, at Lars Løkkes lyst til at hoppe helt over i blå blok må ligge på et meget, meget lille sted."*

## April 7: The pivotal day — four hours with Løkke yield no breakthrough

Tuesday April 7 was the most consequential day in the window. Negotiations moved to Statsministeriet with a full schedule: Enhedslisten and Alternativet from 09:00, then Moderaterne from 14:00.

### Morning: Enhedslisten strikes a conciliatory tone

**Pelle Dragsted** (Enhedslisten) arrived with a deliberate message of openness toward Moderaterne: *"Det er ikke os, der udelukker at forhandle med Løkke."* He invoked geopolitical urgency — *"Vi får en regering på plads hurtigt"* — and even praised Løkke's handling of the Greenland situation. His key appeal: *"Men det kræver, at vi alle sammen, og det gælder også Lars Løkke, viser en vilje til kompromis og en vilje til at bøje os mod hinanden."*

After the meeting, Dragsted was **"optimistisk"**, reporting consensus on drinking water and nature policy: *"Forskellene er ikke så store, og der er en fælles vilje til at få gjort det, som flere generationer har negligeret: At få reddet vores land og genrejst vores natur."* He identified only **one ultimative krav** — that inequality must be addressed: *"At der skal gøres op med den voksende ulighed i Danmark."* Notably, he signaled that a blue alternative is unrealistic: *"Jeg hører ikke nogen stor vilje — hverken hos Venstre eller De Konservative — til at gøre Mette Frederiksen til statsminister eller for den sags skyld Lars Løkke til udenrigsminister."*

**Franciska Rosenkilde** (Alternativet) was briefer but positive, calling it *"et godt møde"* focused on green policy and expressing confidence that parties could agree on economic policy.

### Frederiksen narrows her red lines to just two

Frederiksen's midday press statement contained perhaps the week's most significant strategic signal. She reported progress — *"der begynder at tegne sig noget rigtigt interessant i forhold til drikkevand og folkeskolen"* — while tempering expectations: *"Den er i hvert fald ikke på trapperne, og jeg ved heller ikke, om den kan dannes endnu."*

The critical revelation was the narrowness of her stated ultimatums. She named only **two non-negotiable demands: stram udlændingepolitik and et nationalt sprøjteforbud**. TV 2 political editor Hans Redder immediately noted the significance of what was **absent** — formueskat, the folkeskoleudspil, Arne-pension extension: *"Det er i virkeligheden de eneste to altafgørende og centrale punkter for Mette Frederiksen... Jeg tror, at det er en Mette Frederiksen, som begynder at gøre vælgerne bevidste om og klar på, at når der bliver dannet en regering, så har Socialdemokratiet været nødt til at strege store dele af den politik, de gik til på valg på."*

On international affairs, Frederiksen flagged the Iran/Middle East crisis's domestic impact: *"Det kan mærkes herhjemme på priserne på benzin, olie og diesel. Virksomheder begynder i stigende grad at kunne mærke det."* She reported *"politisk velvilje"* to address energy-cost concerns — potentially shared ground with Moderaterne.

### SF makes its biggest concession: formueskat is negotiable

In what may be the single most consequential coalition signal of the week, **Pia Olsen Dyhr** declared SF's willingness to abandon its wealth tax demand: *"Det er SF-politik at indføre en formueskat, men når vi skal forhandle regering, så er vi også villige til at kigge på andre finansieringskilder."* Formueskat had been one of Moderaterne's explicit dealbreakers. SF's retreat removed what Løkke had previously called a **"no-go"** from the table, substantially narrowing the policy distance between the would-be coalition partners.

### Afternoon: Løkke's four hours end with skepticism intact

Lars Løkke Rasmussen arrived at Statsministeriet at 14:00 and entered through a different door than other party delegations. **Approximately four hours of negotiations** followed — the longest session of the post-Easter period.

Løkke emerged with his skepticism publicly intact. His core statement: *"Det ser stadigvæk tungt ud, sådan som vi bedømmer situationen."* He elaborated on M's concerns: *"Når Mette Frederiksen afsøger en rød regering, så er vi bekymrede. Hvis vi skal videre den vej, så skal vi have forsikring om, at sådan en regering kan føre en politik, der gør Danmark rigere og øger arbejdsudbuddet."* He questioned the mandate itself: *"Vi er fortsat usikre på, om den kongelige undersøger med dette mandat kan føre en tilstrækkelig offensiv økonomisk politik."*

Løkke reiterated his preferred outcome — *"Det bedste er at få en regering hen over midten"* — and again urged Venstre and Konservative to *"træde i karakter."* He confirmed M would return for negotiations on **Friday** (April 10 or 11, sources vary).

Bloomberg's Sanne Wass reported the same day that Frederiksen had entered the third week of talks with a **"stronger hand"** due to Greenland pressure and rival-party turmoil. Trump had reiterated his desire to take control of Greenland on April 6, framing the dispute as the basis for his dissatisfaction with NATO.

TV 2 analyst Camilla Stampe framed the core strategic question: *"Vil Løkke trække stikket på en rød regering med Moderaterne, hvor Enhedslisten er støtteparti, så han i stedet kan få afsøgt muligheden for at få dannet en udvidet SVM-regering?"*

## April 8: A pause for analysis as Greenland raises the stakes

No formal negotiations took place on Wednesday. DR published a summary under the headline **"Løkke efter fire timers forhandlinger: Ser stadigvæk tungt ud"**, noting that *"to uger efter folketingsvalget er det fortsat svært at se en ny regering i horisonten."* A Ritzau analysis circulated widely under the headline *"Ny regering kan tage ugevis på grund af store knaster i forhandlinger,"* identifying economic policy as the primary deadlock.

**Greenland's demands became public.** ArcticToday (Elías Thorsson, April 8) published the formal wish list presented by Greenland's two new Folketing members — **Naaja H. Nathanielsen** (Inuit Ataqatigiit) and **Qarsoq Høegh-Dam** (Naleraq) — to Frederiksen. Their five demands included constitutional amendment for genuine equality between Greenland and Denmark, access to Danish cancer treatment fast-track programs, a larger investment agreement beyond the **1.6 billion kroner** framework signed September 2025, a greater share for Greenlandic businesses in investment projects, and expanded compensation for historical injustices (beyond IUD scandal victims to include legally fatherless individuals and those forcibly adopted). Both MPs stressed compensation must be resolved this election period. Nathanielsen had earlier indicated she sees no viable blue-bloc constellation: *"Lige nu har jeg også svært ved at se, hvordan konstellationen skal være, hvis det skulle være en blå regering."* The North Atlantic seats carry extra weight amid Trump's Greenland pressure.

**Alex Vanopslagh** (LA) drew fire for dismissing the drinking water issue as *"det rene hysteri"* and *"pjat"* in continuing coverage of his Weekendavisen interview. Via SMS on April 8, his press office doubled down: *"Det er ikke farligt at drikke det danske drikkevand. Det har adskillige eksperter slået fast igen og igen."* Alternativet's Rosenkilde responded: *"Jeg ved ikke, hvem Alex laver politik til, når han siger sådan."* SF's Carl Valentin called it *"uærligt og pinligt."* With a national pesticide ban being one of Frederiksen's two stated red lines, Vanopslagh's position further isolated LA from the formation process.

**Hans Engell** (TV 2 commentator) offered his most explicit prediction of the period: *"Jeg tror, at det ender med, at vi får en regering bestående af Socialdemokratiet, SF, Løkke og De Radikale. Enhedslisten og Alternativet bliver de to støttepartier."* But he hedged, noting the difficulty: *"Midt i smørhullet sidder Lars Løkke Rasmussen"* — and suggested a **new kongerunde** may become necessary, with Frederiksen receiving a broader mandate.

B.T.'s **Henrik Qvortrup** raised a provocative hypothesis: that Frederiksen might deliberately delay the formation until after **May 1** (Labour Day) to maximize pressure. He assessed that V and K could eventually rejoin a midterregering but *"risikoen er kæmpestor fra begge partier, og de risikerer et ordentligt hak i tuden af vælgerne."*

## April 9 morning: Commemoration day, no talks, eyes on Friday

April 9 — Besættelsesdagen, Denmark's annual commemoration of the 1940 German occupation — brought no formal negotiations. The day's main political event was the **Publicistklubben debate on the government formation** at 17:00 in Copenhagen, featuring analysts Elisabet Svane (Politiken), Jacob Bruun (former Løkke advisor, now Bruun Advisory), and Jakob Nielsen (Altinget editor-in-chief), moderated by DR's Pia Glud Munksgaard.

All eyes turned to **Friday April 10–11**, when Moderaterne were scheduled to return to the negotiating table, and to the **Folketinget's first post-election session on April 10 at 12:00** — where, in an ironic twist, **Lars Løkke Rasmussen** would chair proceedings as aldersformand (senior member, 32 years in parliament).

## What the three-day window reveals about coalition feasibility

The April 6–9 period crystallized a clear negotiation dynamic. The red-bloc parties are **converging toward Løkke's comfort zone**: Frederiksen stripped her platform to two ultimatums, SF abandoned formueskat, and Enhedslisten kept its demands broad while praising Løkke personally. This represents a systematic clearing of obstacles that Moderaterne had previously flagged. Yet Løkke maintained his posture of dissatisfaction, publicly questioning whether the mandate itself can deliver adequate economic policy.

Several structural factors are pushing toward resolution:

- **Blue-bloc collapse** makes Løkke's alternative increasingly theoretical. With three lost mandates and DF's rise as the largest blue party (while refusing M cooperation), a blue-bloc path requires V and K to reverse their positions — and V's leader is hospitalized.
- **Geopolitical urgency** from Trump's Greenland rhetoric and the Iran/Middle East energy crisis creates shared incentive for rapid government formation, and advantages Frederiksen as the incumbent with foreign policy credibility.
- **Public opinion** has shifted toward a cross-centre solution (42%, up 7 points), and 50% want Frederiksen as PM.

The most likely emerging scenario, as articulated by Hans Engell: **an S+SF+M+RV government** (82 seats) with Enhedslisten and Alternativet as external support parties (adding 16 seats for a combined 98). The critical remaining obstacle is economic policy — specifically, whether Frederiksen can present a framework that simultaneously satisfies Løkke's demand for supply-side reforms and Dragsted's insistence on addressing inequality. As Hans Redder noted, Frederiksen is engaged in the delicate task of *"preparing voters for the fact that Socialdemokratiet will have to cross out much of the platform they campaigned on."*

The alternative — a new kongerunde with a broader mandate, potentially reopening the door to V and K — remains plausible if the Friday meeting fails. But time pressure, international events, and the structural weakness of every alternative configuration all favor the center-left-plus-Moderaterne track that Frederiksen is currently pursuing.
