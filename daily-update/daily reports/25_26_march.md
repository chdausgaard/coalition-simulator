# Denmark's 2026 election yields no majority — Frederiksen begins fragile coalition hunt

**Mette Frederiksen was appointed formateur on 25 March after the most fragmented Danish election in modern history left neither bloc with a majority.** The red bloc won 84 seats (six short of the 90 needed), the blue bloc secured 77, and Lars Løkke Rasmussen's Moderaterne — with 14 seats — emerged as the decisive kingmaker. Frederiksen invited seven parties to initial negotiations starting 27 March, but every mathematically viable coalition requires at least one party to break its own stated red lines. Political commentators expect weeks of difficult talks, with the most likely outcome an S-SF-RV government with Moderaterne participation and external support from Enhedslisten and Alternativet.

---

## Final seat distribution: a historically fragmented Folketing

All **12 parties** on the ballot won representation — the first time this has happened since the 2% threshold was introduced in 1953. The outgoing SVM coalition was punished severely, dropping from 89 seats to just 70. Voter turnout was approximately **84%**, the lowest in over 35 years.

| Socialdemokratiet (S) | A | 38 | 50 | **−12** | 21.9% |
| SF / Socialistisk Folkeparti | F | 20 | 15 | **+5** | 11.6% |
| Venstre (V) | V | 18 | 23 | −5 | 10.1% |
| Dansk Folkeparti (DF) | O | 16 | 5 | **+11** | 9.1% |
| Liberal Alliance (LA) | I | 16 | 14 | +2 | ~9.4% |
| Moderaterne (M) | M | 14 | 16 | −2 | 7.7% |

Plus 4 North Atlantic seats: 2 Greenland (Siumut, Inuit Ataqatigiit), 2 Faroe Islands (Sambandsflokkurin, Javnaðarflokkurin). Total: **179 seats; 90 needed for majority.**

**Bloc arithmetic:**

| Bloc | Seats | Composition |
|---|---|---|
| Red bloc | **84** | S (38) + SF (20) + Ø (11) + B (10) + Å (5) |
| Blue bloc | **77** | V (18) + DF (16) + LA (16) + K (13) + DD (10) + H (4) |
| Centre / unaligned | **14** | Moderaterne (14) |

The biggest winner was **Dansk Folkeparti**, which tripled its vote share from 2.6% to 9.1% and gained 11 seats. SF surged to become the **second-largest party** with 20 seats — its best result ever. Borgernes Parti, founded by Lars Boje Mathiesen in 2024, entered parliament for the first time with 4 seats. The biggest loser was Socialdemokratiet, whose **21.9% was the party's worst result since 1903**. Venstre's 10.1% was its **worst result in its 156-year history**. Despite Frederiksen's snap election gamble — calling it on 26 February partly to capitalize on her popular stance against Trump's Greenland ambitions — voters focused overwhelmingly on domestic issues: cost of living, pensions, drinking water, and animal welfare.

## The kongerunde was completed in a single day on 25 March

This was the first kongerunde under **King Frederik X** (following Queen Margrethe II's abdication in January 2024). The process moved swiftly. At **10:15 on 25 March**, Frederiksen visited the King at Amalienborg, submitted the SVM government's resignation, and recommended consultations with all party representatives. The kongerunde ran from approximately **13:00 to 17:30**, with representatives from all 12 parties visiting the King, largest to smallest, each presenting written recommendations.

**Parties pointing to Mette Frederiksen as formateur (84 mandates):**

- **Socialdemokratiet** — Sent Peter Hummelgaard and Nicolai Wammen (Frederiksen did not attend in person). Recommended she lead negotiations for a new government.
- **SF** — Pointed to Frederiksen "with a view to forming a centre-left government with SF participation." Delivered an explicit ultimatum: **"SF will vote for a motion of no confidence against any government that SF is not part of."**
- **Enhedslisten** — Pointed to Frederiksen on the condition the government be "based on Enhedslisten's mandates" and formed **without any blue-bloc parties**.
- **Radikale Venstre** — Pointed to Frederiksen, aiming to form "Denmark's greenest government" reaching across the centre, **with Radical participation**.
- **Alternativet** — Supported Frederiksen conditionally, demanding a red-green government, "possibly with Moderaterne," with a green and socially responsible policy course.

**Parties pointing to Troels Lund Poulsen (73 mandates):**

- **Venstre** — Recommended its own leader as formateur without specifying conditions.
- **Liberal Alliance** — Recommended Troels Lund Poulsen, tasked with building a "functional government among the Folketing's very broad non-socialist majority."
- **Dansk Folkeparti** — Pointed to Troels Lund Poulsen, with two hard conditions: (1) the government must explicitly pursue "net Muslim emigration from Denmark," and (2) it must **exclude both Socialdemokratiet and Moderaterne**.
- **Konservative** — Recommended Troels Lund Poulsen "with the aim of forming a new government" — notably without specifying detailed conditions.
- **Danmarksdemokraterne** — Pointed to Troels Lund Poulsen for a bourgeois government focused on tax cuts, tighter immigration, rural-urban balance, and pragmatic green transition.

**Moderaterne (14 mandates)** pointed to **Lars Løkke Rasmussen himself**, wanting a government "across the centre of the Folketing." **Borgernes Parti (4 mandates)** declined to point to anyone.

Following the kongerunde, Frederiksen drew the conclusion that 84 mandates — the largest bloc — had pointed to her. The Royal House issued an official statement: **"The King has requested acting Prime Minister Mette Frederiksen to lead negotiations on forming a government with participation of SF and Radikale Venstre."** She was thus formally appointed as **kongelig undersøger** (royal investigator/formateur) on the afternoon of 25 March.

## Coalition signals from all parties reveal deep contradictions

The 48 hours since the election have produced a dense record of party signals. What follows is a party-by-party summary of concrete evidence — quotes, positions, and actions — drawn from press conferences, social media, and media reports.

**Socialdemokratiet (Mette Frederiksen):** Despite her party's devastating loss, Frederiksen moved quickly to consolidate her position. On election night: *"I'm ready to take on the responsibility. It will be difficult."* She identified the "most realistic" coalition formula as stretching "from Franciska [Rosenkilde, Alternativet] to Lars [Løkke Rasmussen, Moderaterne]" — all five red-bloc parties plus Moderaterne. On 26 March, she invited **seven parties** to initial talks beginning 27 March (SF, Enhedslisten, Radikale Venstre, Alternativet, Moderaterne, Venstre, and Konservative), with additional meetings on 29, 31 March, and 1 April. Crucially, she softened her stance on the wealth tax, telling Jyllands-Posten it was "a means to an end" and "can of course be replaced with something else." She declared a national pesticide ban as an "ultimate demand" for any government she leads. NordiskPost reports that internal party calls for her resignation have begun emerging from local Social Democratic councillors.

**SF (Pia Olsen Dyhr):** SF's ultimatum is the hardest red line in these negotiations. Dyhr stated flatly: *"Either SF is in government, or we are in opposition."* On 25 March she added: *"We are willing to negotiate, and we hope that Lars Løkke at some point comes to his senses and also wants to negotiate government."* She acknowledged the need for compromise with Moderaterne despite being "far apart on some areas." SF's policy demands include welfare improvements, pension reform, a pesticide ban over vulnerable groundwater, improved animal welfare in pig production, and a binding nature/biodiversity law. On 26 March, SF joined Ø, R, and Å in a joint press conference with the Danish Nature Conservation Society demanding **30% protected nature** as a government formation condition.

**Moderaterne (Lars Løkke Rasmussen):** The kingmaker positioned himself with deliberate ambiguity. Election night: *"There is no hard-red majority to our left, and no hard-blue majority to our right… What should that be used for? Tomorrow will show."* He appealed directly to both blocs: *"I would say to Troels and Mette, who we have had a good cooperation with these past three years: try now to climb down from those trees."* On 26 March, he said he would approach talks "with an open mind" but warned the current negotiation track "looks a bit narrow and doesn't resemble something we'd end up supporting." His red lines: **no government including Enhedslisten or Dansk Folkeparti**. He has demanded Frederiksen drop the wealth tax. CNN quoted him: *"We're standing in the center. Don't sprint out towards the corner flags. We're standing there in the middle. That's where the game is interesting. Come and play with us."*

**Venstre (Troels Lund Poulsen):** The most dramatic shift of election night. After years of keeping the door open to S, Troels Lund slammed it shut: *"It has worn on Venstre to be in government with Socialdemokratiet… I see two options for Venstre: Either we get a blue centre-government, or we go into opposition."* He appealed directly to Løkke: *"The possibility is there, Lars!"* On 26 March, deputy leader Stephanie Lose reinforced the signal: *"We find it very hard to see what we have to talk about."* Yet Venstre accepted the invitation to Frederiksen's 27 March talks. Troels Lund left a 26 March group meeting declining to answer whether he could "end up in government with Mette Frederiksen again." The party announced a "thorough evaluation" of its historic loss.

**Liberal Alliance (Alex Vanopslagh):** Backed Troels Lund Poulsen but acknowledged a blue government "looks difficult." Election night: *"I can offer Lars Løkke that we don't need a Social Democratic prime minister."* He warned Messerschmidt: *"If Morten Messerschmidt is not ready to cooperate with Lars Løkke Rasmussen, then he gives four more years to Mette Frederiksen."* LA was **not invited** to Frederiksen's first round of talks. Key policies: tax cuts, asylum stop, nuclear energy, opposition to wealth tax.

**Dansk Folkeparti (Morten Messerschmidt):** Messerschmidt received **50,819 personal votes** — the most of any candidate. He called it *"a historic evening."* His formal conditions are the most extreme: "net Muslim emigration from Denmark" as a government goal, and categorical refusal to work with either S or M. He promised to make DF the "main opponent" of Løkke: *"[We] will eat him and his people every single day."* DF was not invited to Frederiksen's talks and is effectively boxed out of any realistic government formation.

**Konservative (Mona Juul):** The Conservatives are the blue-bloc party keeping the most doors open. Juul called the result *"et ægte hattrick"* (a genuine hat trick). She has **not ruled out** working with Socialdemokratiet — creating irritation among DF, DD, and LA. To DR on 25 March: *"I believe people need to bend toward each other."* She was **invited** to Frederiksen's talks on 27 March. Her reservations center on SF's inclusion: wealth tax and early retirement are "not conservative policy."

**Enhedslisten (Pelle Dragsted):** Demanded a pure red government. On Frederiksen opening to a centre-left government: *"That's what I've been trying to wring out of her for the past couple of weeks, and it succeeded five minutes to twelve."* On Løkke: *"It's first and foremost Lars Løkke who needs to knock on my door and try to convince me it's a good idea."* He added: *"I've said my phone is open. Let's see what happens"* — noting he had not yet heard from Løkke. TV2 analyst Morten Nielsen observed: **"There are light-years between Enhedslisten and Moderaterne's economic policy."**

**Danmarksdemokraterne (Inger Støjberg):** Backed Troels Lund Poulsen. Unlike DF, DD does not categorically exclude Moderaterne. Støjberg called Løkke "rød" (red/left-wing) but maintained negotiating flexibility. Not invited to Frederiksen's first round.

**Radikale Venstre (Martin Lidegaard):** Positioned as the most flexible party. On 25 March: *"We are probably the least difficult [party] when it comes to which letter combinations you can make across the centre."* Called for "Denmark's greenest government" and a "responsible economic policy" combined with "a firm immigration policy within the conventions." Had already spoken with Frederiksen by phone before formal negotiations began.

**Alternativet (Franciska Rosenkilde):** Demanded red-green priorities as a precondition, "possibly with M." Instagram statement, 25 March: *"Danes have chosen to pull Denmark in the most progressive direction… You have voted for an alternative to status quo."*

## Policy negotiations center on seven flashpoints

Evidence from the first 48 hours identifies the key policy dimensions that will dominate negotiations:

**Wealth tax (formueskat):** Arguably the campaign's most divisive issue. S proposed a 0.5% tax on fortunes over DKK 25 million. Frederiksen has already signaled retreat, calling it "a means to an end." Løkke called it "stupid." Blue-bloc parties cited a Dansk Industri analysis claiming DKK 16 billion in lost growth — though DR Verificerer reported Princeton Professor Henrik Kleven found "no clear scientific basis" for that figure. **Likely outcome: modified or dropped** if Moderaterne join government.

**Pesticide ban / drinking water:** A Miljøministeriet report found pesticide residues in over 50% of drinking water boreholes. Frederiksen declared a national pesticide ban her "ultimate demand" in the final TV2 debate (23 March). **Broad support** from S, SF, Ø, M, R, K, and Å. Venstre prefers voluntary agreements. Commentator Lawand Hiwa Namo called the pesticide ultimatum "a death blow to any S-V cooperation." **High probability of implementation** in any Frederiksen-led government.

**Pension reform:** Every major party released a pension plan during the campaign. The current system raises the pension age automatically (68 in 2030, 70 in 2040). S wants to freeze automatic increases and massively expand the Arne-pension (early retirement) at an estimated cost of ~DKK 30 billion/year. Løkke proposed scrapping Arne-pension entirely for comprehensive reform. Finansministeriet calculated freezing the pension age at 70 costs **DKK 70 billion/year**. This will be one of the most contentious negotiation items, with S and M on opposite sides.

**Store bededag:** Eight parties want it reinstated (SF, Ø, Å, DF, DD, K, LA, Borgernes Parti). Even Frederiksen said she's "willing to discuss it" and Løkke acknowledged they would have handled it differently. **Very high probability of reinstatement** given overwhelming parliamentary support. Cost: ~DKK 3 billion/year.

**Climate and biodiversity:** The 2026 Klimarådet status report recommends raising the greenhouse gas tax on carbon-rich lowlands from DKK 40/ton to ~DKK 125/ton. Radikale wants 110% reduction by 2045. The joint SF-Ø-R-Å press conference on 26 March demanded a biodiversity law with **30% protected nature**. Nuclear energy united the blue bloc (V, LA, DF, K, DD jointly pledged to lift the 1985 ban) but splits both the blocs and S internally — 3 in 10 S candidates were open to nuclear. Unlikely to feature in a centre-left government.

**Immigration:** Frederiksen declared strict immigration policy one of three "ultimate demands." DF's extreme conditions (net Muslim emigration) box them out but apply pressure. The convention debate — whether to withdraw from EU conventions to enable broader deportation powers — will be legally complex. Key tension: SF and R push for a softer line; S and M want to maintain strictness.

**Defense spending:** Denmark already committed to **3.5% of GDP** (DKK 106.8 billion), exceeding NATO targets. Broad parliamentary consensus makes this less contentious, but the fiscal trade-off against welfare, pensions, and green investment is real.

## Commentators converge on one fragile formula — but all paths have obstacles

The analytical consensus points toward **S + SF + Radikale Venstre + Moderaterne** as the most probable government, with Enhedslisten and Alternativet as external support parties. But every analyst emphasizes this requires parties to abandon stated positions.

**Hans Engell** (veteran commentator, TV2): *"My best guess is it will probably end with a government where S, SF, Radikale Venstre and Løkke are in. Then comes the exciting part: what will Enhedslisten say?"*

**Bent Winther** (Berlingske): Called Frederiksen's mandate "quite narrow" and identified two paths — both requiring "swallowing enormous camels."

**Pernille Skipper** (TV2): *"There are only unlikely scenarios right now. I have difficulty seeing anything. None of the constellations that give 90 mandates fulfill what all parties have demanded."*

**Rune Stubager** (Professor, Aarhus University): *"The result was rather muddy, it resulted in a fragmented parliament. We're looking at rather prolonged negotiations by Danish standards."* Still expects Frederiksen to remain PM because "tripwires" are less flexible in the blue bloc.

**Noa Redington** (TV2, former S spindoctor): *"It's a catastrophic election for the Social Democrats. Mette Frederiksen may have a very high profile on the European stage but in Denmark she is in deep crisis."* Declared: *"The SVM government is stone dead. I can guarantee that."*

**Henrik Kjerrumgaard** (former Radical adviser) proposed an "agurkeregering" (pickle government): **SF + S + R + M + Konservative** — the five parties that haven't explicitly refused to work together. This would yield approximately **95 seats** and avoid dependence on Enhedslisten, though it would require bridging the economic distance between SF and K.

**Lykke Friis** (Think Tank Europa, quoted by CNBC): Noted that Greenland and Ukraine "did not play a role at all" in the result. Core issues were drinking water and animal welfare — "issues that are not very good for the Social Democratic party."

## The central paradox and what to watch next

The **mathematical reality** is stark. S + SF + R = only 68 seats. Adding Moderaterne yields 82 — still short. The full red bloc (S+SF+Ø+R+Å) plus M reaches 98, a comfortable majority, but Løkke has said he will not accept dependence on the far left, and Enhedslisten demanded no blue-participation government. The blue bloc plus M reaches 91, but Messerschmidt's categorical refusal to work with Løkke (and vice versa) makes this arithmetically possible but politically impossible.

Frederiksen's decision to also invite Venstre and Konservative to negotiations — despite being mandated to explore an S-SF-R government — signals she has not abandoned the idea of a broader coalition. The "agurkeregering" formula (S+SF+R+M+K = ~95 seats) avoids the Enhedslisten-Moderaterne incompatibility problem but requires Konservative to cross the aisle despite pointing to Troels Lund Poulsen. Mona Juul's refusal to close that door is the most important signal to watch.

**Scheduled next steps:** Bilateral sættemøder (preliminary meetings) at Statsministeriet begin 27 March, continuing 29 and 31 March and 1 April. North Atlantic members will be consulted separately the following week. The new Folketing convenes on **10 April**, when a Speaker will be elected. The 2022 formation took six weeks; commentators expect a similar or longer timeline. The SVM government continues as a caretaker (forretningsministerium) in the interim.

For your simulation model, the key parameter-relevant data points are: SF's no-confidence ultimatum (maximum negotiation harshness), Løkke's deliberate ambiguity (moderate harshness, high leverage), Venstre's dramatic shift to opposition posture (high harshness, but still attending talks), Konservative's open-door signal (low harshness, high coalition flexibility), DF's self-imposed isolation (maximum harshness, zero coalition viability), Enhedslisten's conditional cooperation (high demands but phone remains open), and Frederiksen's early softening on wealth tax (signaling willingness to make concessions to secure Moderaterne). The wealth tax retreat and pesticide-ban ultimatum together define Frederiksen's revealed negotiation strategy: sacrifice economic-left positions to hold the green-left coalition together while making space for centrist participation.
